## Basic Dividend Growth Rate Formula

The simplest way to calculate dividend growth rate is using the following formula:

Dividend Growth Rate = (Ending Dividend - Beginning Dividend) / Beginning Dividend × 100

### Example Calculation

Let's say Company XYZ paid:
- 2022 dividend: $2.00 per share
- 2021 dividend: $1.80 per share

Growth Rate = ($2.00 - $1.80) / $1.80 × 100 = 11.11%

## Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) Method

For longer periods, the CAGR method provides a more accurate picture of dividend growth. The formula is:

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n) - 1

Where 'n' is the number of years

### CAGR Example

Consider Johnson & Johnson's dividend history:
- 2018 dividend: $3.54
- 2023 dividend: $4.76
- Time period: 5 years

CAGR = ($4.76 / $3.54)^(1/5) - 1
= 1.0610 - 1
= 0.0610 or 6.10%

## Rolling Average Method

To smooth out short-term fluctuations, calculate the average growth rate over several periods:

1. Calculate year-over-year growth rates
2. Sum the rates
3. Divide by the number of periods

### Rolling Average Example

Company ABC's dividend history:
- 2020: $1.00
- 2021: $1.08 (8% growth)
- 2022: $1.15 (6.5% growth)
- 2023: $1.25 (8.7% growth)

Average growth rate = (8% + 6.5% + 8.7%) / 3 = 7.73%

## Advanced Calculation Methods

### Geometric Mean Method

This method accounts for compounding effects over multiple periods:

Geometric Mean = ((1 + r₁) × (1 + r₂) × ... × (1 + rₙ))^(1/n) - 1

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Using Too Short a Time Period

- Minimum 5-year history recommended
- Longer periods provide better insight
- Account for business cycles

### 2. Ignoring Special Dividends

- Exclude one-time special dividends
- Focus on regular dividend payments
- Consider total shareholder returns

### 3. Not Adjusting for Stock Splits

- Account for stock splits
- Normalize historical dividends
- Use split-adjusted data
