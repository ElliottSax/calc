# Compound Interest Calculator: How to Calculate Investment Growth

## What Is Compound Interest and How Do You Calculate It?

Compound interest is interest earned on both principal and previously accumulated interest, calculated using the formula A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) where A = final amount, P = principal, r = annual rate, n = compounding frequency, t = years. Example: $10,000 invested at 10% annual return compounding monthly for 20 years = $10,000 × (1 + 0.10/12)^(12×20) = $67,275 (versus $30,000 simple interest).

**5 Key Takeaways:**
- **Compounding frequency impact**: $10,000 at 10% for 20 years yields $67,275 (monthly compounding) vs $67,275 (annual) = $6,116 difference—daily compounding adds just $83 more, so monthly vs daily is negligible for most investors
- **Time matters more than rate**: Starting at age 25 vs 35 (10-year headstart) with $5,000/year at 8% results in $861,584 vs $367,471 at age 65—$494,113 advantage (134% more) from earlier start despite identical contributions ($200K total)
- **Doubling rule (Rule of 72)**: Years to double = 72 ÷ interest rate—8% doubles in 9 years (72÷8), 12% doubles in 6 years (72÷12), helping quick mental math for long-term projections
- **$100/month beats $1,200/year lump sum**: Monthly contributions at 10% for 30 years = $226,048 vs annual lump sum = $217,132 ($8,916 less) due to earlier compounding on each monthly deposit throughout the year
- **1% fee difference costs 28% of wealth**: $10,000 at 10% for 30 years with 0.5% fee = $72,425 vs 1.5% fee = $52,450—$19,975 lost (28% of final value) to that "small" 1% annual fee difference compounded
