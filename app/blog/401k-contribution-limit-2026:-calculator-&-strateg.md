# 401k Contribution Limit 2026: Complete Guide & Strategic Planning Calculator

## What Are the 401k Contribution Limits for 2026?

The 401k contribution limit for 2026 is projected at $23,500-$24,500 for standard employee contributions (up from $23,000 in 2025), with an additional $7,500-$8,000 catch-up contribution for participants age 50+ (total $31,000-$32,500 maximum employee contribution). The combined employer + employee contribution limit is projected at $69,000-$71,000 (up from $69,000 in 2025). These projections follow the IRS's annual inflation adjustments under Section 415(c) of the Internal Revenue Code—official limits are typically announced October-November of the prior year. To maximize contributions: divide your annual goal by pay periods ($24,000 ÷ 26 bi-weekly paychecks = $923/paycheck), prioritize employer match first (typical 50% match on first 6% of salary = free money), then fill remaining limit with additional contributions up to the $23,500-$24,500 cap.

## 5 Key Takeaways

- **401k limits increase $500-$1,500 annually based on inflation adjustments**: 2024 limit was $23,000, 2025 was $23,000 (unchanged), 2026 projected $23,500-$24,500 (2-6.5% increase). Historical pattern: IRS rounds adjustments to nearest $500 increment based on CPI-W (Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners). During high inflation (2021-2023), limits jumped $1,000-$2,000/year. At 2-3% inflation, expect $500 increases. Maxing out 2026 at projected $24,000 = $2,000/month or $923 bi-weekly contribution.

- **Catch-up contributions for age 50+ add $7,500-$8,000 extra capacity**: Participants who turn 50 or older by December 31, 2026 can contribute additional $7,500-$8,000 beyond standard $23,500-$24,500 limit = total $31,000-$32,500 maximum employee contribution. This accelerates retirement savings in final working years. Example: 52-year-old earning $100K can defer $31,000 (31% of gross income) vs $24,000 for under-50 colleague. Over 15 years age 50-65, catch-up adds $112,500+ in extra contributions.

- **Employer match doesn't count toward $23,500-$24,500 employee limit**: Your deferrals are capped at $23,500-$24,500, but employer contributions are SEPARATE and count toward the higher combined limit ($69,000-$71,000). Example: You contribute $24,000 + employer matches $6,000 (50% on first 6% of $200K salary) = $30,000 total, well under $69,000 combined limit. This means you should ALWAYS contribute enough to capture full employer match—it's free money on top of your deferrals.

- **Front-loading contributions risks missing employer match if paid per-paycheck**: Some employers match on a per-paycheck basis rather than annual true-up. If you max out 401k in 6 months ($4,000/month × 6 = $24,000), you receive $0 employer match for remaining 6 months = losing 50% of potential match. Solution: Spread contributions evenly across all paychecks ($923 bi-weekly for 26 paychecks) to capture match every pay period. Check your plan's match policy—if it has annual true-up, front-loading is safe.

- **Roth 401k contributions count toward the same $23,500-$24,500 limit as traditional**: You can't contribute $24,000 traditional + $24,000 Roth = $48,000 total. The $23,500-$24,500 limit is COMBINED across traditional (pre-tax) and Roth (post-tax) deferrals. Example: $15,000 traditional + $9,000 Roth = $24,000 total = at limit. Strategic split: Younger workers (under 40) favor Roth for tax-free growth over 25-40 years. Higher earners (32%+ tax bracket) favor traditional for immediate tax deduction. Many split 50/50 for tax diversification in retirement.

## Strategic Planning for 2026 Contributions

### 1. Front-Loading vs. Dollar-Cost Averaging

#### Front-Loading Strategy
- Contribute higher amounts early in the year
- Benefits: Earlier market exposure, guaranteed max contribution
- Example: Contributing $4,000 monthly for first 6 months

#### Dollar-Cost Averaging Strategy
- Equal contributions throughout the year
- Benefits: Consistent budgeting, reduced market timing risk
- Example: $2,000 monthly for 12 months
