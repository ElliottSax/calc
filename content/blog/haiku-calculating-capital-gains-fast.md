# Calculating Capital Gains Fast

Capital gains represent profits from selling investments for more than you paid. Calculating gains accurately determines tax obligations and true investment returns. This guide provides quick methods for calculating gains on any security.

## Understanding Capital Gains

Capital gains occur when you sell an investment at a price higher than your cost basis:

**Formula:**
**Capital Gain = Selling Price - Purchase Price**

**Simple Example:**
- Purchased stock at: $50 per share
- Sold stock at: $75 per share
- Capital gain per share: $25
- Percentage gain: $25 / $50 × 100 = 50%

## Types of Capital Gains

The tax treatment of gains depends on holding period:

**Short-Term Capital Gains** (held less than 1 year):
- Taxed as ordinary income
- Tax rate: up to 37%
- Most expensive tax treatment

**Long-Term Capital Gains** (held more than 1 year):
- Preferential tax rate
- 15% for most investors
- 0% for low-income individuals
- 20% for high-income individuals

### Real Tax Impact Example

Apple (AAPL) purchase and sale:

**Scenario A: Short-term (6 months)**
- Purchase price: $200
- Sale price: $250
- Capital gain: $50
- Tax at 37% bracket: $18.50
- After-tax gain: $31.50

**Scenario B: Long-term (2 years)**
- Purchase price: $200
- Sale price: $250
- Capital gain: $50
- Tax at 15% bracket: $7.50
- After-tax gain: $42.50

Holding long-term creates $11 additional after-tax profit on the same $50 gain—22% more return.

## Calculating Multi-Lot Gains

When you purchase the same security multiple times at different prices, calculating gains becomes more complex:

### Purchase History Example

| Purchase Date | Shares | Price/Share | Total Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 2024 | 10 | $50 | $500 |
| Mar 2024 | 15 | $55 | $825 |
| Jun 2024 | 12 | $52 | $624 |
| Total | 37 | N/A | $1,949 |

**When selling 20 shares at $75:**

**Average Cost Method** (simplest):
- Average cost per share: $1,949 / 37 = $52.68
- Gain for 20 shares: ($75 - $52.68) × 20 = $445.60

**FIFO Method** (First-In, First-Out):
- Sell the 10 shares from Jan (cost $50)
- Sell the 10 shares from Mar (cost $55)
- Gain = (10 × $25) + (10 × $20) = $250 + $200 = $450

**Specific ID Method** (most tax-efficient):
- Sell the 12 shares from Jun (cost $52)
- Sell the 8 shares from Jan (cost $50)
- Gain = (12 × $23) + (8 × $25) = $276 + $200 = $476

Different methods create different taxable gains ($445-$476), affecting tax liability by $5-10 per lot.

## Quick Calculation Tools and Methods

### Percentage Gain Formula

**Percentage Gain = (Selling Price - Purchase Price) / Purchase Price × 100**

For VOO purchased at $470 and sold at $520:
Percentage gain = ($520 - $470) / $470 × 100 = 10.64%

### Dollar Gain on Multiple Shares

Stock position: 100 shares purchased at $50, sold at $65

Dollar gain = (Sale price - Purchase price) × Quantity
= ($65 - $50) × 100
= $1,500

### Annualized Return Calculation

When calculating return rate over specific period:

**Annualized Return = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/years) - 1**

Position held 2.5 years:
- Beginning value: $5,000
- Ending value: $7,500

Annualized return = ($7,500 / $5,000)^(1/2.5) - 1
= (1.5)^0.4 - 1
= 1.1609 - 1
= 16.09% annually

## Tax Loss Harvesting Calculations

Strategic selling of losing positions offsets gains, reducing tax liability:

### Tax Loss Example

**Positions:**
- Position A: $5,000 investment, now worth $6,500 (gain $1,500)
- Position B: $8,000 investment, now worth $6,800 (loss $1,200)

**Without harvesting:**
- Tax on $1,500 gain at 15%: $225

**With tax loss harvesting:**
- Net gain: $1,500 - $1,200 = $300
- Tax at 15%: $45
- Tax savings: $180

### Multi-Year Loss Carryover

Losses exceeding gains in a year can offset future gains:

| Year | Gains | Losses | Net | Carryover |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2024 | $2,000 | $5,000 | $(3,000) | $(3,000) |
| 2025 | $4,000 | $1,000 | $3,000 | $(2,000) |
| 2026 | $5,000 | $0 | $5,000 | $(2,000) |

The 2024 loss offsets 2025 gains, carrying forward remaining loss to 2026. Tax deduction in 2025: only $1,000 gain is taxable.

## Wash Sale Rule Implications

IRS disallows losses if you repurchase the same security within 30 days (30 days before or after sale):

### Wash Sale Scenario

- Jan 15: Sell VTI at $110 (purchased at $120, loss $10)
- Jan 20: Purchase VTI at $108
- Tax treatment: Loss disallowed; cost basis of new purchase rises to $118

The $10 loss is deferred to the new position, not immediately deductible.

## Calculating Unrealized vs. Realized Gains

**Unrealized Gains** (not yet sold):
- Paper profits/losses
- Not taxable until sold
- Important for monitoring positions

**Realized Gains** (sold):
- Actual taxable profits
- Create immediate tax obligation
- Require tax payment in current year

### Portfolio Example

| Position | Cost Basis | Current Value | Unrealized Gain |
|---|---|---|---|
| AAPL | $10,000 | $15,000 | $5,000 |
| MSFT | $8,000 | $7,200 | $(800) |
| GOOGL | $12,000 | $16,500 | $4,500 |
| VOO | $20,000 | $23,000 | $3,000 |
| **Total** | **$50,000** | **$61,700** | **$11,700** |

The $11,700 unrealized gain is not taxable until you sell positions.

If you sell AAPL and GOOGL:
- Realized gains: $5,000 + $4,500 = $9,500
- Tax at 15%: $1,425
- Remaining unrealized gains: $2,200 (MSFT loss + VOO gain)

## ETF and Dividend Gain Scenarios

### ETF Distribution Gains

SCHD (Dividend ETF) typically distributes quarterly:
- Shares purchased: 100 at $75 = $7,500
- Dividend distribution: $0.85 per share = $85
- Distribution is taxable income (if in taxable account)

If you later sell SCHD at $80:
- Capital gain: ($80 - $75) × 100 = $500
- Total gain: $85 (distributions) + $500 (appreciation) = $585
- But distributions were already taxed, so capital gain only: $500

### REIT Capital Gains

REITs distribute gains to shareholders:

REIT purchase: 200 shares at $40 = $8,000
Year 1 distributions:
- Income distribution: $300 (taxed as ordinary income)
- Capital gain distribution: $200 (long-term capital gains)
- Total taxable: $500

If you sell at $42:
- Capital gain on sale: ($42 - $40) × 200 = $400
- Previous capital gain distributions already taxed

## FAQ

**Q: What's the fastest way to calculate multiple-position gains?**
A: Use your broker's built-in tools. They automatically calculate using your actual lot purchases and tax treatment.

**Q: Should I use FIFO or average cost?**
A: Use specific ID (identifying exact lots sold) for tax optimization. FIFO is default; average cost simplifies but may not minimize taxes.

**Q: How does dividend reinvestment affect gain calculation?**
A: Reinvested dividends increase cost basis. If you reinvested $500 in dividends, your cost basis rises by $500 before calculating capital gains.

**Q: When do I owe taxes on gains?**
A: When you sell. Unrealized gains have no tax consequence. Short-term gains are taxed in year of sale; long-term gains may be deferred if you hold into next year.

**Q: Can I use investment losses to offset regular income?**
A: Only $3,000 per year. Excess losses carry forward indefinitely until used.

## Conclusion

Capital gain calculation—while appearing complex with multiple lots and holding periods—follows straightforward formulas. Understanding the difference between realized and unrealized gains, leveraging tax-advantaged loss harvesting, and selecting optimal lot identification methods allows investors to optimize after-tax returns.

The key insight: holding investments long-term (>1 year) creates 15% tax rates versus 37% short-term rates—a 22-percentage-point advantage. This tax-rate differential often justifies holding profitable positions longer despite market uncertainties.
