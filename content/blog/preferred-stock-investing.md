# Preferred Stock Investing: Fixed Income with Equity Upside

Preferred stocks occupy a unique middle ground between bonds and common stocks. They offer higher yields than most common stocks, priority over common stockholders for dividend payments, and potential for capital appreciation. For dividend investors seeking to diversify their income sources and boost portfolio yield, preferred stocks deserve serious consideration.

Despite their advantages, preferred stocks are poorly understood by many retail investors. Their hybrid nature, various structural types, and sensitivity to interest rates create complexity that rewards educated investors and penalizes those who treat them as simple high-yield stocks.

## What Are Preferred Stocks?

Preferred stocks are equity securities that combine features of both stocks and bonds. Like bonds, they typically pay a fixed dividend at regular intervals. Like stocks, they represent ownership in a company and trade on stock exchanges.

Key characteristics that distinguish preferred stocks from common stocks:

**Fixed dividends.** Most preferred stocks pay a fixed dividend, expressed as a percentage of the par value (usually $25). A preferred stock with a 6% coupon and $25 par value pays $1.50 per share annually.

**Dividend priority.** If a company cannot pay all dividends, preferred shareholders are paid before common shareholders. The company must pay all preferred dividends before it can pay any common dividends.

**Limited voting rights.** Preferred shareholders typically cannot vote on corporate matters. Some preferred stocks gain voting rights if dividends are not paid for a specified number of consecutive periods.

**Par value and call provisions.** Most preferred stocks have a $25 par value and can be redeemed (called) by the issuer after a specified date, usually 5 years after issuance.

## Types of Preferred Stocks

### Cumulative Preferred

If the company misses a preferred dividend payment, cumulative preferred stocks require that all missed payments be paid in arrears before any common dividends can resume. This provides important protection for income investors.

Most institutional-grade preferred stocks are cumulative, which significantly reduces the practical risk of missing dividend payments.

### Non-Cumulative Preferred

Non-cumulative preferred stocks do not require payment of missed dividends. If the company skips a payment, it is gone forever. Non-cumulative preferred is less protective but sometimes offers higher yields to compensate.

Banks often issue non-cumulative preferred stock because banking regulators prefer this structure, as it provides more flexibility for banks to conserve capital during financial stress.

### Callable Preferred

Callable preferred stocks can be redeemed by the issuer at par value after a specified date. If interest rates decline, the issuer may call the preferred stock and reissue new preferred at a lower rate.

Call risk limits your upside. If you purchase a callable preferred at $26 (above par) and it is called at $25, you lose $1 per share in addition to the future income stream.

### Convertible Preferred

Convertible preferred stocks can be converted into a specified number of common shares. This gives you the income stability of preferred stock plus participation in common stock appreciation if the company performs well.

Convertible preferred typically offers lower yields than non-convertible preferred because the conversion feature has value.

### Fixed-to-Floating Rate Preferred

These preferred stocks pay a fixed rate for an initial period (usually 5-10 years), then switch to a floating rate tied to a benchmark (usually LIBOR or SOFR plus a spread). They provide some protection against rising rates after the fixed period expires.

### Perpetual Preferred

Perpetual preferred stocks have no maturity date. They pay dividends indefinitely unless called by the issuer. Their perpetual nature makes them very sensitive to interest rate changes, similar to long-duration bonds.

## Why Preferred Stocks Appeal to Income Investors

### Higher Yields Than Common Stocks

Preferred stocks typically yield 5-7%, significantly more than the S&P 500's average dividend yield of approximately 1.5%. For income investors with large portfolios, this yield premium can represent thousands of dollars in additional annual income.

### Priority of Payment

In the capital structure, preferred stockholders have priority over common stockholders. If a company must choose between paying preferred dividends or common dividends, preferred holders are paid first. This priority provides an additional layer of income security.

### Lower Volatility Than Common Stocks

Because preferred stock prices are driven primarily by interest rates and credit quality rather than earnings growth, they tend to be less volatile than common stocks. This stability can be valuable for retirees and conservative investors who prioritize capital preservation alongside income.

### Diversification

Preferred stocks have different return drivers than common stocks and bonds, providing genuine diversification benefits. Adding preferred stocks to a portfolio of common stocks and bonds can reduce overall volatility while maintaining or increasing income.

## Risks of Preferred Stock Investing

### Interest Rate Sensitivity

Preferred stocks are highly sensitive to interest rate changes. Like bonds, preferred stock prices decline when rates rise and increase when rates fall. The longer the duration (perpetual preferred has the longest), the greater the sensitivity.

During the 2022-2023 rate hiking cycle, many preferred stocks declined 15-25% as rates rose rapidly. Investors who needed to sell during this period realized significant capital losses.

### Credit Risk

If the issuing company experiences financial distress, preferred dividends may be suspended and the stock price may decline dramatically. Unlike bondholders, preferred stockholders cannot force bankruptcy if dividends are not paid.

In a bankruptcy scenario, preferred stockholders are paid after all debt holders but before common stockholders. In practice, preferred holders in bankrupt companies often recover little or nothing.

### Call Risk

When rates decline, issuers call their preferred stock and reissue at lower rates. This limits your upside during falling rate environments and forces you to reinvest at lower yields. Never pay a significant premium above par for a callable preferred approaching its call date.

### Liquidity Risk

Many preferred stocks have limited trading volume, resulting in wide bid-ask spreads. Selling a large position in an illiquid preferred stock can be costly. Stick to preferred stocks with average daily volume above 50,000 shares.

### Inflation Risk

Fixed-rate preferred stocks provide no inflation protection. A 6% preferred dividend has the same nominal value in 20 years as it does today, but its purchasing power will be significantly reduced by inflation.

## How to Evaluate Preferred Stocks

### Credit Quality

Evaluate the issuing company's credit rating and financial health. Preferred stocks from investment-grade companies (BBB- or above) carry lower default risk. High-yield preferred stocks (below BBB-) offer higher yields but significantly higher credit risk.

Focus on preferred stocks issued by financially strong companies with long histories of preferred dividend payment.

### Current Yield vs. Yield to Call

Current yield is the annual dividend divided by the current price. Yield to call accounts for the potential capital gain or loss if the stock is called at par on the earliest call date.

If a preferred stock trades at $23 with a $25 par value and can be called in 3 years, the yield to call includes both the annual dividends and the $2 capital gain, annualized over 3 years.

Always calculate yield to call for preferred stocks trading below par. If the yield to call is significantly higher than current yield, you may benefit from both income and capital appreciation.

### Dividend Coverage

Check whether the issuing company's earnings comfortably cover all preferred dividend obligations. Calculate the preferred dividend coverage ratio:

**Preferred Coverage = Net Income / Total Preferred Dividend Obligations**

A ratio above 3x indicates strong coverage. Below 1.5x suggests elevated risk.

### Call Date Proximity

Avoid paying above par for preferred stocks that are past their call date or approaching it. The issuer can call the stock at $25, so paying $26 or more exposes you to an immediate capital loss if the call is exercised.

## Tax Treatment of Preferred Dividends

Preferred dividends receive different tax treatment depending on the issuer:

**Qualified dividends** from domestic corporations and qualifying foreign corporations are taxed at the lower capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income).

**Non-qualified dividends** from REITs, banks (on certain issues), and foreign entities are taxed as ordinary income.

**Trust-preferred securities** may generate interest income rather than dividend income, which is always taxed as ordinary income.

Check the tax classification of any preferred stock before purchasing, especially if held in a taxable account. The after-tax yield can differ significantly from the stated yield depending on classification.

## Building Preferred Stock Allocation

### Position Sizing

Allocate 5-15% of an income portfolio to preferred stocks. This provides meaningful yield enhancement without creating excessive interest rate or credit concentration.

### Diversification Rules

Own preferred stocks from at least 8-10 different issuers across multiple sectors. Financial companies dominate the preferred stock market, so actively seek preferred issues from utilities, pipelines, telecom, and other sectors to avoid over-concentration in financials.

### Laddering Call Dates

Similar to bond laddering, spread your preferred stock call dates across multiple years. This ensures that if some preferreds are called, you are not forced to reinvest all proceeds at once, potentially at lower yields.

## Key Takeaways

- Preferred stocks offer yields of 5-7%, priority over common stockholders for dividend payments, and lower volatility than common shares
- Cumulative preferred stocks provide the strongest income protection because missed dividends must be paid before common dividends can resume
- Interest rate sensitivity is the primary risk, with preferred stock prices declining significantly during rising rate environments
- Evaluate preferred stocks using current yield, yield to call, credit quality, and dividend coverage rather than treating them as simple high-yield investments
- Limit preferred stock allocation to 5-15% of an income portfolio and diversify across issuers, sectors, and call dates

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are preferred stocks safer than common stocks?

In terms of dividend priority, yes. Preferred dividends must be paid before common dividends. In terms of capital preservation, preferred stocks are less volatile in normal markets but can decline significantly during credit crises or rapid rate increases. In bankruptcy, preferred stockholders rank above common stockholders but below all debt holders.

### When should I buy preferred stocks?

The best time to buy preferred stocks is when interest rates are at or near peaks, as subsequent rate declines will boost preferred stock prices. Buying during periods of credit stress, when preferred yields spike due to fear rather than actual credit deterioration, can also create attractive entry points.

### Can preferred stocks lose all their value?

Yes, though it is rare for investment-grade preferred stocks. In a bankruptcy, preferred stockholders may recover nothing if the company's assets are insufficient to cover all debt obligations. This happened to preferred stockholders of companies like Lehman Brothers and Washington Mutual during the 2008 crisis.

### How do I compare preferred stocks to bonds?

Preferred stocks typically offer higher yields than bonds from the same issuer because they are lower in the capital structure. However, bonds have contractual obligations that preferred stocks do not. Compare after-tax yields, credit quality, interest rate sensitivity, and your priority in the capital structure when deciding between preferred stocks and bonds.

### Should I hold preferred stocks in tax-advantaged accounts?

For non-qualified preferred dividends (from REITs, certain bank preferreds), holding in tax-advantaged accounts eliminates the tax disadvantage. For qualified preferred dividends, holding in taxable accounts may be acceptable since the tax rate is favorable. The optimal account placement depends on the specific preferred stock's tax classification and your marginal tax rate.
