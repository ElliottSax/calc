## Calculating Risk-Adjusted Return for International ETFs

Risk-adjusted returns reveal whether international ETF performance compensates you for currency risk, geopolitical uncertainty, and other factors. This metric guides optimal international allocation within your global portfolio.

## Understanding Risk-Adjusted Returns

International ETFs often deliver competitive returns but with higher volatility from currency fluctuations and economic diversity. Risk-adjusted metrics reveal true value delivered per unit of risk.

## The Sharpe Ratio Formula

**Sharpe Ratio = (Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation**

## Worked Example: International ETF Risk-Adjusted Return

Compare two international equity ETFs:

**Developed Markets ETF:**
- 5-year return: 8.5%
- Annual volatility: 16.2%
- Risk-free rate: 4.5%

**Calculation:**
Sharpe Ratio = (8.5% - 4.5%) / 16.2%
Sharpe Ratio = 4% / 16.2%
**Sharpe Ratio = 0.247**

**Emerging Markets ETF:**
- 5-year return: 10.2%
- Annual volatility: 19.8%
- Risk-free rate: 4.5%

**Calculation:**
Sharpe Ratio = (10.2% - 4.5%) / 19.8%
Sharpe Ratio = 5.7% / 19.8%
**Sharpe Ratio = 0.288**

Emerging markets required more volatility but delivered better risk-adjusted returns.

## Why International ETFs Have Different Risk-Adjusted Returns

Currency Impact: Currency movements significantly affect returns and volatility for US investors.

Diversification: International stocks reduce US concentration.

Economic Cycles: Different regions cycle differently, affecting correlations.

## Step-by-Step Calculation

1. Gather 5-year monthly returns
2. Calculate average return
3. Calculate standard deviation
4. Subtract risk-free rate from average
5. Divide excess return by volatility

## Comparing International ETFs Using Sharpe Ratio

When selecting international ETFs:
1. Calculate Sharpe ratios for same time period
2. Adjust for currency hedging (hedged vs. unhedged)
3. Compare to US equity benchmarks
4. Evaluate expense ratios
5. Consider portfolio diversification benefits

## Currency Risk Impact

Currency fluctuations increase international ETF volatility:
- Unhedged: Full currency exposure
- Hedged: Currency neutralized (lower volatility but higher costs)
- Sharpe ratios differ between approaches

## FAQ

**Q: What's a good Sharpe ratio for international ETFs?**
A: 0.2-0.4 is typical; above 0.4 is excellent.

**Q: Why are international Sharpe ratios lower than US?**
A: Currency risk and geopolitical factors increase volatility.

**Q: Should I use hedged or unhedged international ETFs?**
A: Depends on your view of currency trends and risk tolerance.

**Q: How much international exposure should my portfolio have?**
A: Typically 15-30%, adjusted based on your risk tolerance.

**Q: Does international diversification improve portfolio Sharpe ratio?**
A: Yes, if correlations remain low. International stocks reduce overall portfolio volatility.

## Conclusion

Calculating risk-adjusted returns for international ETFs reveals whether international exposure justifies the additional risks undertaken. By understanding Sharpe ratios for international allocations, you optimize global portfolio construction and diversification strategy.
