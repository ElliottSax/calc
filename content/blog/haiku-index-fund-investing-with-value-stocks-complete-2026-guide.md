## Value Stock Characteristics

Value stocks trade at low multiples of earnings, book value, or cash flow. VTV (Vanguard Value ETF) holds 200+ value stocks with a 2.8% yield and average P/E ratio of 11.5, compared to VTI's 19.2 average P/E.

Value stocks often represent mature companies with established cash flows, making them attractive for income-focused investors seeking stability.

## Value Investing Formula

Professional value investors use specific metrics:

**Price-to-Book Ratio:** Stock Price / Book Value Per Share
- VTV average: 1.2
- VTI average: 3.1
- Value threshold: Below 1.5

**Price-to-Earnings Ratio:** Stock Price / Annual Earnings Per Share
- VTV average P/E: 11.5
- S&P 500 average: 19.2
- Value threshold: Below 12

**Price-to-Sales Ratio:** Market Cap / Annual Revenue
- VTV average: 0.9
- VTI average: 1.8
- Value threshold: Below 1.2

Stocks meeting multiple thresholds indicate deep value potential.

## Sector Rotation with Value Stocks

Value returns vary significantly by sector and economic cycle:

**Economic Expansion:**
- Value outperforms: +3-5% annual advantage
- Financials and industrials surge
- Example: 2009-2010 post-crisis expansion

**Economic Slowdown:**
- Value underperforms: -3-5% annual disadvantage
- Growth's pricing power persists
- Example: 2015-2019 slow growth period

**Inflationary Environment:**
- Value outperforms: +2-4% annual advantage
- Energy and commodity stocks gain
- Dividend growth exceeds growth capital appreciation
- Example: 2021-2022 inflation surge

## Building Conviction: Value Stocks in Portfolios

Value stocks serve important portfolio functions:
- Higher current income (2.8% vs. 1.5%)
- Dividend growth over time
- Defensive characteristics during crashes
- Tax efficiency through dividends vs. capital gains
- Lower valuations offer margin of safety

Value stocks become problematic when:
- Economic conditions hurt fundamental earnings (2008 crisis)
- Sector rotation favors growth for extended periods (2010-2020)
- Investor needs near-term capital appreciation
- Market revaluation of valuations becomes negative

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Should I buy VTV instead of VTI?**
A: Not necessarily. VTI holds VTV plus growth stocks. Buy VTI and accept market-weighted sector allocation unless seeking specific value exposure.

**Q2: Are value stocks riskier than growth stocks?**
A: Different risks. Value stocks offer lower volatility but cyclical earnings sensitivity. Growth stocks offer higher volatility but less earnings cyclicality.

**Q3: When do value stocks outperform?**
A: During economic expansion, inflation, and rising interest rates. Growth wins during expansion, low inflation, and falling rates.

**Q4: Can I combine value stock picking with index investing?**
A: Yes. 80% VTI + 20% personally selected value stocks provides a disciplined framework with conviction positions.

**Q5: Are dividend-focused value stocks better than pure value?**
A: For income, yes. VTV emphasizes dividend sustainability, screening for companies maintaining payments through cycles.
