# REITs Risk-Adjusted Return Calculator: Step-by-Step Guide

Risk-adjusted returns measure how much return you earned per unit of risk taken. A REIT generating 8% return with low volatility outperforms one delivering 9% with extreme volatility. The Sharpe Ratio quantifies this critical trade-off between returns and risk.

## Understanding Risk-Adjusted Returns for REITs

Comparing raw returns is deeply misleading when risk differs significantly. A highly volatile REIT might show better short-term returns by luck or sector cyclicality. Risk-adjusted metrics reveal sustainable performance and true skill versus temporary outperformance.

## Why Risk-Adjusted Returns Matter

The Sharpe Ratio forces consideration of volatility alongside returns. A 9% return with 20% volatility is riskier than an 8% return with 10% volatility. Risk-adjusted metrics help identify truly superior investments rather than just lucky ones.

## Sharpe Ratio Formula for REITs

**Sharpe Ratio = (REIT Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation**

Example:
- REIT return: 8.5%
- Risk-free rate (10-year Treasury): 4.5%
- REIT volatility (std dev): 12%
- Sharpe Ratio = (8.5% - 4.5%) / 12% = 0.33

## Calculating REIT Volatility (Standard Deviation)

1. Gather 36 monthly REIT returns (3 years of data)
2. Calculate average monthly return
3. Find difference between each month and average
4. Square each difference
5. Average the squared differences (variance)
6. Take square root (standard deviation)
7. Annualize: Multiply by √12 for yearly volatility

## Interpretation: What's a Good Sharpe Ratio?

- Below 0.0: Underperforming Treasury bonds (unacceptable)
- 0.0 to 0.3: Poor risk adjustment (mediocre)
- 0.3 to 0.6: Adequate returns for risk (acceptable)
- 0.6 to 1.0: Excellent risk-adjusted returns (superior)
- Above 1.0: Exceptional performance (very rare)

Most REITs cluster in the 0.2-0.4 range, indicating acceptable but not exceptional risk adjustment.

## Using Sortino Ratio for Downside Protection

The Sortino Ratio refines Sharpe by focusing only on downside volatility:

**Sortino = (Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Downside Deviation**

This penalizes only negative volatility, not positive surprises, revealing how well REITs cushion losses during downturns. A REIT with high positive volatility but stable lows shows better Sortino than Sharpe.

## FAQ

**Q: Why is REIT Sharpe Ratio lower than stock indices?**
A: REITs carry more risk than broad stock indices due to sector concentration and interest rate sensitivity. Their specialized nature limits diversification.

**Q: Can Sharpe Ratio change for the same REIT?**
A: Yes. It depends on the time period analyzed. Use 3+ years for stability. Recent Sharpe Ratios may differ from long-term patterns.

**Q: Should I only buy high-Sharpe-Ratio REITs?**
A: No. Consider diversification. Combine lower-Sharpe REITs for sector exposure and risk spreading.

**Q: Is a 0.25 Sharpe Ratio acceptable?**
A: It's below average but acceptable if the REIT provides necessary portfolio diversification or sector exposure.

**Q: How often should I recalculate REIT Sharpe Ratios?**
A: Quarterly for monitoring, but use 3-year rolling Sharpe for stability. Avoid over-reacting to short-term volatility spikes.
