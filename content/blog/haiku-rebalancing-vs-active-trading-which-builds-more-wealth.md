# Rebalancing vs Active Trading: Which Builds More Wealth?

Rebalancing adjusts portfolio allocations quarterly or annually to target percentages. Active trading frequently buys and sells individual securities to capitalize on perceived opportunities. Understanding which approach builds wealth reveals the power of disciplined, systematic strategies.

## Rebalancing vs Active Trading Defined

Rebalancing: Adjusting portfolio allocations quarterly or annually to target percentages (e.g., 60% stocks, 40% bonds).

Active Trading: Frequently buying/selling individual securities to capitalize on perceived market opportunities, often several times weekly or monthly.

## The Wealth-Building Case for Rebalancing

Rebalancing automatically forces "buy low, sell high" behavior. When stocks surge beyond your target, rebalancing cuts positions and locks gains. When they fall, you buy the dip. This systematic approach removes emotion from investing and prevents dangerous overconcentration.

## The Wealth-Building Case for Active Trading

Skilled traders identify mispricings and momentum shifts before others. Early recognition of a recovery trade can compound returns substantially. However, most traders underperform due to costs and taxes, making this case theoretical rather than practical.

## Hidden Costs of Active Trading

Active traders face multiple drags on returns:

- Commissions: 0.05-0.1% per trade (10-15 trades yearly = 0.5-1.5% drag)
- Bid-ask spreads: 0.02-0.1% per trade (10-15 trades = 0.2-1.5% annually)
- Short-term capital gains taxes: 37% top rate vs. 20% long-term
- Opportunity cost of time: Emotional stress from constant monitoring
- Slippage: Difference between expected price and actual execution

Total drag: 2-4% annually for active traders, making market-beating nearly impossible.

## Rebalancing Best Practices for Wealth Building

1. Set quarterly or annual rebalancing schedule (consistent timing works best)
2. Rebalance when allocations drift 5%+ from target (tight discipline)
3. Use new contributions to rebalance (minimize tax consequences)
4. In taxable accounts, harvest tax losses during rebalancing
5. In retirement accounts, rebalance freely without tax concern

## When Active Trading Occasionally Works

Active traders succeed briefly when they:
- Trade illiquid securities where inefficiencies exist
- Have information advantages (legitimate research)
- Trade short-term trends for <5 years (unlikely to persist)
- Focus on options/derivatives (requires advanced skills)

Most retail traders lack these edges, making sustained outperformance extremely unlikely.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I rebalance too frequently?**
A: Yes. Rebalancing more than annually increases costs without benefit. Quarterly or annual is optimal.

**Q: Does rebalancing reduce returns?**
A: No. It locks gains and reduces risk through diversification, improving risk-adjusted returns over time.

**Q: Should I time the market between rebalancing?**
A: No. Studies show market-timing attempts underperform scheduled rebalancing by 1-2% annually.

**Q: Can a skilled trader beat rebalancing?**
A: Perhaps initially, but not consistently. Ninety-five percent of active traders underperform rebalancing over 10+ years.

**Q: Is rebalancing boring?**
A: Yes. Boring investments compound wealth reliably. Exciting trading strategies typically destroy wealth through costs and taxes.
