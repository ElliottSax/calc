# Understanding Capital Gains Fast

Capital gains represent your investment profits—the difference between sale price and purchase price. The U.S. tax system treats these gains very differently depending on holding period: long-term gains (held over one year) receive preferential 0-20% tax rates, while short-term gains (held one year or less) face ordinary income tax rates up to 37%. This 15-37 percentage point gap makes understanding capital gains essential for after-tax return optimization.

## Capital Gains Fundamentals

A capital gain occurs whenever you sell an asset for more than your cost basis (original purchase price). Capital losses occur when you sell for less than your cost basis.

**Basic formula:**
Capital Gain/Loss = Sale Price − Cost Basis

**Simple example:**
- Purchase 100 shares VTI at $180 = $18,000 cost basis
- Sell all 100 shares at $245 = $24,500 proceeds
- Capital gain = $24,500 − $18,000 = $6,500

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capital Gains

The IRS defines holding periods from purchase date to sale date:
- **Short-term**: Held one year or less → taxed as ordinary income (up to 37%)
- **Long-term**: Held over one year → taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)

For the same $6,500 gain:

**Short-term tax at 32% bracket:** $6,500 × 0.32 = $2,080 tax
**Long-term tax at 15% bracket:** $6,500 × 0.15 = $975 tax
**Tax savings from waiting:** $1,105 (53% reduction)

This single-year wait transforms your after-tax return from 61% to 74% on the original $18,000 investment.

## Long-Term Capital Gains Tax Brackets 2026

Preferential long-term rates apply across three tiers:

**Single Filers:**
- 0% rate: Income up to $47,025
- 15% rate: Income $47,025 to $518,900
- 20% rate: Income over $518,900

**Married Filing Jointly:**
- 0% rate: Income up to $94,050
- 15% rate: Income $94,050 to $583,750
- 20% rate: Income over $583,750

**Tax optimization:** Your long-term capital gains "fill up" the income brackets from bottom to top. Understanding your marginal bracket prevents unnecessary taxes.

## Cost Basis Methods: Strategic Selection

When selling holdings purchased at multiple times, choosing which shares to sell reduces tax liability.

**Scenario: SCHD investor selling position**
- Original purchase Jan 2021: 200 shares @ $70.50 = $14,100
- Additional purchase Aug 2022: 150 shares @ $79.25 = $11,887
- Additional purchase Apr 2024: 180 shares @ $85.60 = $15,408
- Total holdings: 530 shares
- Current price: $105.00
- Want to sell 200 shares

**FIFO method (oldest first):**
- Cost basis: 200 × $70.50 = $14,100
- Proceeds: 200 × $105 = $21,000
- Gain: $6,900
- Tax (15%): $1,035

**Specific ID method (highest cost):**
- Cost basis: 180 × $85.60 + 20 × $79.25 = $15,408 + $1,585 = $16,993
- Proceeds: $21,000
- Gain: $4,007
- Tax (15%): $601

**Tax savings: $434** from choosing specific identification.

## Dividend Reinvestment and Cost Basis

Dividend reinvestment increases cost basis automatically. Unlike ordinary reinvestment, dividend purchase prices get added to cost basis.

**Example: 10 years of SCHD dividend reinvestment**
- Initial investment 2015: 500 shares @ $68.40 = $34,200
- Year 1-10 dividend reinvestment: Additional $8,950 cost basis
- Total cost basis: $43,150
- Current portfolio value (530 shares @ $105): $55,650
- Capital gain: $12,500

The reinvested dividends reduced your taxable gain by $8,950. Without tracking this carefully, you'd overstate capital gains by 72%.

## Tax-Loss Harvesting Strategy

Strategic selling of losing positions offsets capital gains dollar-for-dollar.

**Scenario: Mixed portfolio results**
- VTI gains: $15,000 (bought 2020, sold 2025)
- VXUS losses: $4,000 (bought 2023, sold 2025, market downturn)
- Net taxable gain: $15,000 − $4,000 = $11,000

Tax savings: $4,000 × 0.15 = $600 in federal taxes

**Wash-sale rules to observe:**
Cannot buy identical security within 30 days before or after selling at a loss. However, can switch to similar fund (VXUS → VTIAX) and maintain international exposure while locking in the loss.

## Capital Gains Distribution from Mutual Funds and ETFs

Passively managed index funds like VTI and VOO generate minimal capital gains distributions because holdings are rarely sold. Actively managed funds distribute substantial gains annually.

**2025 distribution example:**
- Passive index fund (VOO): $0.15 per share capital gains distribution
- Owner of 500 shares: $75 taxable gain (likely long-term)
- Actively managed equity fund (SYMBOL): $3.20 per share distribution
- Same $500 shares: $1,600 taxable gain

The passive fund created 1/21st the tax liability while likely delivering similar returns.

## Qualified Dividend Income

Dividends receive preferential tax treatment if qualified—held 60+ days around ex-dividend date and meeting other criteria.

**Dividend tax efficiency:**
- Qualified dividends: Taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% (same as long-term gains)
- Non-qualified dividends: Taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%)

A $5,000 qualified dividend on $150,000 income:
- Qualified dividend tax: $5,000 × 0.15 = $750

Same $5,000 as non-qualified dividend:
- Non-qualified tax: $5,000 × 0.32 = $1,600
- Additional tax: $850

Most dividend stocks (JNJ, KO, PG) distribute qualified dividends, making them tax-efficient for non-retirement accounts.

## Charitable Donations of Appreciated Securities

Donating appreciated securities to qualified charities eliminates capital gains tax while providing full deduction at fair market value.

**Example: $50,000 VTI position with $20,000 gain**

**Option A: Sell and donate proceeds**
- Capital gains tax: $20,000 × 0.15 = $3,000
- Proceeds after tax: $47,000
- Donate: $47,000
- Tax deduction: $47,000

**Option B: Donate appreciated shares directly**
- Capital gains tax: $0
- Donate: $50,000 worth
- Tax deduction: $50,000

**Benefit: $3,000 in capital gains taxes + $3,000 additional deduction = $6,000 total benefit**

This strategy transfers wealth to charity while eliminating embedded capital gains taxes.

## Capital Gains Timing and Market Cycles

Strategic realization of gains during down-market years reduces overall tax liability when adjusted gross income drops.

**Example: Year of substantial investment loss**
- Normal income: $150,000
- Investment loss: $50,000
- Adjusted gross income: $100,000

Realizing a $30,000 long-term gain in this year:
- Gain taxed at 15%: $4,500

Versus normal income year ($150,000 AGI):
- Same gain: $4,500

However, if the loss year drops you to the 0% capital gains bracket (up to $94,050 for married couples):
- First $94,050 of income: Combined salary + gains = $100,000
- Wait—exceeds bracket

Actually in this case, the loss created space in the 0% bracket. Moving $30,000 into the 0% bracket generates:
- Tax: $0
- Savings: $4,500

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: When exactly is the one-year holding period met?**
A: If purchased on March 15, 2025, the date becomes long-term on March 16, 2026 (one year and one day). The specific purchase date anniversary matters.

**Q: Do I report capital gains immediately upon selling?**
A: You report the gain in the tax year of the sale. If you sold in March 2026, you report it on your 2026 tax return (filed April 2027).

**Q: What if I inherited stocks? What's my cost basis?**
A: Step-up basis—your cost basis becomes the fair market value at death date. Inherited stocks receive a "reset" eliminating all appreciation before inheritance date.

**Q: Can I use losses from one year to offset gains from another year?**
A: Losses carry forward indefinitely. 2025 losses offset 2025 gains, then 2026 gains, etc. Excess losses above $3,000 annually carry to future years.

**Q: Are capital gains treated differently in retirement accounts?**
A: No—retirement accounts (401k, IRA) generate no immediate capital gains tax. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as income. This is why retirement accounts excel for trading-heavy strategies.
