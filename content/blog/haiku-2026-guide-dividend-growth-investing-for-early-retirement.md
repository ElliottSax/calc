# 2026 Guide: Dividend Growth Investing for Early Retirement

Early retirement through dividend growth investing differs from traditional FIRE: instead of relying on capital appreciation and 4% withdrawals, dividend growth investors build income that grows faster than inflation, providing psychological comfort and financial security.

## The Dividend Growth Early Retirement Model

Dividend growth retirement means building a portfolio where dividends grow to cover all living expenses, making withdrawals unnecessary.

### Formula: Dividend Growth to FI

```
Goal: Annual Dividends = Annual Expenses
Dividend Growth Rate: 5-7% annually
Timeline: 15-20 years
```

**Worked Example:**

Annual expenses: $60,000
Initial portfolio: $50,000
Annual investment: $30,000
Target dividend yield: 3.5%

**Year 1:**
- Portfolio: $80,000 (initial + first contribution)
- Dividend income: $2,800 (3.5% yield)

**Year 5:**
- Portfolio: $465,200
- Dividend income: $16,282 (growing at 5-6% annually)

**Year 10:**
- Portfolio: $1,031,050
- Dividend income: $36,087 (3.5% of portfolio, growing)

**Year 15:**
- Portfolio: $1,891,340
- Dividend income: $63,200 (3.3% yield, but growing 6% annually)

**Year 15 analysis:**
- Dividend income exceeds expenses ($63,200 > $60,000)
- Early retirement is now achievable
- Invested portfolio continues growing
- Dividends provide safety margin (not relying on capital appreciation)

## Dividend Growth Yield on Cost

Dividend growth investing creates "yield on cost"—annual return based on original investment amount.

### Formula: Yield on Cost

```
Yield on Cost = Annual Dividend Received / Original Investment Amount
```

**Worked Example:**

Original investment: $100,000 in SCHD
Original dividend: $3,500 (3.5% yield)

**Year 1:** Yield on cost = 3.5%
**Year 5:** Dividends grown to $5,124 annually
- Yield on cost = $5,124 / $100,000 = 5.12%

**Year 10:** Dividends grown to $8,273 annually
- Yield on cost = $8,273 / $100,000 = 8.27%

**Year 15:** Dividends grown to $13,271 annually
- Yield on cost = $13,271 / $100,000 = 13.27%

**Year 20:** Dividends grown to $21,321 annually
- Yield on cost = $21,321 / $100,000 = 21.32%

This demonstrates the magic of dividend growth: a $100K investment originally yielding 3.5% grows to yield 21% of original investment after 20 years.

**For early retirement, this is powerful:** $1M portfolio in Year 1 yielding 2.95% ($29,500) grows to yield 7% ($70,000) by Year 15 without adding capital.

## Dividend Growth Reinvestment Strategy

For early retirement building, dividend reinvestment is critical.

### DRIP (Dividend Reinvestment Plan)

**What it does:** Automatically reinvests dividends to purchase more shares

**Worked Example:**

SCHD position: $100,000 (2,000 shares at $50/share)
Annual dividend: $3,500 (3.5% yield)

**Year 1 with DRIP:**
- Dividend: $3,500

- Ownership: 2,070 shares

**Year 2 with DRIP:**
- Dividend on 2,070 shares: $3,645
- New shares: 73 shares
- Ownership: 2,143 shares

**Compounding continues each year.**

**Year 15:**
- Original shares: 2,000
- DRIP-purchased shares: 1,250+
- Total shares: 3,250+
- Annual dividend: $13,271

The DRIP created 1,250+ additional shares (worth $62,500+ at current price) through automatic compounding.

## Tax Implications of Dividend Growth Early Retirement

Dividend growth retirement is tax-efficient through qualified dividend treatment.

### Qualified Dividend Tax Rates

**Long-term capital gains / Qualified dividends:**
- 0% bracket: Income up to $95,375 (married, 2026)
- 15% bracket: Income up to $595,375
- 20% bracket: Income above $595,375

**Worked Example:**

Early retiree, age 45, married filing jointly, filing standard 529 plan dividends.

**Dividend income: $65,000** (Year 15 level)
**Other income: $0** (retired from job)
**Standard deduction: $29,200** (2026)

**Taxable income: $65,000 - $29,200 = $35,800**

**Tax calculation:**
- Standard deduction covers first $29,200: $0 tax
- Remaining $35,800 is in 15% long-term capital gains bracket
- Tax: $35,800 × 15% = $5,370
- **Effective tax rate: 8.3%**

This is dramatically lower than the 24-37% that earned income would face.
