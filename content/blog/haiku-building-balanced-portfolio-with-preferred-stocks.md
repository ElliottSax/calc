## Introduction

Preferred stocks blend equity and fixed income characteristics: higher yields (5-6%) than bonds, higher priority than common stock in bankruptcy, typically less volatile than common stock. This guide shows how to incorporate preferred stocks (5-15% allocation) into balanced portfolios for enhanced yield.

## Preferred Stock Characteristics

**Features:**
- Yield: 5-7% (higher than bonds)
- Maturity: Perpetual (no maturity date)
- Priority: Above common stock (lower loss in bankruptcy)
- Tax: Typically 15% federal dividend tax

**Risks:**
- Call risk (issuer can redeem early)
- Rate sensitivity (like bonds)
- Credit risk (company could default)
- Illiquidity (harder to sell quickly)

## Preferred Stock Considerations

**When preferred stocks work well:**
- Low interest rate environment (relative yields attractive)
- Stable credit environment (low default risk)
- Patient investors (5-10 year holding)
- Tax-efficient accounts (avoid 15% tax drag)

**Preferred stock risks:**
```
2022 rising rate scenario:
Preferred stocks fell 15% (rate sensitivity)
Bonds fell 13%
VOO fell 18%

Preferred provided yield during decline but suffered from rates
```

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What percentage preferred stocks is optimal?**
A: 10-15% maximum. Too much creates rate risk concentration. 10% is good balance.

**Q: Are preferred stocks safer than common stock?**
A: Yes—better priority in bankruptcy, higher yield. But less safe than bonds.

**Q: Should I use preferred stock ETFs or individual preferreds?**
A: ETFs for <$100k (diversification). Individual for $250k+ (can manage concentration).

**Q: How are preferred stocks taxed?**
A: As dividends, typically 15% federal. Same as qualified dividends.

**Q: Do preferred stocks hurt in recessions?**
A: Sometimes. Credit risk becomes reality if companies struggle. Quality preferreds (investment-grade) weather recessions better.

## Conclusion

Preferred stocks (5-15% of portfolio) provide yield enhancement (0.5-1.5% additional annual return) with moderate risk increase. Best used in tax-advantaged accounts where 15% dividend tax doesn't apply.
