## Calculating Current Yield vs. Yield-On-Cost in 2026

Current yield measures annual dividend relative to today's stock price. Yield-on-cost measures annual dividend relative to your original purchase price. These differ significantly if you've held positions for years.

**Example: Holding Johnson & Johnson since 2014**

| Year | Stock Price | Annual Dividend | Current Yield | Yield-on-Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2014 | $95 | $2.76 | 2.9% | 2.9% |
| 2018 | $128 | $3.60 | 2.8% | 3.8% |
| 2022 | $165 | $3.73 | 2.3% | 3.9% |
| 2026 | $158 | $4.40 | 2.8% | 4.6% |

Current yield of 2.8% understates your actual return. Yield-on-cost of 4.6% reflects the real income on your original $95 investment. Long-term dividend investors accumulate positions with increasingly favorable yield-on-cost compared to new buyers.

## How 2026 Dividend Cuts Affect Portfolio Yield

Even quality companies occasionally cut dividends. Model impact on your portfolio:

**Scenario: 15% Dividend Cut by SCHD**
- Current SCHD holding: $60,000
- Current SCHD yield: 4.45%
- Post-cut yield: 3.78%
- Income loss: $201 annually

Impact on portfolio:
- Portfolio yield: 3.82% → 3.68%
- Annual income: $3,823 → $3,680
- Percentage decline: 3.7%

Diversification limits impact. Even if SCHD cut dividends 15% (extremely unlikely), portfolio yield declines only 3.7%. A focused portfolio of 5 stocks where one cut dividends 15% would lose 3% of yield.

A married couple receiving $100,000 in qualified dividends pays zero federal taxes (0% bracket for married filing jointly up to $94,050). A single person in high tax bracket pays 20% federal tax plus 3.8% net investment income tax, resulting in 23.8% total rate.

Tax-efficient dividend selection means focusing on qualified dividends from stocks and ETFs, avoiding non-qualified dividend sources (mutual fund short-term gains, REITs at higher earner levels).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I chase higher yields in 2026?**
A: No. Chasing yields by buying high-yielding positions with risks (higher debt, declining earnings) typically results in dividend cuts destroying returns. SCHD's 4.45% yield with growth and safety beats a 6% yield with decline risk.

**Q: How much impact do interest rates have on stock yields?**
A: Moderate impact. Rising rates typically compress valuations (lower dividend stock prices) and reduce dividend attractiveness. Declining rates typically expand valuations and increase dividend appeal. However, dividend growth matters more long-term than rate movements.

**Q: Is 4% yield achievable across an entire portfolio?**
A: Yes, with appropriate sector weighting. 60% SCHD (4.45%), 20% international (3.2%), 15% quality dividend stocks (2.8%), and 5% growth ETFs (1.85%) yields 3.82-4.0%.

**Q: What if I need higher yields for retirement income?**
A: Either build larger portfolio (3% yield on $2M = $60,000 vs. 4% on $1.5M = $60,000) or accept dividend cuts risk with higher-yielding positions. Most advisors recommend larger portfolio with lower-risk yields over concentrated positions with distribution risk.

**Q: How often do dividend yields change?**
A: Stock dividends change quarterly when companies announce new amounts. Stock prices change constantly. Your portfolio yield changes whenever stock prices or dividend amounts change significantly. Recalculate portfolio yield quarterly for accuracy.
