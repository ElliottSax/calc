## Understanding Total Return for Growth Stocks

Total return represents your investment's complete profitability, combining capital appreciation and dividend income. For side hustlers, calculating total return helps measure whether your growth stocks strategy outpaces inflation and builds sustainable wealth.

## The Total Return Formula

**Total Return (%) = [(Ending Value - Beginning Value + Dividends) / Beginning Value] × 100**

**Annualized Return (CAGR) = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/Years) - 1**

### Worked Example: Growth Stock Portfolio

Sarah invested $10,000 in a growth-focused portfolio with the following results:
- Beginning value: $10,000
- Ending value (3 years): $14,200
- Dividends received: $420

Total Return = [($14,200 - $10,000 + $420) / $10,000] × 100 = **46.2%**

CAGR = ($14,620 / $10,000)^(1/3) - 1 = **13.4% annually**

## Calculating Total Return Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Gather Your Data
- Track beginning balance (cost basis)
- Record ending account value
- Document all dividend payments
- Note cash deposits/withdrawals

### Step 2: Apply the Formula
For a growth stocks investment:
- **Growth Portfolio**: Focus on capital appreciation; dividends may be lower
- **Income Portfolio**: Higher dividend yield; lower capital appreciation

### Step 3: Calculate Annualized Return
With a 46.2% 3-year total return:
- Simple annual average: 46.2% ÷ 3 = 15.4%
- **True CAGR: 13.4%** (accounts for compounding)

## FAQ

**Q: How often should I calculate my total return?**
A: Quarterly reviews help track progress. Annual calculations provide clearer trends for tax planning.

**Q: Does my total return include reinvested dividends?**
A: The formula accounts for dividends received. If reinvested, they increase ending value, boosting total return.

**Q: What's a "good" total return for growth stocks?**
A: Targets vary by time horizon:
- 1-year: 8-15%
- 3-year: 10-14% annually
- 5-year: 9-12% annually
- 10-year: 8-11% annually

**Q: How do I calculate returns on irregular deposits?**
A: Use the Modified Dietz method for accounts with frequent cash flows:
- Weight each cash flow by the proportion of time it remained invested
- More accurate than simple CAGR for active savers

**Q: Should total return influence my investment decisions?**
A: Avoid chasing returns. Focus on:
- Asset allocation matching your time horizon
- Expense ratios below 0.20%
- Tax-loss harvesting opportunities
- Consistent contributions regardless of returns

## Key Takeaways

- Total return combines capital appreciation and income; it's more meaningful than price appreciation alone
- CAGR reveals your true annualized return, accounting for compounding
- Growth stocks offer different return profiles: growth stocks favor appreciation, dividend funds favor income
- Expense ratios compound over decades; target 0.03-0.25% for broad-market funds
- Side hustlers maximize returns through consistent investing and tax-efficient account selection
- Monitor returns quarterly but rebalance annually to maintain target allocations
