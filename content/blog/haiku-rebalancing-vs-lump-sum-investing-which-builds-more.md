# Rebalancing vs Lump Sum Investing: Which Builds More Wealth?

Lump sum investing means deploying all available capital into the market immediately, rather than gradually. Receiving a $100,000 inheritance and investing it immediately is lump sum investing. Adding rebalancing discipline improves outcomes.

## Lump Sum Investing Defined

Lump sum investing means deploying all available capital into the market immediately, rather than gradually over time. Receiving a $100,000 inheritance and investing it right away is lump sum investing.

## When Lump Sum Investing Wins

Historically, markets rise more than they fall. The longer your capital sits in cash, the higher the opportunity cost. Research shows lump sum investing beats dollar-cost averaging approximately 2 out of 3 times when markets appreciate.

## Data: Lump Sum vs. Rebalancing Lump Sums

Scenario: $100,000 to invest over 12 months:

**Lump Sum (all month 1):**
- Deployed immediately
- 20-year value: $673,000
- CAGR: 10.1%

**Rebalancing the Lump Sum (deploy, then rebalance quarterly):**
- Same initial deployment but disciplined rebalancing
- 20-year value: $685,000
- CAGR: 10.3%
- Benefit: 1.8% additional wealth through rebalancing discipline

Lump sum plus rebalancing beats both approaches separately.

## Rebalancing Principle Applied to Lump Sums

1. Invest lump sum immediately (don't wait for perfect timing)
2. Establish target allocation (60/40, 70/30, etc.)
3. Rebalance quarterly or annually
4. Add new money to asset classes below target weight
5. Harvest losses strategically during market downturns

This combines lump sum timing benefits with rebalancing discipline.

## Sequence of Returns Risk

Rebalancing protects against sequence risk—the order of returns mattering:

**Scenario A: Markets down 20%, then up 30%**
- Lump sum in month 1: 4% total return
- Rebalanced monthly: 6% total return (bought dips)

**Scenario B: Markets up 30%, then down 20%**
- Lump sum in month 1: 4% total return
- Rebalanced monthly: 3.5% total return

Rebalancing thrives in volatile markets, offering steady returns regardless of sequence.

## Real-World Example: Inheritance Investing

Inheriting $250,000:

**Month 1: Invest in 70/30 target allocation**
- Stocks: $175,000
- Bonds: $75,000

**Quarter 2: Rebalance after market moves**
- Stocks now worth $162,000 (80% of portfolio)
- Bonds now worth $78,000 (20% of portfolio)
- Sell $7,000 stocks, buy bonds
- Maintains discipline, locks gains

This approach captures immediate market exposure while maintaining diversification.

## Psychological Benefits of Rebalancing Lump Sums

1. Reduces regret if markets fall immediately after investment
2. Enforces discipline to not chase performance
3. Provides decision rules that reduce emotional trading
4. Ensures buying low and selling high systematically

These psychological factors often outweigh pure math returns.

## Lump Sum Timing Risk

The risk of lump sum investing:
- Invested $100,000 at market peak in 2000
- Market declined 50% by 2002
- Recovery required 15 years
- During that time, dollar-cost averaging would have captured lower prices

This extreme scenario is why rebalancing adds psychological protection.

## FAQ

**Q: Should I wait to invest a large lump sum?**
A: No. Time in market beats market timing. Invest immediately and establish rebalancing discipline.

**Q: Can I rebalance too soon after a lump sum investment?**
A: Wait 1-3 months, then establish quarterly rebalancing. This avoids reacting to short-term noise.

**Q: What if I can't invest the lump sum all at once?**
A: Dollar-cost average across 3-6 months, then rebalance quarterly. This captures market exposure while managing risk.

**Q: Is rebalancing lump sums different from regular rebalancing?**
A: No. Same principles apply. Just rebalance toward target allocations after initial deployment.

**Q: Should I use limit orders when deploying lump sums?**
A: No. Buy at market price immediately. Waiting for limit prices defeats the time-in-market advantage.
