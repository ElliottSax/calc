## What Is Compound Interest?

**Compound interest** is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest (which only applies to the principal), compound interest allows your investment to grow exponentially.

The formula for compound interest is:

$$
A = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}
$$

Where:
- $ A $ = future value of the investment
- $ P $ = principal investment amount
- $ r $ = annual interest rate (decimal)
- $ n $ = number of times interest is compounded per year
- $ t $ = number of years

### Example: $10,000 at 7% compounded annually for 30 years

$$
A = 10,000 \left(1 + \frac{0.07}{1}\right)^{1 \times 30} = 10,000 \times (1.07)^{30} = \$76,122.55
$$

Compare that to **simple interest**:
$$
10,000 + (10,000 \times 0.07 \times 30) = \$31,000
$$

The **difference of \$45,122.55** is the power of compounding.

## Why Compound Interest Matters for Long-Term Growth

Time is the most critical variable in compound growth. The longer your money compounds, the greater the acceleration.

Let’s illustrate with two investors:

| Investor | Starts at Age | Monthly Investment | Annual Return | Stops Investing at Age | Final Value at Age 65 |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|----------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Alex    | 25            | \$500               | 7%             | 65                       | \$1,178,345            |
| Jamie   | 35            | \$500               | 7%             | 65                       | \$567,733              |

**Calculation for Alex:**
- 40 years of investing
- Monthly contribution: \$500
- Annual return: 7%, compounded monthly
- Formula: Future Value of Annuity

$$
FV = P \times \frac{(1 + r)^n - 1}{r}
$$
- $ P = \$500 $
- $ r = 0.07 / 12 = 0.005833 $
- $ n = 40 \times 12 = 480 $

$$
FV = 500 \times \frac{(1 + 0.005833)^{480} - 1}{0.005833} = \$1,178,345
$$

Jamie, starting 10 years later, ends with **less than half**—despite investing the same amount monthly.

This is the **mathematical penalty of delay**. Starting early isn’t just helpful—it’s transformative.

## How Frequency of Compounding Changes Outcomes

More frequent compounding = faster growth.

Let’s compare annual vs. monthly compounding on a \$50,000 investment at 8% over 25 years.

| Compounding Frequency | Formula | Final Value |
|------------------------|--------|-------------|
| Annual | $50,000 \times (1 + 0.08)^{25}$ | \$342,424 |
| Monthly | $50,000 \times \left(1 + \frac{0.08}{12}\right)^{300}$ | \$366,093 |

**Difference: \$23,669** — just from monthly compounding.

This is why ETFs and mutual funds that pay and reinvest dividends **quarterly or monthly** outperform those with annual payouts.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### **How does compound interest work with ETFs like VOO or SCHD?**  
VOO and SCHD pay dividends quarterly. When reinvested, those dividends buy more shares, which then generate their own dividends. Over time, this creates exponential growth. For example, \$10,000 in SCHD in 2012 is now worth over \$50,000, with reinvested dividends accounting for ~40% of total return.

### **Is compound interest only for stocks?**  
No. Bonds, CDs, and savings accounts also compound—but at lower rates. A 4% CD compounded annually turns \$10,000 into \$26,658 in 25 years. That’s growth, but it’s dwarfed by equities. For **long-term growth**, equities are essential.

### **What if I can only invest \$100/month?**  
Start anyway. \$100/month at 9% for 40 years = \$464,354. Time magnifies small contributions. The key is consistency.

### **Does inflation hurt compound interest?**  
Yes—but equities historically outpace inflation. The S&P 500 has averaged 10.2% nominal returns vs. 2.9% inflation (1926–2023). Real return: **7.3%**. Your \$10,000 grows to \$80,000 in 30 years in real terms.

### **Can I lose money with compound interest?**  
Only if the underlying asset loses value or you stop contributing. Market downturns are temporary. From 2000–2010, the S&P 500 had zero returns—but with dividends reinvested, it gained 1.8% annually. Stay invested.

### **How do taxes affect compound interest?**  
Taxes slow compounding. Use tax-advantaged accounts:
- **Roth IRA**: Tax-free growth
- **401(k)**: Tax-deferred growth
- **Taxable account**: Dividends and capital gains taxed annually

A \$500,000 portfolio in a Roth IRA compounds fully. In a taxable account, taxes on dividends (15–20%) reduce reinvestment power.

### **Should I pick high-dividend or high-growth stocks?**  
Balance both. SCHD offers yield + growth. VOO offers broad exposure. A portfolio of VOO + SCHD captures total market growth and dividend stability.

### **What’s the minimum time needed to benefit from compounding?**  
You see real acceleration after **10+ years**. But even 5 years helps. \$10,000 at 8% for 5 years = \$14,693. The longer you wait, the more you lose.
