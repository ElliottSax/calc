# How to Use Stock Valuation for Retirement

Stock valuation determines whether your retirement portfolio generates sustainable income. Overpaying for stocks destroys long-term returns; undervaluing quality companies misses compounding opportunities. This guide reveals mathematical valuation approaches enabling retirement investors to identify stocks generating reliable income while protecting purchasing power.

## Understanding Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio Valuation

The P/E ratio measures how expensive a stock is relative to earnings:

$$\text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{\text{Stock Price}}{\text{Earnings Per Share (EPS)}}$$

**Example: AAPL valuation analysis (2026)**

Stock price: $185
EPS: $5.90
P/E ratio: $185 ÷ $5.90 = 31.4×

This means investors pay $31.40 for every $1 of annual earnings—expensive by historical standards.

Compare valuation frameworks:

| Company | Stock Price | EPS | P/E Ratio | Valuation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AAPL | $185 | $5.90 | 31.4 | Expensive |
| KO | $64 | $2.50 | 25.6 | Reasonable |
| JNJ | $155 | $5.75 | 27.0 | Reasonable |
| VOO (S&P 500) | - | - | 24.5 | Reasonable |

For retirement portfolios, target P/E ratios below market average (24.5) to identify valuation margin of safety.

## Dividend Yield as Valuation Indicator

Dividend yield indicates the income-generation rate of retirement portfolios:

$$\text{Dividend Yield} = \frac{\text{Annual Dividend Per Share}}{\text{Stock Price}}$$

**Example: JNJ dividend valuation (2026)**

Stock price: $155
Annual dividend: $6.50
Dividend yield: $6.50 ÷ $155 = 4.19%

Compare dividend yields across portfolio:

SCHD and JNJ offer superior income (4-4.5% yield) while VOO prioritizes growth (1.3% yield).

## The Dividend Discount Model for Retirement Valuation

Intrinsic value calculation through dividend discount model:

$$\text{Intrinsic Value} = \frac{D_1}{r - g}$$

Where:
- D₁ = Next year's expected dividend
- r = Required return (discount rate)
- g = Dividend growth rate

**Example: KO valuation using dividend discount model**

Current dividend: $2.08
Expected dividend growth: 3% annually
Required return: 8%

Next year's dividend: $2.08 × 1.03 = $2.142

$$\text{Intrinsic Value} = \frac{\$2.142}{0.08 - 0.03} = \frac{\$2.142}{0.05} = \$42.84$$

Current price: $64

Valuation assessment: Stock trades at $64 vs. intrinsic value of $42.84—overvalued by 49%.

**Decision framework:**
- Intrinsic value $42.84 suggests avoid purchase at $64
- However, if KO increases dividend growth to 4% (higher growth model):

$$\text{Intrinsic Value} = \frac{\$2.142}{0.08 - 0.04} = \frac{\$2.142}{0.04} = \$53.55$$

At 4% growth assumption, KO's intrinsic value rises to $53.55—still below $64 but less egregiously overvalued.

## Free Cash Flow Analysis for Retirement Sustainability

Dividend sustainability depends on free cash flow (FCF):

$$\text{Free Cash Flow} = \text{Operating Cash Flow} - \text{Capital Expenditures}$$

$$\text{Dividend Payout Ratio} = \frac{\text{Annual Dividends}}{\text{Free Cash Flow}}$$

**Example: Johnson & Johnson dividend sustainability**

Operating cash flow: $28.5 billion
Capital expenditures: $3.5 billion
Free cash flow: $25.0 billion

Annual dividends: $10.5 billion

Payout ratio: $10.5B ÷ $25.0B = 42%

Assessment: JNJ pays dividends from only 42% of free cash flow—highly sustainable. The company retains 58% ($12.5B) for reinvestment and growth, enabling future dividend increases.

Compare to risky payout ratio:

| Company | FCF | Dividends | Payout Ratio | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| JNJ | $25.0B | $10.5B | 42% | Safe |
| PG | $18.0B | $8.5B | 47% | Safe |
| KO | $8.5B | $3.5B | 41% | Safe |
| Concerning Company | $2.0B | $1.9B | 95% | Risky |

Payout ratios above 75% signal risk—cuts occur when earnings decline. Target companies with 40-60% payout ratios for sustainable retirement income.

## Earnings Growth Rate Analysis

Valuation multiples should reflect growth rates:

$$\text{PEG Ratio} = \frac{\text{P/E Ratio}}{\text{Earnings Growth Rate \%}}$$

A PEG ratio below 1.0 suggests undervaluation; above 2.0 suggests overvaluation.

**Example: Dividend growth stock valuation**

| Stock | P/E | Growth Rate | PEG | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SCHD | 22.5 | 5% | 4.5 | Overvalued |
| JNJ | 27.0 | 4% | 6.75 | Overvalued |
| KO | 25.6 | 3% | 8.5 | Very Overvalued |
| VOO | 24.5 | 7% | 3.5 | Reasonable |

Dividend aristocrats (slow growth, stable dividends) typically show high PEG ratios. They're valued for reliability and dividend income, not growth. Retirees accept high PEG ratios for dividend quality.

## Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) for Retirement Planning

DCF projects all future cash flows and discounts to present value:

**Simplified DCF: PG dividends, 30-year horizon**

Current annual dividend: $2.88
Expected dividend growth: 4% annually
Discount rate: 8%

Formula:
$$\text{DCF Value} = \sum_{t=1}^{30} \frac{D_t}{(1.08)^t}$$

Where D_t = Dividend in year t

Year 1: $2.88 ÷ 1.08 = $2.67
Year 5: $3.51 ÷ (1.08)^5 = $2.39
Year 10: $4.27 ÷ (1.08)^10 = $1.98
Year 20: $6.34 ÷ (1.08)^20 = $1.36
Year 30: $9.41 ÷ (1.08)^30 = $0.80

Summing all 30 years: Approximately $48-50 intrinsic value

Current price: $165

Assessment: At current price, PG appears expensive for dividend investors. However, for long-term dividend growth, PG's 4% annual dividend growth justifies premium valuations.

## Valuation Shifts During Market Cycles

Market cycles create valuation opportunities and risks for retirees:

**Valuation expansion/contraction impact:**

A stock with $5 earnings can trade at various multiples:

| Market Condition | P/E Multiple | Stock Price | Valuation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Recession/bear market | 15× | $75 | Depressed |
| Normal/moderate | 20× | $100 | Fair |
| Expansion/bull market | 25× | $125 | Elevated |
| Bubble | 30× | $150 | Dangerous |

The same $5 earnings per share trades from $75 to $150 based purely on valuation multiple—no earnings change required.

Retirement strategy: Buy quality dividend stocks during recessions (15× multiples) and hold through bull markets. Forced selling during downturns locks losses.

## Currency Risk in International Valuation

International dividend stocks introduce currency risk:

**VXUS (international) dividend comparison**

Company: German DAX firm
Annual dividend: €2.50
Stock price: €50
Dividend yield: 5% (excellent)

However, if EUR/USD exchange rate declines from 1.15 to 1.05:
- Dividend in USD: €2.50 × 1.05 = $2.625 (was €2.50 × 1.15 = $2.875)
- Effective yield declines from 5.75% to 5.25% purely from currency movement

For retirement portfolios, 70% allocation to U.S.-based dividends (SCHD, JNJ, KO, PG) reduces currency risk while maintaining international diversification (30% VXUS).

## FAQ

**Q: What P/E ratio should I target for retirement dividend stocks?**

Target P/E ratios 20-25× for quality dividend companies. Below 20× offers margin of safety. Above 30× requires conviction in future growth. Dividend stocks sacrifice growth for income—don't overpay. The best buy is a quality company trading at 15-20× earnings with 3.5-4.5% dividend yield.

**Q: Is dividend yield the only valuation metric I should use?**

No. Combine dividend yield with payout ratio, dividend growth history, and free cash flow. A 6% yield with 90% payout ratio signals risk (dividend cuts likely). A 3% yield with 40% payout ratio signals safety and future growth potential. Always verify dividend sustainability through cash flow analysis.

**Q: Should I use DCF or simpler metrics like P/E?**

For beginners, P/E and dividend yield suffice. For precision, DCF provides deeper insights but requires earnings growth assumptions. Use both: P/E for quick screening, DCF for serious purchases. A quality dividend stock at 20× earnings with 4% yield combined with DCF valuation of $150 vs. current $120 confirms attractiveness.

**Q: How do I know when to sell based on valuation?**

Sell when a position becomes overvalued (P/E >30×, yield <2%, payout >80%) or fundamentals deteriorate (dividend cuts, earnings declines). Hold through moderate overvaluation if dividend growth remains strong. Rebalance by redirecting new capital to undervalued positions rather than selling winners.

**Q: Does valuation matter for ETFs like SCHD or VOO?**

Yes. SCHD trades at market P/E × composition (currently elevated because dividend stocks are popular). VOO reflects S&P 500 average valuation. When broad market P/E exceeds 28×, consider increasing bond allocation. When P/E falls below 20×, maximize stock allocation. Valuation signals still apply to broadly diversified funds.
