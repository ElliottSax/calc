# Rebalancing vs Income Investing: Which Builds More Wealth?

Income investing prioritizes generating cash flow through dividends and interest. Portfolios concentrate on dividend aristocrats like JNJ, KO, and PG, plus REITs, yielding 4-6% annually. Rebalancing maintains discipline while allowing dividend focus.

## Income Investing Defined

Income investing prioritizes generating cash flow through dividends and interest. Portfolios concentrate on dividend aristocrats (JNJ, KO, PG), REITs, and bonds yielding 4-6% annually. The goal is steady cash flow rather than capital appreciation.

## Rebalancing's Role in Income Portfolios

Even income investors benefit from rebalancing. As bonds decline in yield, rebalancing forces taking profits from strong dividend stocks and reinvesting in bonds, maintaining risk balance.

## 30-Year Wealth Comparison: Income vs. Rebalancing

$100,000 starting capital:

**Income-Focused Portfolio (70% dividend stocks, 30% bonds):**
- Average yield: 4.5%
- Annual dividend: $4,500
- Reinvested: compounds to $892,000
- CAGR: 8.7%

**Rebalanced Growth Portfolio (70% growth stocks, 30% bonds):**
- Average yield: 1.5%
- Annual appreciation: 8.5%
- Reinvested: compounds to $1,042,000
- CAGR: 9.2%

Growth with rebalancing edges out pure income investing by modest amounts.

## The Reinvestment Imperative

Income investors often make this critical mistake: taking dividends as cash instead of reinvesting.

- Taking dividends: 30-year return = $592,000
- Reinvesting dividends: 30-year return = $892,000
- Difference: $300,000 (50% more wealth)

Automatic dividend reinvestment (DRIP) is absolutely essential for wealth building.

## Rebalancing Within Income Portfolios

Rebalancing enhances income investing by:
1. Reducing concentration risk in best performers
2. Locking in gains from surging dividend stocks
3. Systematically deploying cash to high-yield opportunities
4. Managing tax efficiency through strategic sales

Income plus rebalancing discipline equals sustainable wealth growth.

## Tax Efficiency: Income vs. Rebalancing

In taxable accounts:
- Income portfolio generates 4.5% taxable income annually (substantial burden)
- Rebalanced growth portfolio defers taxes until sale
- Place income-generating investments in tax-advantaged accounts
- Use growth stocks in taxable accounts for preferential capital gains treatment

Proper placement of assets by account type amplifies after-tax returns significantly.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I build wealth solely on dividend income?**
A: Yes, if you reinvest. The combination of 4-5% dividends plus reinvestment compounds to 8-9% annually.

**Q: Should retirees skip rebalancing for income?**
A: No. Even retirees benefit from rebalancing to avoid concentration risk in their now-static portfolio.

**Q: Is 5% dividend portfolio income retirement-proof?**
A: Partly. 5% on $1 million = $50,000 annually. Plan for additional Social Security or other income sources for security.

**Q: Does rebalancing reduce dividend income?**
A: No. Rebalancing maintains overall yield. Selling appreciated dividend stocks funds bond purchases without reducing total income.

**Q: Should I choose JNJ or dividend ETF?**
A: Dividend ETF is simpler. JNJ single stocks create concentration risk. SCHD (dividend ETF) provides diversification with similar yields.
