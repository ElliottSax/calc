## Introduction
Dividend investing is a popular strategy among investors seeking regular income and long-term growth. Two common approaches to dividend investing are investing in top dividend ETFs and individual dividend-paying stocks. As of 2026, the dividend investing landscape offers a wide range of opportunities, with many ETFs and stocks providing attractive yields and relatively stable payouts. In this article, we will explore the top dividend ETFs and individual stocks, analyzing their yields, payout ratios, and other key metrics to help investors make informed decisions.

## Analysis
When evaluating dividend ETFs, investors should consider factors such as the fund's underlying holdings, dividend yield, and expense ratio. One of the top dividend ETFs is the Vanguard Dividend Appreciation ETF (VIG), which has a dividend yield of 2.15% and a payout ratio of 54.6% as of February 2026. Another popular option is the iShares Select Dividend ETF (DVY), which offers a dividend yield of 3.18% and a payout ratio of 63.2%. The SPDR S&P 500 Dividend ETF (SDY) is also a notable option, with a dividend yield of 2.63% and a payout ratio of 58.5%.

In contrast, individual dividend-paying stocks offer investors the opportunity to own a specific company with a proven track record of dividend payments. For example, Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) has a dividend yield of 2.83% and a payout ratio of 55.6% as of February 2026, making it an attractive option for income-seeking investors. Another individual stock worth considering is Procter & Gamble (PG), which has a dividend yield of 2.45% and a payout ratio of 61.3%. The Coca-Cola Company (KO) is also a notable dividend payer, with a dividend yield of 2.93% and a payout ratio of 77.4%.

## Comparison
When comparing top dividend ETFs to individual stocks, investors should consider the benefits and drawbacks of each approach. One key advantage of dividend ETFs is their diversification benefits, as they typically hold a large portfolio of stocks, reducing the risk of any one individual stock. For example, the Vanguard Dividend Appreciation ETF (VIG) holds over 180 stocks, including well-known companies such as Microsoft (MSFT) and 3M (MMM). In contrast, individual stocks offer investors the potential for higher yields and more control over their portfolio, but also come with higher risk.

A comparison of the dividend yields and payout ratios of top dividend ETFs and individual stocks reveals some interesting insights. As of February 2026, the iShares Select Dividend ETF (DVY) has a higher dividend yield than many individual stocks, including Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) and Procter & Gamble (PG). However, the payout ratio of DVY is also higher, at 63.2%, compared to 55.6% for JNJ and 61.3% for PG. This suggests that DVY may be more vulnerable to dividend cuts or reductions in the future.

## Strategy
Investors seeking to implement a dividend investing strategy should consider a combination of top dividend ETFs and individual stocks. One potential approach is to allocate a core portion of the portfolio to a dividend ETF, such as the Vanguard Dividend Appreciation ETF (VIG) or the SPDR S&P 500 Dividend ETF (SDY), and then supplement with individual stocks, such as Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) or The Coca-Cola Company (KO). This approach can provide a diversified portfolio with a relatively stable income stream and the potential for long-term growth.
