# Best Dividend ETFs for Passive Income in 2026

Dividend ETFs offer the simplest path to diversified income investing. Instead of picking individual stocks and monitoring their financials, you can buy a single fund that holds dozens or hundreds of dividend-paying companies. In 2026, the dividend ETF landscape is more competitive than ever, with options ranging from high-yield funds to dividend growth strategies. Here is our analysis of the best dividend ETFs for passive income this year.

## Why Choose Dividend ETFs Over Individual Stocks

Individual dividend stock picking can be rewarding, but it demands significant time and expertise. Dividend ETFs solve several problems:

- **Instant diversification** across 50 to 500+ companies
- **Professional selection** based on quantitative screens and rules
- **Automatic rebalancing** when holdings change
- **Lower risk** from any single company cutting its dividend
- **Tax efficiency** through in-kind creation and redemption
- **Low costs** with expense ratios as low as 0.06%

The trade-off is that you accept the fund's methodology rather than customizing your own portfolio. But for most investors, this is a feature rather than a limitation.

## Top Dividend ETFs for 2026: Comprehensive Comparison

### Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF (SCHD)

SCHD has become the most popular dividend ETF among income investors, and for good reason. It tracks the Dow Jones U.S. Dividend 100 Index, which selects stocks based on financial strength, dividend consistency, and yield.

- **Yield:** ~3.5%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.06%
- **Holdings:** ~100 stocks
- **Top Holdings:** Broadcom, AbbVie, Merck, Home Depot, Cisco
- **5-Year Annualized Return:** ~12.4%
- **Strategy:** Quality dividend stocks with growth potential

**Why We Like It:** SCHD combines a meaningful yield with strong total return. Its quality screens eliminate dividend traps, and the 0.06% expense ratio is among the lowest available. This is the single best all-around dividend ETF for most investors.

### Vanguard High Dividend Yield ETF (VYM)

VYM tracks the FTSE High Dividend Yield Index and casts a wider net than SCHD, holding approximately 450 stocks. It focuses purely on above-average yielding U.S. stocks.

- **Yield:** ~2.9%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.06%
- **Holdings:** ~450 stocks
- **Top Holdings:** Broadcom, JPMorgan, ExxonMobil, Johnson & Johnson
- **5-Year Annualized Return:** ~10.8%
- **Strategy:** Broad high-yield exposure

**Why We Like It:** VYM offers the broadest diversification of any pure dividend ETF. With 450+ holdings, the impact of any single dividend cut is minimal. Ideal for conservative investors who want set-it-and-forget-it income.

### iShares Core High Dividend ETF (HDV)

HDV focuses on high-yield U.S. stocks screened for financial health. It holds roughly 75 stocks, making it more concentrated than VYM but with a higher yield.

- **Yield:** ~3.8%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.08%
- **Holdings:** ~75 stocks
- **Top Holdings:** ExxonMobil, AbbVie, Johnson & Johnson, Chevron, Verizon
- **5-Year Annualized Return:** ~9.5%
- **Strategy:** High income with quality screen

**Why We Like It:** HDV delivers a higher yield than most competitors by concentrating in energy, healthcare, and consumer staples. Its financial health screen helps avoid dividend traps.

### Vanguard Dividend Appreciation ETF (VIG)

VIG takes a different approach — it focuses on dividend growth rather than current yield. It holds companies with 10+ consecutive years of dividend increases.

- **Yield:** ~1.8%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.06%
- **Holdings:** ~315 stocks
- **Top Holdings:** Apple, Broadcom, Microsoft, JPMorgan, UnitedHealth
- **5-Year Annualized Return:** ~13.2%
- **Strategy:** Dividend growth and capital appreciation

**Why We Like It:** VIG's lower current yield is offset by superior total returns. Companies that consistently grow dividends tend to be high-quality businesses. This is the best choice for investors who want income growth over time.

### ProShares S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats ETF (NOBL)

NOBL tracks the S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats — companies that have increased dividends for 25+ consecutive years. This is the purest play on dividend reliability.

- **Yield:** ~2.3%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.35%
- **Holdings:** ~67 stocks
- **Top Holdings:** Equal-weighted (each holding ~1.5%)
- **5-Year Annualized Return:** ~9.8%
- **Strategy:** Proven dividend growers, equal-weighted

**Why We Like It:** NOBL's equal weighting prevents any single stock from dominating. Its Aristocrat requirement ensures every holding has a proven 25+ year dividend growth track record.

### JPMorgan Equity Premium Income ETF (JEPI)

JEPI has become one of the fastest-growing ETFs in history. It combines dividend stocks with covered call options to generate high monthly income.

- **Yield:** ~7.5%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.35%
- **Holdings:** ~130 stocks + ELN options overlay
- **Top Holdings:** Progressive, Amazon, Meta, Mastercard, Visa
- **Strategy:** High current income through options premium

**Why We Like It:** JEPI delivers the highest yield on this list with monthly distributions. The covered call strategy caps some upside but generates consistent premium income. Best for investors who prioritize current income over growth.

### SPDR Portfolio S&P 500 High Dividend ETF (SPYD)

SPYD holds the 80 highest-yielding stocks in the S&P 500, equally weighted. This simple methodology captures the highest yields from large-cap stocks.

- **Yield:** ~4.3%
- **Expense Ratio:** 0.07%
- **Holdings:** ~80 stocks
- **Top Holdings:** Equal-weighted across REITs, utilities, financials, energy
- **5-Year Annualized Return:** ~8.5%
- **Strategy:** Highest-yielding S&P 500 stocks

**Why We Like It:** SPYD provides the highest pure dividend yield among traditional ETFs at a very low cost. Heavy REIT and utility exposure makes it interest rate sensitive, but the equal weighting keeps it diversified.

## How to Choose the Right Dividend ETF

Your choice depends on what you value most:

**Maximum current income:** JEPI (7.5%) or SPYD (4.3%) deliver the most cash today. JEPI uses options to boost yield beyond what pure dividend stocks can provide.

**Best total return:** VIG (13.2%) and SCHD (12.4%) have delivered the strongest risk-adjusted total returns over the past five years. Their focus on quality over yield drives superior long-term performance.

**Broadest diversification:** VYM with its 450+ holdings provides the most diversification and lowest single-stock risk.

**Lowest cost:** SCHD, VYM, and VIG all charge just 0.06% — that is $6 per year on a $10,000 investment.

## Building a Multi-ETF Income Portfolio

You do not have to choose just one. A blended approach can optimize for both current income and growth:

| ETF | Allocation | Role |

| SCHD | 40% | Core income + growth engine |
| VIG | 25% | Dividend growth component |
| JEPI | 20% | Income booster |
| HDV | 15% | High-yield complement |

This blend would produce approximately 3.8% yield with strong total return potential. On a $500,000 portfolio, that generates roughly $19,000 per year, or $1,583 per month.

## Tax Considerations for Dividend ETFs

Most dividends from U.S. equity ETFs qualify for the lower qualified dividend tax rate (0%, 15%, or 20%). However, JEPI's options premium income is taxed as ordinary income, making it less tax-efficient in taxable accounts.

Consider holding JEPI and other income-heavy funds in tax-advantaged accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s) while keeping tax-efficient funds like VIG and VYM in taxable accounts.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is SCHD or VYM better for dividends?

SCHD offers a higher yield (3.5% vs 2.9%) and has delivered better total returns over the past five years. However, VYM provides broader diversification with 450+ holdings. For most investors, SCHD is the better choice, but VYM is preferable if you want maximum diversification.

### Can I live off dividend ETF income?

Yes, but you need sufficient capital. At a 3.5% yield (SCHD), you would need approximately $685,000 to generate $24,000 per year ($2,000/month). Using higher-yield ETFs like JEPI can reduce the capital required but with different risk characteristics.

### How often do dividend ETFs pay?

Most dividend ETFs pay quarterly (SCHD, VYM, VIG, NOBL). JEPI and a few others pay monthly. You can combine quarterly payers with different schedules to receive income every month.

### Are dividend ETFs better than bonds for income?

Dividend ETFs offer growing income that keeps pace with inflation, while bonds pay fixed interest. Over the long term, dividend ETFs have historically provided better total returns. However, bonds are less volatile and may be more appropriate for short-term income needs within 1-3 years.

### What is the minimum investment to start with dividend ETFs?

Most brokers allow you to buy single shares of ETFs. With SCHD trading around $80 per share, you can start investing in dividend ETFs with under $100. Many brokers also offer fractional shares, allowing you to start with as little as $1.
