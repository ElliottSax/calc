# Government Employee Dividend Plan: TSP and Supplemental Income

Government employees enjoy some of the best retirement benefits in the American workforce: the Thrift Savings Plan with its ultra-low fees, FERS pension providing guaranteed lifetime income, and Social Security. Yet many federal and state workers leave significant wealth on the table by parking TSP funds in the G Fund, not maximizing their match, and failing to build supplemental dividend income outside of retirement accounts.

The average federal employee retires with a FERS annuity of $24,000-$38,000/year. Combined with Social Security ($24,000-$36,000/year), that is $48,000-$74,000 annually -- comfortable but not luxurious. Adding $20,000-$40,000 in annual dividend income transforms a modest government retirement into a genuinely affluent one.

## The Government Employee Triple Advantage

### Advantage 1: TSP -- The Best Retirement Plan in America

The Thrift Savings Plan offers:
- **Expense ratio**: 0.048% ($4.80 per $10,000 invested annually)
- **FERS matching**: 1% automatic + up to 4% matched = 5% employer contribution
- **Annual limit**: $23,500 employee + employer match (2025)
- **Catch-up**: Additional $7,500 at age 50+ ($31,000 total)
- **Roth option**: Tax-free growth and withdrawals

No private sector 401(k) matches the TSP's combination of low fees and generous matching. The 5% match on a $90,000 salary contributes $4,500/year in free money.

### Advantage 2: FERS Pension

The Federal Employees Retirement System provides:
- **Annuity formula**: 1% (or 1.1% if retiring at 62+) x years of service x high-3 average salary
- **30 years at $90,000**: 1.1% x 30 x $90,000 = $29,700/year
- **COLA adjustments**: CPI-based annual increases (though capped)
- **Survivor benefits**: Spouse receives 50% or 25% of annuity

### Advantage 3: Social Security

Unlike some state pension systems, FERS employees participate in Social Security:
- **30-year federal employee retiring at 67**: ~$28,000-$35,000/year in SS benefits
- **Combined with FERS pension**: $57,700-$64,700/year before any personal investments

### The Gap That Dividends Fill

$60,000/year from FERS + SS is solid. But many retirees want $80,000-$100,000+ to travel, support grandchildren, handle healthcare costs, and enjoy retirement fully. The $20,000-$40,000 gap is exactly what a dividend portfolio provides.

## TSP Optimization for Maximum Growth

### Stop Using the G Fund

The TSP G Fund (government securities) is the default and the most popular fund. It is also the worst choice for long-term wealth building:

- G Fund 10-year average return: 2.3%
- C Fund (S&P 500) 10-year average return: 12.1%
- S Fund (Small/Mid-cap) 10-year average return: 10.8%

A $500,000 TSP balance growing at 2.3% (G Fund) for 10 years: $628,000
A $500,000 TSP balance growing at 11% (C/S blend) for 10 years: $1,420,000

The difference: $792,000. The G Fund's "safety" costs you hundreds of thousands of dollars over a career.

### Optimal TSP Allocation by Age

**Ages 25-40: Aggressive Growth**
- C Fund: 60% (S&P 500 index)
- S Fund: 25% (Small/mid-cap index)
- I Fund: 15% (International index)
- Expected return: 10-11% annually

**Ages 41-50: Growth with Moderation**
- C Fund: 50%
- S Fund: 20%
- I Fund: 15%
- F Fund: 10% (Bond index)
- G Fund: 5%

**Ages 51-57: Balanced**
- C Fund: 40%
- S Fund: 10%
- I Fund: 10%
- F Fund: 20%
- G Fund: 20%

**Ages 58-62: Conservative Growth**
- C Fund: 30%
- F Fund: 30%
- G Fund: 30%
- I Fund: 10%

### TSP Contribution Strategy

Always contribute at least 5% to receive the full employer match. Ideally, max out at $23,500/year ($31,000 at 50+):

| Salary | 5% (Match) | 10% | Max ($23,500) |
|--------|-----------|-----|---------------|
| $60,000 | $3,000 | $6,000 | $23,500 |
| $80,000 | $4,000 | $8,000 | $23,500 |
| $100,000 | $5,000 | $10,000 | $23,500 |

With 5% match, maxing at $100,000 salary means $23,500 + $5,000 match = $28,500/year into TSP.

## Building Supplemental Dividend Income

### Beyond TSP: The Dividend Layer

After maximizing TSP, build a separate dividend portfolio for supplemental income and early-retirement flexibility:

**Step 1: Roth IRA ($7,000/year)**
- All government employees (under income limits) should max a Roth IRA
- Invest in dividend growth ETFs: SCHD (60%), VIG (40%)
- Tax-free growth and withdrawals supplement TSP and pension

**Step 2: Taxable Brokerage (Remaining Available Savings)**
- Focus on qualified dividend stocks for preferential tax treatment
- Build toward $500,000-$800,000 for $17,500-$28,000/year in dividends
- This income starts flowing before retirement age, providing early flexibility

## The Complete Government Retirement Package

### 30-Year Career Scenario: GS-13 Retiring at 60

**FERS Pension:**
- High-3 salary: $115,000
- Annuity: 1.0% x 30 x $115,000 = $34,500/year
- COLA adjustments: ~2% annually

**Social Security (starting at 62):**
- Estimated benefit: $30,000/year (reduced for early claiming)
- At age 67 (FRA): $36,000/year

**TSP (maxed for 30 years, C/S/I allocation):**
- Estimated balance: $1,800,000-$2,500,000

- Estimated portfolio: $760,000

**Total Retirement Income at 62:**

This federal employee retires at 60 with income exceeding their working salary, plus growing dividend income that increases every year.

### Early Retirement: The FERS MRA+10 Option

FERS employees can retire as early as their Minimum Retirement Age (55-57) with 10+ years of service, receiving a reduced annuity. Dividend income makes this viable:

**MRA+10 at age 57 (20 years of service):**
- Reduced FERS annuity: ~$16,000/year
- No Social Security until 62
- TSP access: Available at separation
- Dividend income: $15,000-$25,000/year

Without dividends, the MRA+10 annuity alone is insufficient. With a dividend portfolio providing $15,000-$25,000, the combination ($31,000-$41,000) covers basic expenses until SS kicks in at 62.

## State and Local Government Employee Considerations

### State Pension Integration

Many state employees participate in state pension systems instead of (or in addition to) Social Security:

- **CalPERS (California)**: 2% at 60 formula. 30 years at $80,000 = $48,000/year pension
- **NYSLRS (New York)**: Tier 6: 1.67-2% formula depending on years of service
- **TRS (Texas Teacher Retirement)**: 2.3% formula. 30 years at $60,000 = $41,400/year pension

State employees without Social Security (about 25% of state/local workers) need larger dividend portfolios to compensate.

### 457(b) Deferred Compensation

State and local government employees often have access to 457(b) plans in addition to 401(k)/403(b):
- Additional $23,500/year contribution (separate from 401(k) limit)
- No 10% early withdrawal penalty at any age (unlike 401(k))
- Penalty-free access upon separation from service

The 457(b) combined with a Roth IRA and taxable dividend account creates a powerful supplemental income stream.

## FEHB: The Federal Healthcare Advantage

### Carrying FEHB Into Retirement

Federal employees who have been enrolled in FEHB (Federal Employees Health Benefits) for 5+ consecutive years before retirement can carry it into retirement. This is an enormous advantage:

- Government continues paying ~75% of the premium
- Coverage available from retirement until Medicare at 65 (and continues as supplement after 65)
- No coverage gap like many private sector retirees face

This removes the biggest wildcard from early retirement planning. Your dividend income does not need to cover expensive marketplace health insurance.

## Key Takeaways

- The TSP at 0.048% expense ratio with 5% match is the most powerful government benefit -- always max it out
- Stop using the G Fund for long-term investing; C/S/I funds provide dramatically higher returns
- A supplemental dividend portfolio of $760,000 adds $26,600/year on top of FERS pension, Social Security, and TSP
- FEHB continuity into retirement eliminates the healthcare planning burden that private sector retirees face
- A 30-year federal career with maxed TSP and supplemental dividends can produce $170,000+ in total retirement income
- The 457(b) plan (state/local employees) provides additional tax-advantaged space with no early withdrawal penalty

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I use the Roth TSP or Traditional TSP?

For most federal employees under GS-13, the Roth TSP is superior. Your current tax bracket (22-24%) is likely lower than your retirement bracket (when combining pension, SS, and TSP withdrawals). Roth contributions grow tax-free and are withdrawn tax-free, providing maximum flexibility. For GS-14+ employees in the 32%+ bracket, Traditional TSP may provide more immediate tax savings.

### How does the FERS Supplement work?

If you retire before 62 with 20+ years of service under FERS, the Special Retirement Supplement (SRS) provides an amount approximately equal to your Social Security benefit, paid monthly from retirement until age 62. However, it is subject to an earnings test if you work. Dividend income does not count as earnings for this test, making your dividend portfolio an ideal income supplement during the SRS period.

### My agency offers a 401(k) match in addition to TSP matching. Is that possible?

Some federal agencies (and most state/local governments) offer different matching structures. Always contribute enough to capture the full match from your employer. If you have access to both a 457(b) and a 401(k)/403(b), you can contribute the maximum to each, doubling your tax-advantaged investing space.

### I am a GS-7 earning $48,000. Can I really build a meaningful dividend portfolio?

Yes. At GS-7, focus on the TSP match (5% = $2,400/year) and a Roth IRA ($3,600/year to start). That is $6,000/year invested. Over 20 years at 10% returns, $6,000/year grows to $378,000, generating $13,230/year in dividends. As you receive step increases and promotions (GS-7 to GS-12 is common over 5-8 years), increase investments proportionally.

### Should I roll my TSP into an IRA after retiring?

Keep some money in TSP for the ultra-low 0.048% expense ratio. Roll a portion into a self-directed IRA if you want access to individual dividend stocks and dividend ETFs not available in the TSP's limited fund menu. A common approach: leave TSP funds in the L (Lifecycle) fund and roll over a portion for dividend-focused investing in an IRA.
