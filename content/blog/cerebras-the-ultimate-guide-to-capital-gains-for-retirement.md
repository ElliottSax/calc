# The Ultimate Guide to Capital Gains for Retirement

Retirement planning isn’t just about how much you save—it’s also about how your investments grow and how that growth is taxed. Among the most misunderstood components of investment taxation is **capital gains**. When managed strategically, capital gains can significantly enhance retirement income. Mismanaged, they can erode decades of disciplined saving.

This guide demystifies **capital gains for retirement**, explaining how long-term capital gains work, how tax rates apply, and how smart investors use asset location, timing, and tax-efficient funds to minimize tax drag. We’ll use real-world examples, current tax brackets, and data from major ETFs and stocks like **VTI**, **SCHD**, **VOO**, **JNJ**, and **KO** to ground our analysis.

---

## What Are Capital Gains?

A **capital gain** occurs when you sell an investment (such as stock, ETF, or real estate) for more than its purchase price. The difference between your **cost basis** (what you paid) and the **sale price** is your capital gain.

There are two types:

- **Short-term capital gains**: Apply to assets held **one year or less**. Taxed at your **ordinary income tax rate**.
- **Long-term capital gains**: Apply to assets held **more than one year**. Taxed at preferential rates: **0%, 15%, or 20%**, depending on taxable income.

**Why this matters for retirement**: Long-term capital gains are taxed more favorably than salary or short-term gains. By holding investments longer and selling strategically, retirees can reduce their tax burden and keep more of their returns.

## 2024 Long-Term Capital Gains Tax Rates

As of 2024, long-term capital gains tax rates are tied to taxable income and filing status. Here’s the current structure:

| Filing Status | 0% Rate | 15% Rate | 20% Rate |
|---------------|--------|--------|--------|
| Single | $0 – $47,025 | $47,026 – $518,900 | Over $518,900 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $0 – $94,050 | $94,051 – $583,750 | Over $583,750 |
| Head of Household | $0 – $63,000 | $63,001 – $551,350 | Over $551,350 |

*Source: IRS Rev. Proc. 2023-34 (inflation-adjusted 2024 brackets)*

**Example**: A married couple withdrawing $100,000 from a taxable brokerage account in retirement, with $80,000 in long-term capital gains, may pay **0% in capital gains tax**—if their total taxable income falls under $94,050.

This creates a powerful **tax arbitrage**: Retirees can sell appreciated assets in low-income years and avoid taxes entirely.

## Capital Gains vs. Ordinary Income: The Retirement Tax Advantage

Most retirement income sources are taxed as ordinary income:

- Traditional IRA/401(k) withdrawals
- Pension payments
- Social Security (partially)
- Short-term capital gains

But **long-term capital gains** are taxed more favorably. Here’s a comparison using a $50,000 gain:

| Tax Type | Marginal Rate (Single, $60k income) | Tax on $50k Gain | After-Tax Amount |
|--------|-----------------------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Ordinary Income | 22% | $11,000 | $39,000 |
| Long-Term Capital Gains | 15% | $7,500 | $42,500 |

**Savings: $3,500 per $50k gain**

For retirees with large taxable brokerage accounts, shifting withdrawals from traditional accounts to capital gains can save tens of thousands in taxes over time.

## Asset Location: Where to Hold Investments for Tax Efficiency

**Asset location**—placing the right investments in the right account type—is as important as asset allocation.

| Investment Type | Best Account Type | Why |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| Bonds / High-Yield Dividends | Tax-deferred (IRA, 401k) | Interest taxed as ordinary income |
| High-Growth Stocks (e.g., VOO, VTI) | Taxable Brokerage | Qualify for long-term capital gains |
| Dividend Growers (e.g., JNJ, KO) | Taxable (qualified dividends at 0/15/20%) | Dividends taxed at capital gains rates |
| REITs | Roth IRA | Dividends are ordinary income, so tax-free growth in Roth |

**Real-World Example**:

- **Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)**: 3.4% dividend yield, most dividends are **qualified**, taxed at 0/15/20%.
- **Kinder Morgan (KMI)**: 6% yield, but mostly **non-qualified**, taxed at ordinary rates.

Placing JNJ in a taxable account and KMI in an IRA avoids unnecessary tax drag.

## Capital Gains in Practice: A Retirement Withdrawal Strategy

Let’s build a real-world retirement plan using capital gains.

### Scenario:
- Retiree: 65, married, no earned income
- Portfolio:
  - $500,000 in Traditional IRA
  - $400,000 in Taxable Brokerage (bought VTI 15 years ago at $50/share, now $250/share)
  - $100,000 in Roth IRA
- Annual spending: $60,000

### Strategy: Step-Wise Withdrawal

1. **Withdraw $40,000 from taxable account via capital gains**
   - Sell 160 shares of VTI at $250 = $40,000
   - Cost basis: 160 × $50 = $8,000
   - Capital gain: $32,000
   - Taxable income: $32,000 gain + $6,400 in VTI dividends (1.6% yield) = $38,400
   - **Total taxable income: $38,400**
   - Since this is below $94,050 (MFJ threshold), **0% long-term capital gains tax**

2. **Withdraw $20,000 from Traditional IRA**
   - Taxed at ordinary rates
   - Combined income: $38,400 + $20,000 = $58,400
   - 2024 standard deduction (MFJ): $29,200
   - **Taxable income: $29,200**
   - Federal tax due: ~$3,000 (12% bracket)

3. **Roth IRA untouched** – grows tax-free

### Result:
- Withdrawn: $60,000
- **Capital gains tax: $0**
- Federal income tax: ~$3,000
- **Effective tax rate: 5%**

Without this strategy—pulling all $60k from the IRA—the retiree would owe **~$6,700** in taxes (11.2% effective rate).

By using capital gains strategically, they **save $3,700 per year**—$111,000 over 30 years.

## The 0% Capital Gains Bracket: A Hidden Retirement Tool

The **0% long-term capital gains bracket** is one of the most underutilized tools in retirement planning.

### How to Use It:

1. **Calculate your taxable income floor**:
   - Standard deduction: $29,200 (MFJ, 2024)
   - Add any unavoidable income (e.g., Social Security, pensions)

2. **Determine how much capital gains you can realize tax-free**:
   - If your non-gain income is $20,000, you can realize **$74,050** in long-term gains (up to $94,050 total) at **0% tax**.

**Example**:
- Social Security: $20,000 (fully taxable)
- Max long-term gain at 0%: $94,050 – $20,000 = **$74,050**

You can sell $74,050 of appreciated stock—no capital gains tax.

This is equivalent to a **free withdrawal** from your portfolio.

## Timing Matters: When to Sell for Capital Gains

Selling decisions should align with your tax bracket. Consider this:

| Year | Taxable Income (Before Sale) | Max 0% Gain Room | Action |
|------|-------------------------------|------------------|--------|
| 2024 | $30,000 | $64,050 | Sell $60,000 of VTI |
| 2025 | $80,000 | $14,050 | Sell $10,000 |
| 2026 | $100,000 | $0 | Delay sales |

**Advanced tactic**: “**Tax gain harvesting**”—selling and immediately repurchasing to reset cost basis.

**Example**:
- You own 100 shares of **Apple (AAPL)** at $100/share ($10,000 basis), now worth $200/share.
- You sell → $10,000 gain.
- If in 0% bracket, **$0 tax**.
- Immediately buy back 100 shares at $200.

Now your cost basis is $20,000. Future gains start from there. If you die, heirs get a **step-up in basis**—$20,000 gain erased.

This strategy locks in tax-free gains and reduces future tax liability.

## Capital Gains and Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

After age 73 (as of 2024), you must take **RMDs** from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s.

RMDs are **ordinary income**, which can push you into a higher capital gains bracket.

### Problem:
- RMDs increase taxable income.
- May push you from 0% to 15% capital gains rate.

### Solution:
- **Roth conversions** before RMDs start.
- Convert pre-tax IRA funds to Roth IRA in low-income years (e.g., ages 65–72).
- Pay tax at a low rate now to avoid higher taxes later.

**Example**:
- $500,000 Traditional IRA at age 68
- Annual spending: $50,000 (covered by Social Security and taxable account)
- Convert $50,000/year to Roth from 68–72
  - Taxable income: $50,000 (conversion) + $20,000 SS = $70,000
  - Taxed at 12% or 22%
- By 73, IRA reduced to $250,000 → smaller RMDs
- More room to use 0% capital gains bracket

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Q1: Are capital gains counted as income for retirement?

**Yes**, capital gains are included in **taxable income**, but only **long-term gains** qualify for the 0/15/20% rates. Short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income.

### Q2: How much capital gains can I make tax-free in retirement?

It depends on your filing status:
- **Married filing jointly**: Up to **$94,050** in total taxable income (including gains) to qualify for 0% rate (2024).
- **Single**: Up to **$47,025**.

You can realize gains up to that limit with no federal tax.

### Q3: Do I pay capital gains tax if I don’t sell?

**No**. Capital gains are **unrealized** until you sell. You can hold appreciated stock indefinitely without tax. This is known as the “**buy, hold, and die**” strategy—your heirs get a step-up in basis.

### Q4: Are dividends the same as capital gains?

**No**. Dividends are **income**, paid regularly. Capital gains are **price appreciation** when you sell.

But **qualified dividends** (e.g., from JNJ, KO, VOO, SCHD) are taxed at the same 0/15/20% rates as long-term capital gains.

### Q5: What happens if I sell stock and reinvest the money?

Selling triggers capital gains tax based on your holding period and income. **Reinvesting doesn’t avoid the tax**. However, if done in a Roth IRA or during a 0% capital gains year, you can reinvest tax-free.

### Q6: Can I avoid capital gains tax by moving to another state?

Some states tax capital gains, others don’t:
- **No state capital gains tax**: FL, TX, WA, NV, SD, WY, NH (dividends taxed), TN (eliminated 2021)
- **High tax states**: CA (13.3%), NY (8.8%), OR (9.9%)

Moving to a no-tax state can save thousands, especially with large gains.
