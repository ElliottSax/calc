## Understanding Expense Ratios

Expense ratios measure annual fund costs as percentage of assets. Lower ratios compound dramatically over decades.

Formula: Annual Cost = AUM × Expense Ratio

## Individual Preferred Stocks vs. ETF

**$100,000 allocation comparison:**

ETF approach (PFF):
- Annual expense: $450 (0.45%)
- Diversification: 400+ securities
- Trading costs: Minimal (buy once, hold)
- Tax efficiency: High
- Management: Passive

Individual preferred stocks approach:
- Annual expense: $0
- Diversification: 10 positions typical
- Trading costs: $100-200 annually (buying/selling)
- Tax efficiency: Lower (frequent rebalancing)
- Management: Significant (research, monitoring)

Net cost after 20 years:
- PFF: $450 × 20 = $9,000 in expenses
- Individual approach: $150 × 20 + Tax inefficiency ($5,000) = $8,000

Negligible difference, but PFF provides vastly better diversification.

## Dividend Yield vs. Expense Ratio

High-yield funds must overcome higher expenses:

- Gross return: 6.5%
- After expense ratio: 5.3%

**Low-cost dividend ETF (3.2% yield, 0.05% expense ratio):**

High-yield fund provides 2.15% more yield after expenses, but at what risk?

Often high-yield comes from credit risk, not superior management.

## Expense Ratio Savings with Scale

As portfolio grows, absolute dollar expense grows but percentage becomes less burdensome:

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Is 0.45% expense ratio high for preferred stocks?**
A: No, very low. Most mutual funds charge 0.6-1.5%; CEFs charge 0.8-1.5%.

**Q2: Should I avoid funds with 1%+ expense ratios?**
A: Usually yes, unless fund manager demonstrates consistent 1%+ outperformance (rare).

**Q3: Does expense ratio matter with 6% yields?**
A: Yes significantly. 0.5% ratio on 6% yield is 8.3% of returns—not trivial.

**Q4: Can I build preferred portfolio for 0% expense?**
A: Yes, with individual securities, but lose diversification benefit. Stick with 0.4-0.5% ETF for most investors.

**Q5: How much does expense ratio outweigh stock picking ability?**
A: For most managers, consistently beating index by >1% annually is nearly impossible (requires true skill + luck).
