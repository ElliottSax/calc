# Tech Worker Dividend Strategy: RSUs, Options, and Income Investing

Tech workers sit on a paradox: they earn among the highest total compensation packages in any industry but often have the least diversified investment portfolios. A senior software engineer at a FAANG company might earn $350,000+ in total compensation, with $150,000-$200,000 in RSUs -- all in a single stock. When that stock drops 40% (as Meta did in 2022 or Google did during various corrections), their net worth drops with it.

Dividend investing solves the concentration problem while creating a growing income stream. By systematically converting RSU vests, option exercises, and ESPP proceeds into a diversified dividend portfolio, tech workers build a reliable income floor that does not depend on any single company's stock price -- or on the tech industry at all.

## The Tech Compensation Landscape

### Typical Total Compensation Packages (2025)

**Mid-Level Software Engineer (L4/E4, 3-5 years):**
- Base salary: $160,000-$200,000
- RSUs (annual vesting): $50,000-$100,000
- Annual bonus: $15,000-$30,000
- ESPP: Up to $25,000 (at 15% discount)
- **Total: $250,000-$355,000**

**Senior Software Engineer (L5/E5, 5-8 years):**
- Base salary: $200,000-$250,000
- RSUs (annual vesting): $80,000-$200,000
- Annual bonus: $30,000-$60,000
- ESPP: Up to $25,000
- **Total: $335,000-$535,000**

**Staff/Principal Engineer (L6+/E6+):**
- Base salary: $250,000-$350,000
- RSUs (annual vesting): $150,000-$500,000+
- Annual bonus: $50,000-$150,000
- **Total: $450,000-$1,000,000+**

### The Concentration Problem

A senior engineer at Google with $200,000/year in RSU vests who has been at the company for 5 years may hold $500,000-$1,000,000 in a single stock. If Google drops 30% (as it did from 2021-2022 highs), that is $150,000-$300,000 evaporated from a single position.

Rule of thumb: never hold more than 10% of your net worth in your employer's stock. For a tech worker with $1 million net worth, that means a maximum of $100,000 in employer stock at any time.

## The RSU-to-Dividend Pipeline

### How RSUs Work

Restricted Stock Units vest on a schedule (typically quarterly or annually over 4 years). Upon vesting:
- The vested shares become yours
- The value is taxed as ordinary income (federal + state + FICA)
- Your employer automatically sells a portion to cover tax withholding (typically 22-37%)
- You receive the remaining shares in your brokerage account

### The Automatic Diversification Strategy

Implement this rule: sell 100% of RSUs at vesting and immediately redeploy into your dividend portfolio.

**Why sell immediately?**
1. You are already over-exposed to your employer (your salary depends on them)
2. Holding creates additional tax complexity (tracking cost basis by lot)
3. Historically, concentrated positions underperform diversified portfolios
4. You convert non-income-producing growth stock into income-producing dividend stocks

**RSU Vest to Dividend Conversion Example:**

$50,000 quarterly RSU vest:
- Gross value at vest: $50,000
- Tax withholding (sold automatically): ~$17,500 (35% effective)
- Net proceeds: $32,500
- Deployed to dividend portfolio: $32,500
- Annual dividend income generated: $1,138 (at 3.5% yield)

**Annual RSU conversion ($200,000/year in RSU vests):**
- Net proceeds after tax: ~$130,000
- Annual dividend income added: $4,550
- After 5 years of RSU conversion: $650,000+ portfolio, $22,750/year dividends

### Stock Options Strategy

**Incentive Stock Options (ISOs):**
- Exercise when the spread (current price - strike price) is manageable for AMT purposes
- Hold for 1 year after exercise and 2 years after grant for qualifying disposition (long-term capital gains)
- Sell and convert to dividend stocks after the holding period

**Non-Qualified Stock Options (NQSOs):**
- Exercise and sell immediately (spread taxed as ordinary income)
- Deploy net proceeds into dividend portfolio
- No holding period advantage, so immediate conversion is optimal

### ESPP: The Guaranteed Return

If your employer offers a 15% ESPP discount:
1. Max out ESPP contributions ($25,000/year)
2. Buy stock at 15% discount
3. Sell immediately for the guaranteed ~15% return
4. Deploy proceeds into dividend portfolio

Even after short-term capital gains tax, the ESPP provides a ~10% return in 6 months. This is effectively risk-free funding for your dividend portfolio.

This allocation ensures that a tech sector downturn (which could also threaten your job) does not simultaneously devastate your dividend income.

## Tax Optimization for Tech Workers

### The RSU Tax Problem

RSU vesting creates large, lumpy ordinary income events:
- Q1 vest: $50,000 → pushes into 35% bracket
- Q2 vest: $50,000 → stays in 35% bracket
- Q3 vest: $50,000 → may push into 37% bracket
- Q4 vest: $50,000 → in 37% bracket

Total RSU income: $200,000, much of it taxed at 35-37%.

### Tax Mitigation Strategies

**Max pre-tax 401(k):** $23,500 reduces taxable income

**Mega backdoor Roth:** If your employer plan allows after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversions, contribute up to $69,000 total (employee + employer) to 401(k). The after-tax portion converts to Roth for tax-free growth.

**Tax-loss harvesting:** Sell losing positions in your dividend portfolio to offset RSU income. A $20,000 harvested loss saves $7,000-$7,400 in taxes at the 35-37% bracket.

**Charitable giving with appreciated stock:** Donate RSU shares (held 1+ year post-vest) to a donor-advised fund. You receive a deduction at full market value and avoid capital gains tax on the appreciation since vesting.

**State tax optimization:** Tech workers in California (13.3% state tax) or New York (10.9%) face combined marginal rates of 50%+. Consider whether relocating to a no-income-tax state (Texas, Washington, Florida, Nevada) makes financial sense. On $400,000 income, moving from California to Washington saves ~$35,000/year in state taxes alone.

### Asset Location for Tech Workers

- **Roth IRA/401(k)**: Hold highest-growth dividend stocks (tax-free compounding)
- **Traditional 401(k)**: Hold REITs, bonds, and covered call ETFs (defer ordinary income)
- **Taxable brokerage**: Hold qualified dividend stocks (0-20% preferential rates)
- **HSA**: Hold aggressive growth for maximum tax-free compounding

## Layoff-Proofing with Dividends

### The Tech Layoff Reality

Between 2022-2024, the tech industry laid off over 400,000 workers. Even "safe" positions at profitable companies are not immune. Dividend income provides:

- **Immediate income floor**: $2,000-$5,000/month in dividends continues regardless of employment
- **Extended runway**: 12-24 months of expenses covered without touching principal
- **Negotiating power**: No desperate need to accept the first offer at a lower salary

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I hold any of my employer's RSUs?

In most cases, no. You are already exposed to your employer's success through your salary, bonus, and future RSU grants. Adding stock ownership on top creates dangerous concentration. The exception: if you have strong insider conviction about an upcoming catalyst AND the position represents less than 10% of your net worth, a modest hold is reasonable. Otherwise, sell at vesting.

### My RSU vest schedule has a one-year cliff. Should I invest my base salary while waiting?

Absolutely. Invest your base salary savings into your dividend portfolio from day one. The RSU cliff vest (often 25% of the grant after year one) provides a significant lump sum to deploy when it arrives. Do not wait for RSUs to start investing.

### How do I handle the emotional loss of selling RSUs that later appreciate?

This is the most common mistake tech workers make -- holding RSUs because "what if it goes up more?" Reframe the decision: you are not selling a stock, you are converting a concentrated risk into a diversified income stream. If your employer's stock doubles after you sell, your diversified dividend portfolio has also likely appreciated, just with less volatility and actual income payments.

### I am at a startup with stock options, not RSUs. How does this change the strategy?

Startup options are less predictable. If your startup IPOs or is acquired, exercise options strategically (considering AMT for ISOs) and deploy proceeds into your dividend portfolio immediately. Before a liquidity event, focus on investing your base salary into dividends. Do not count on startup equity in your financial plan -- treat any eventual payout as a windfall bonus.

### What about investing in tech dividend stocks since I understand the industry?

Limit tech dividend stocks to 5-10% of your portfolio, and only in companies you do not work for. If you work at Microsoft, do not also hold Microsoft in your dividend portfolio. Your professional knowledge gives you an edge in evaluating tech companies, but this edge is offset by the correlation risk between your job and your investments.
