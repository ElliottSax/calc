# The Ultimate Guide to Stock Valuation for Beginners

Stock valuation separates informed investors from speculators. While headlines trumpet stock prices, intelligent investors understand the mathematical relationships revealing when stocks offer genuine value. This beginner-friendly guide introduces fundamental valuation concepts using real companies and straightforward calculations.

## Why Stock Valuation Matters

Every stock has two prices: the price you pay and the value you're receiving. Buying undervalued stocks builds wealth; overpaying destroys it.

**Real Example:**

Stock trades at $100 per share. Two investors:
- Investor A: Calculates intrinsic value at $150, buys at $100 (33% discount)
- Investor B: Sees uptrend, buys at $100 (no analysis)

If stock eventually reaches fair value of $150, both gain $50. However, if market corrects to $80:
- Investor A: Still undervalued at $80, continues holding (gains expected later)
- Investor B: Panics, sells at $80 (locked in 20% loss)

Valuation prevents emotional decisions.

## Earnings per Share (EPS): Foundation of Valuation

EPS reveals company profitability on per-share basis.

**EPS Calculation:**

EPS = Net Income / Total Shares Outstanding

### Real Example: Apple (AAPL)

Trailing twelve-month net income: $95 billion
Shares outstanding: 16.2 billion
EPS = $95 billion / 16.2 billion = $5.86

Each share receives $5.86 in annual profits.

## Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E): Valuation Baseline

P/E divides stock price by earnings per share, revealing how much you pay for each dollar of earnings.

**P/E Ratio Calculation:**

P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share

### Real Examples (2026)

Coca-Cola (KO): $65 stock / $2.45 EPS = 26.5x P/E
Johnson & Johnson (JNJ): $155 stock / $6.82 EPS = 22.7x P/E
Procter & Gamble (PG): $168 stock / $5.55 EPS = 30.3x P/E

What does this mean?
- KO investors pay $26.50 for each dollar of annual earnings
- JNJ investors pay $22.70 for each dollar of earnings
- PG investors pay $30.30 for each dollar of earnings

If all companies had equal growth prospects, JNJ would offer best value at lowest P/E.

## Interpreting P/E Ratios

P/E ratios vary by sector. Technology typically commands higher multiples than utilities.

| Sector | Average P/E | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Technology | 25-35x | Growth premium justified |
| Healthcare | 18-25x | Moderate growth |
| Consumer Staples | 20-30x | Stable businesses |
| Utilities | 15-20x | Stable, mature |
| Financial | 12-18x | Lower growth |

A 30x P/E in consumer staples signals expensive valuation (above average).
A 30x P/E in technology signals reasonable valuation (below average for sector).

## Forward P/E vs. Trailing P/E

Trailing P/E uses actual historical earnings. Forward P/E uses analyst estimates for future earnings.

### Real Comparison: Johnson & Johnson

Trailing P/E (based on 2025 actual earnings of $6.82): 22.7x
Forward P/E (based on 2026 estimated earnings of $7.70): 20.1x

Forward P/E is lower because analysts expect earnings growth. This provides perspective on whether current valuations reflect growth expectations or not.

**Caution:** Forward estimates often prove optimistic. Conservative investors weight trailing P/E more heavily.

## Earnings Yield: Reverse P/E Perspective

Earnings yield (inverse of P/E) reveals the return stocks generate on invested capital.

**Earnings Yield Formula:**

Earnings Yield = EPS / Stock Price (same as 1 / P/E)

### Example: Apple (AAPL)

EPS: $5.86
Stock price: $185
Earnings yield: $5.86 / $185 = 3.17%

At current price, Apple stocks generate 3.17% earnings yield. Compare to:
- Corporate bonds: 4.5% yield
- Treasury 10-year: 4.25% yield
- S&P 500 average: 3.8% yield

At 3.17% yield, Apple trades below S&P 500 average, suggesting it's slightly expensive.

## Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B): Asset-Based Valuation

P/B divides stock price by book value per share (total assets minus liabilities, divided by shares).

**Formula:**

P/B = Stock Price / Book Value Per Share

### Real Example: Johnson & Johnson

Stock price: $155
Book value per share: $22.50
P/B ratio: $155 / $22.50 = 6.9x

JNJ trades at 6.9 times book value. Is this expensive?

For pharmaceutical companies with valuable patents and brand recognition, 6.9x is reasonable (intangible assets exceed tangible assets). For a bank with mostly tangible assets, 6.9x would signal overvaluation.

## Price-to-Sales Ratio (P/S): Revenue-Based Valuation

P/S avoids earnings accounting tricks by basing valuation on pure revenue.

**Formula:**

P/S = Market Capitalization / Total Revenue

### Real Example: Coca-Cola (KO)

Market cap: $280 billion
Annual revenue: $44 billion
P/S: $280 / $44 = 6.4x

KO trades at 6.4x sales. Reasonable? Compare to industry:
- Average consumer staple P/S: 2.5-4.0x
- KO P/S of 6.4x: Above average (expensive)

High P/S suggests market expects strong future profit growth, or KO is overvalued.

## Dividend Yield: Income Valuation

Dividend yield reveals income generated relative to investment.

**Formula:**

Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend / Stock Price

### Example: Procter & Gamble (PG)

Annual dividend: $3.80
Stock price: $168
Dividend yield: $3.80 / $168 = 2.26%

$10,000 invested in PG generates $226 annually in dividends.

**Interpreting Yields:**

2-3% yield: Typical for quality dividend stocks
1-2% yield: Low dividend focus, growth emphasis
3-4% yield: High yield, potentially attractive
Above 4% yield: Investigate for sustainability

## Price-to-Earnings Growth Ratio (PEG)

PEG adjusts P/E for growth rates, comparing value across different growth profiles.

**Formula:**

PEG = P/E Ratio / Expected Earnings Growth Rate

### Real Example

Stock A: P/E = 20x, Expected growth = 10%
Stock B: P/E = 30x, Expected growth = 25%

PEG Score A: 20 / 10 = 2.0
PEG Score B: 30 / 25 = 1.2

Despite higher P/E, Stock B offers better value (lower PEG) because its faster growth justifies premium pricing.

PEG of 1.0-2.0 considered fairly valued.
PEG below 1.0 often undervalued.
PEG above 2.0 often expensive relative to growth.

## Free Cash Flow Analysis

FCF reveals cash available for dividends and reinvestment after capital expenses.

**Formula:**

FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures

### Real Example: Apple

Operating cash flow: $110 billion
Capital expenditures: $12 billion
Free cash flow: $98 billion

Shares outstanding: 16.2 billion
FCF per share: $98 billion / 16.2 billion = $6.05

FCF yield: $6.05 / $185 = 3.27%

Apple generates 3.27% annual free cash flow, exceeding dividend yield (0.50%), demonstrating substantial cash generation beyond shareholder distributions.

## Investment Decision Framework

Use valuation metrics to guide decisions.

**Step 1: Screen by P/E**
Target P/E 15-25x (reasonable for most sectors)

**Step 2: Check Dividend Yield**
Target 2-4% for dividend investors

**Step 3: Verify Growth**
Confirm dividend or earnings growth justifies valuation

**Step 4: Check FCF**
Ensure cash flow covers dividends (sustainability)

**Step 5: Compare Peers**
Ensure valuation reasonable versus similar companies

## FAQ

**Q: What P/E ratio indicates undervaluation?**
A: Generally below 15x for mature companies, 20x for growth companies. Always compare to peer group—one stock's 20x may be cheap, another's 20x expensive.

**Q: Can I rely solely on P/E ratio?**
A: No. A 10x P/E might indicate great value or a distressed company about to fail. Use multiple metrics for comprehensive picture.

**Q: Is high dividend yield always good?**
A: No. 6-8% yields often signal unsustainable dividends about to be cut. Seek 2-4% yields from companies with growing dividends.

**Q: How often should I revalue stocks?**
A: Quarterly earnings provide new data. Annual reviews work for passive investors. Don't obsess over daily price movements.

**Q: Should I buy low-P/E stocks?**
A: Yes, if businesses remain healthy. Avoid value traps (cheap stocks about to decline further). Verify growth and cash flow justify low valuation.

By analyzing multiple metrics systematically, beginner investors develop ability to identify genuine value—underpriced stocks with strong fundamentals, growing dividends, and healthy cash flow. This systematic approach transforms stock selection from guesswork to mathematical discipline, building portfolios positioned for decades of compounding wealth.
