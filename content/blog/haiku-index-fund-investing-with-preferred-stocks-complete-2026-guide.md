## Understanding Preferred Stocks

Preferred stocks occupy a middle ground between bonds and common stocks. They pay fixed dividends before common stock dividends and receive priority in bankruptcy. PFF (iShares Preferred Stock ETF) holds 400+ preferred shares with a 5.8% yield, attracting income-focused investors.

Unlike bonds, preferred stocks offer growth potential if issued-at prices fall. Unlike common stocks, preferred stocks provide regular distributions unaffected by company performance.

## Preferred vs. Common Stock Characteristics

The fundamental differences shape investment decisions:

| Feature | Common Stock | Preferred Stock | Bond |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dividend Priority | Last | Second | First |
| Bankruptcy Recovery | Last | Middle | First |
| Yield | 1.5% | 5.8% | 4.2% |
| Capital Growth | High | Low | None |
| Tax Treatment | Long-term capital gains | Ordinary income | Interest income |
| Volatility | High | Moderate | Low |

Preferred stocks balance income, stability, and some growth potential.

## Preferred Stock Mechanics

Preferred shares have specific features:

**Example: Bank of America Preferred Stock (BAC-PJ)**
- Issued at: $25 per share
- Coupon rate: 5.75%
- Annual distribution: $1.44 per share
- Current price: $23.50 (below par)
- Current yield: 6.13% (5.75 / 23.50)

If price returns to $25, investor gains both income and $1.50 price appreciation per share (6.38% total return).

$100,000 PFF yielding 5.8%:

Despite higher tax rates, PFF provides superior after-tax income.

## Preferred Stock Risk: Callability

Preferred stocks often include call features—the issuer can redeem shares at par value. This limits upside but protects downside:

**Example: BAC-PJ at $23.50 (below par) with 6.13% yield**

Interest rate scenario A: Rates fall, corporate credit spreads narrow
- BAC likely calls the preferred at $25 par
- Investor gains $1.50 ($25 - $23.50)
- Plus $1.44 annual distributions
- Total return: 12.5% (capped)

Interest rate scenario B: Rates rise, spreads widen
- BAC won't call (keeps lower-cost debt)
- Investor collects $1.44 annually
- Price might fall further
- Uncapped downside risk

Call features protect issuers but cap investor upside.

## Duration and Interest Rate Risk

Preferred stocks react to interest rate changes similarly to bonds:

**$100,000 PFF at 5.8% yield**

If interest rates rise 1%:
- New preferred yield: 6.8%
- PFF value falls: 7-9%
- $100,000 investment: Falls to $92,000

If interest rates fall 1%:
- New preferred yield: 4.8%
- PFF value rises: 8-10%
- $100,000 investment: Rises to $107,000

This interest rate sensitivity is significant—prefer holding to maturity or having long time horizons.

## Building Conviction: Preferred Stocks for Income

Preferred stocks make sense for investors:
- Seeking yields above bond rates
- In moderate tax brackets (ordinary income tax manageable)
- With long time horizons (duration risk manageable)
- Willing to hold through market cycles

Preferred stocks don't make sense for:
- Low-income earners (ordinary tax rates at 22%+ bite hard)
- Investors needing near-term funds (call risk and duration risk)
- Those seeking growth (preferred stocks provide limited appreciation)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Are preferred stocks safer than common stocks?**
A: Yes, during bankruptcy. In normal times, preferred stocks fluctuate more than bonds but less than common stocks.

**Q2: Should I use individual preferred stocks or PFF?**
A: For most investors, PFF provides better diversification. Individual preferred stocks require expertise and monitoring.

**Q3: What happens to preferred stocks during market crashes?**
A: They typically fall 10-20% during equity crashes. They're not crash-proof but better than common stocks.

**Q4: Can I use preferred stocks in a retirement account?**
A: Yes. Ordinary income tax rate applies regardless of account type, but use taxable accounts where ordinary rates matter more.

**Q5: Is a 6% preferred stock yield sustainable?**
A: Usually, for investment-grade issuers. Financial institutions use preferred stocks as regulatory capital and maintain distributions carefully.
