# MLP Investing Guide: Master Limited Partnerships for Income

Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs) offer some of the highest yields available in public markets, often ranging from 5% to 9%. These unique investment structures dominate the midstream energy sector, owning the pipelines, storage facilities, and processing plants that transport oil and gas across North America.

For income investors, MLPs provide an attractive combination of high current yield, inflation-linked revenue growth, and tax-advantaged distributions. However, they also bring complexity through K-1 tax forms, UBTI considerations, and unique business risks that require careful analysis. This guide covers everything you need to make informed MLP investment decisions.

## What Is a Master Limited Partnership?

An MLP is a publicly traded limited partnership that combines the tax advantages of a partnership with the liquidity of public stock exchange trading. MLPs have two types of partners:

**General Partner (GP):** Manages the operations and makes business decisions. The GP typically owns a small percentage of the MLP but may receive incentive distribution rights (IDRs) that increase their share of distributions as total distributions grow.

**Limited Partners (LP):** Public investors who buy units (equivalent to shares) on stock exchanges. Limited partners receive quarterly cash distributions and have limited liability, meaning they cannot lose more than their investment.

### Why MLPs Pay High Yields

MLPs do not pay corporate income tax. Instead, income passes through to the individual unit holders, who pay tax on their share of the partnership's income. This pass-through structure eliminates the double taxation that C-corporations face (corporate tax plus shareholder dividend tax), allowing MLPs to distribute more cash to investors.

Additionally, MLPs in the midstream energy sector generate very stable cash flows from long-term contracts with volume commitments. Pipelines operate as toll roads for energy: producers pay fees to transport products through the pipeline regardless of commodity prices. This fee-based model provides predictable revenue that supports high distribution rates.

## The Midstream Business Model

Most MLPs operate in the midstream segment of the energy value chain, positioned between upstream production and downstream refining.

### Gathering and Processing

Gathering systems collect natural gas from individual wells and transport it to processing plants where natural gas liquids (NGLs) are separated. Revenue is typically based on volume processed under long-term contracts.

### Transportation (Pipelines)

Long-haul pipelines transport oil, natural gas, and refined products across hundreds or thousands of miles. Pipeline revenue is primarily fee-based, with shippers paying tariffs per barrel or per unit of gas transported. Rates are often indexed to inflation, providing built-in revenue growth.

### Storage

MLPs operate storage terminals for crude oil, refined products, and natural gas. Storage contracts typically involve fixed monthly fees, providing steady, predictable revenue.

### Distribution Coverage Ratio

The distribution coverage ratio (DCR) measures how well an MLP's distributable cash flow covers its distributions:

**DCR = Distributable Cash Flow / Total Distributions Paid**

A DCR of 1.2x means the MLP generates 20% more cash than it distributes, providing a safety cushion. Industry standards suggest:

- Above 1.2x: Strong coverage
- 1.0x to 1.2x: Adequate but thin
- Below 1.0x: Distribution may be unsustainable

## Tax Complexity of MLPs

### K-1 Tax Forms

MLPs issue Schedule K-1 forms instead of Form 1099-DIV. K-1 forms report each unit holder's share of partnership income, deductions, and credits. They are more complex than 1099s and may not be received until March or April, potentially delaying tax filing.

Each K-1 requires separate entry on your tax return, and holding multiple MLPs means multiple K-1 forms. This administrative burden is the most common complaint among MLP investors.

### Tax-Deferred Distributions

A significant portion of MLP distributions (typically 70-90%) is classified as return of capital rather than taxable income. Return of capital distributions reduce your cost basis in the MLP rather than creating an immediate tax liability.

This tax deferral is a major advantage. You effectively receive current income without paying current taxes on most of it. However, when you eventually sell your MLP units, the lower cost basis results in a larger capital gain.

### Depreciation Recapture

When you sell MLP units, the portion of your gain attributable to accumulated depreciation (which reduced your cost basis) is taxed at ordinary income rates rather than the lower capital gains rate. This depreciation recapture can create a significant tax bill upon sale.

For long-term holders, the tax deferral during the holding period often outweighs the recapture upon sale. But selling MLPs requires careful tax planning.

### Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI)

When MLPs are held in tax-advantaged accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s), the partnership income may generate unrelated business taxable income (UBTI). If UBTI exceeds $1,000 in any tax year, the IRA must file a tax return (Form 990-T) and pay tax on the excess.

This does not make IRA-held MLPs impractical, but it adds complexity. Many investors avoid holding MLPs directly in IRAs and instead use MLP ETFs or mutual funds that do not generate UBTI for the end investor.

## Evaluating MLPs for Investment

### Distribution Growth Track Record

The best MLPs have multi-year records of distribution increases. Look for MLPs with 5 or more consecutive years of distribution growth, which demonstrates management's confidence in cash flow stability and their commitment to returning capital.

### Contract Quality

Evaluate the percentage of revenue under long-term, fee-based contracts. MLPs with 80% or more fee-based revenue are less exposed to commodity price swings. Contracts with minimum volume commitments provide additional security.

### Counterparty Risk

Who is paying the MLP for its services? If a major shipper or producer faces financial distress, the MLP's revenue may be at risk. Diversification across multiple counterparties reduces this risk.

### Growth Project Backlog

Organic growth projects (new pipelines, expansions, processing plant additions) drive future distribution growth. A healthy backlog of projects with attractive returns signals continued growth potential.

### Balance Sheet Strength

MLPs use significant debt to fund capital projects. A debt-to-EBITDA ratio below 4.0x is generally considered prudent. Above 4.5x raises concerns about financial flexibility and distribution safety.

## Top MLP Considerations

### Enterprise Products Partners (EPD)

Enterprise Products Partners is the largest publicly traded MLP with a diversified network of pipelines, processing plants, and storage facilities. It has increased distributions for over 25 consecutive years and maintains strong coverage ratios.

### Energy Transfer (ET)

Energy Transfer operates one of the most extensive pipeline networks in the United States. It offers a high yield with a diversified asset base spanning natural gas, crude oil, and NGL transportation.

### MPLX (MPLX)

A midstream MLP formed by Marathon Petroleum, MPLX benefits from stable gathering, processing, and transportation operations with strong contract coverage.

### Plains All American Pipeline (PAA)

Plains focuses on crude oil transportation and storage with a large domestic footprint. The company has strengthened its balance sheet and coverage ratios in recent years.

## MLP ETFs and Funds

For investors who want MLP exposure without K-1 forms and UBTI concerns:

**Alerian MLP ETF (AMLP)** - The most popular MLP ETF, providing broad MLP exposure. Structured as a C-corporation, so it pays corporate tax but issues 1099 forms instead of K-1s. This simplifies taxes but creates a tax drag on returns.

**Global X MLP ETF (MLPA)** - Similar broad MLP exposure with a C-corp structure.

**First Trust North American Energy Infrastructure Fund (EMLP)** - Includes MLPs and energy infrastructure corporations, offering diversification beyond pure MLPs.

The trade-off with MLP ETFs is that the C-corp structure creates a tax layer that reduces after-tax returns. However, the simplicity of 1099 reporting and the elimination of UBTI concerns make ETFs appropriate for most individual investors.

## Portfolio Integration Strategies

### Optimal Account Placement

**Taxable accounts:** Best for direct MLP unit ownership. The tax-deferred nature of distributions creates a tax advantage in taxable accounts, and K-1 reporting, while complex, is manageable.

**IRA/401(k):** Best for MLP ETFs or mutual funds that do not generate UBTI. Avoid holding direct MLP units above $1,000 in annual UBTI to avoid Form 990-T filing requirements.

### Position Sizing

MLPs should represent no more than 10-15% of a diversified income portfolio. Energy concentration risk, regulatory risk, and the cyclical nature of energy markets warrant limiting exposure despite attractive yields.

### Distribution Reinvestment

Many brokerages offer DRIP for MLPs, which automatically reinvests distributions into additional units. However, each reinvestment creates a new tax lot with a different cost basis, significantly complicating tax reporting upon sale. Consider whether the reinvestment benefit outweighs the administrative complexity.

## Key Takeaways

- MLPs offer yields of 5-9% through pass-through tax structures that eliminate corporate-level taxation
- Most MLPs operate fee-based midstream energy businesses with predictable, contract-backed cash flows
- Distribution coverage ratios above 1.2x indicate sustainable payouts with room for growth
- K-1 tax forms, UBTI concerns in retirement accounts, and depreciation recapture upon sale create complexity that investors must plan for
- MLP ETFs like AMLP provide simplified exposure with 1099 reporting but sacrifice some tax efficiency through their C-corp structure

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are MLPs good for retirement accounts?

Direct MLP units in IRAs can generate UBTI above $1,000, requiring Form 990-T filing. MLP ETFs and mutual funds structured as C-corps avoid this issue and are better suited for retirement accounts. The tax deferral benefit of MLPs is also less valuable in accounts that are already tax-deferred.

### Will MLPs continue to exist as the energy transition progresses?

Midstream infrastructure will remain necessary for decades, even under aggressive energy transition scenarios. Natural gas, in particular, is expected to play a significant role as a transition fuel. However, long-term demand trends for fossil fuels create uncertainty about MLP growth beyond the next 10-15 years.

### How do MLP distributions compare to dividends in a recession?

MLP distributions held up better than many expected during the 2020 pandemic, with most large midstream MLPs maintaining or modestly reducing distributions. The fee-based business model provides insulation from commodity price declines. However, during severe prolonged downturns, volume declines can eventually pressure cash flows and distributions.

### Should I sell my MLPs before year-end for tax purposes?

Selling MLPs triggers depreciation recapture, which is taxed at ordinary income rates. This can create a surprisingly large tax bill. If possible, plan MLP sales in years when your income is lower to minimize the recapture tax impact. Some investors choose to hold MLPs indefinitely and pass them to heirs, who receive a stepped-up basis that eliminates the recapture.

### What happens to my MLP if it converts to a C-corporation?

Several MLPs have converted to C-corporations in recent years, including Kinder Morgan and Oneok. Conversions typically involve a taxable event for unit holders, and the resulting C-corp pays dividends rather than distributions. While conversions eliminate K-1 complexity, they also eliminate the tax-deferred distribution advantage.
