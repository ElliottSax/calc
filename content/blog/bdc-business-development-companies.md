# BDCs Explained: Business Development Companies for High Yield

Business Development Companies (BDCs) are among the highest-yielding publicly traded investments available, with many paying 8-12% annual dividends. These specialty finance companies provide loans and equity investments to middle-market businesses, passing the interest income through to shareholders as dividends.

BDCs give individual investors access to a private credit market that was previously reserved for institutional investors and wealthy individuals. However, their high yields come with real risks including credit losses, interest rate sensitivity, and portfolio quality deterioration. This guide explains how BDCs work, how to evaluate them, and how to incorporate them into an income portfolio responsibly.

## What Is a Business Development Company?

A BDC is a closed-end investment company regulated under the Investment Company Act of 1940. BDCs were created by Congress in 1980 to encourage capital flow to small and mid-sized businesses that have difficulty accessing traditional bank financing.

BDCs function as publicly traded private lenders. They raise capital by selling stock and issuing debt, then lend that capital to businesses too small for the bond market but too large or too risky for traditional bank loans. These middle-market companies typically have annual revenues between $10 million and $1 billion.

### Why BDCs Pay High Dividends

Like REITs, BDCs must distribute at least 90% of their taxable income to shareholders to maintain their tax-advantaged status. This requirement, combined with the high interest rates they charge on loans (typically 8-14%), produces extraordinary dividend yields.

The lending spread is the primary profit driver. A BDC might borrow at 5-6% and lend at 10-12%, capturing a 4-6% net spread. With leverage of 1.0-1.5x (meaning $1.00-1.50 of debt for every $1.00 of equity), total returns on equity can exceed 10-15% before credit losses.

### The Role of BDCs in the Economy

BDCs serve an important economic function by providing capital to businesses that cannot access public debt markets. They fund:

- Growth capital for expanding businesses
- Acquisition financing for private equity sponsors
- Refinancing of existing debt at competitive rates
- Working capital facilities for seasonal businesses
- Mezzanine financing (subordinated debt with equity features)

## Types of BDC Investments

### First Lien Senior Secured Loans

The safest BDC investment type. First lien loans are secured by the borrower's assets and have priority over all other debt in case of default. Recovery rates for first lien loans typically range from 60-80% of principal.

BDCs focused primarily on first lien senior secured loans offer lower yields but better capital preservation. These BDCs are best for conservative income investors.

### Second Lien Loans

Second lien loans are secured but rank behind first lien loans in recovery priority. They offer higher interest rates (1-3% more than first lien) to compensate for lower recovery expectations. Typical recovery rates are 20-50%.

### Unsecured Debt and Mezzanine

Unsecured loans and mezzanine debt carry the highest risk and highest returns. Without collateral backing, recovery in default is typically 0-20%. BDCs with significant exposure to unsecured lending generate higher current yields but face greater credit loss risk.

### Equity Co-Investments

Many BDCs take small equity positions alongside their debt investments. These equity stakes can produce substantial gains if the borrower company grows or is sold, but they also add portfolio risk. Equity co-investments can be a source of special dividends when companies are sold at a profit.

## Key Metrics for BDC Evaluation

### Net Asset Value (NAV) Per Share

NAV per share represents the fair value of the BDC's portfolio after deducting liabilities, divided by shares outstanding. Track NAV trends over time:

- **Stable or growing NAV:** Indicates the portfolio is performing well and credit losses are manageable
- **Declining NAV:** Suggests credit losses or write-downs are eroding the portfolio. This is the most important warning sign for BDC investors

### Net Investment Income (NII)

NII is the BDC's investment income (primarily loan interest) minus operating expenses and interest on the BDC's own borrowings. NII is the most sustainable source of dividend funding.

Compare NII per share to the dividend per share:

- **NII covers the dividend:** Sustainable dividend from core operations
- **NII falls short:** The BDC must rely on realized gains or spillover income to cover the dividend, which is less reliable

### Dividend Coverage Ratio

**Coverage = NII Per Share / Dividend Per Share**

A coverage ratio of 1.0x or above means the dividend is fully covered by net investment income. Ratios consistently below 0.90x indicate the dividend may need to be reduced.

### Price to NAV

Like closed-end funds, BDCs can trade at premiums or discounts to NAV:

- **Below NAV (discount):** Potentially attractive entry point, but investigate why the discount exists
- **At NAV:** Fair value
- **Above NAV (premium):** Potentially overvalued. Be cautious buying BDCs at significant premiums

### Non-Accrual Rate

Loans on non-accrual status are those where the BDC is no longer recording interest income because the borrower is unable to make payments. A non-accrual rate above 3-5% of the portfolio (at fair value) indicates growing credit problems.

### Portfolio Composition

Evaluate the mix of first lien, second lien, unsecured, and equity investments. A portfolio heavily weighted toward first lien senior secured loans (above 70%) offers more capital protection than one with significant second lien or unsecured exposure.

## Leading BDCs for Income Investors

### Ares Capital Corporation (ARCC)

The largest publicly traded BDC with approximately $22 billion in assets. ARCC benefits from the Ares Management platform, one of the largest alternative asset managers globally. Strong underwriting track record with consistent NAV stability and dividend coverage.

### Main Street Capital (MAIN)

Unique among BDCs for its internally managed structure (no external management fees). Main Street targets lower middle-market companies and pays monthly dividends plus regular supplemental dividends. Consistently trades at a premium to NAV due to its strong track record.

### Blue Owl Capital Corporation (OBDC)

A large, externally managed BDC with a portfolio focused on senior secured first lien loans. Benefits from the Blue Owl Capital platform and maintains strong dividend coverage.

### Golub Capital BDC (GBDC)

Focuses on one-stop senior secured loans to sponsor-backed middle-market companies. Conservative underwriting philosophy with strong loss history.

### FS KKR Capital Corp (FSK)

One of the largest BDCs by assets, leveraging KKR's private equity relationships for deal flow. Higher yield reflects a more aggressive investment approach.

## Risks of BDC Investing

### Credit Risk

The primary risk. BDC borrowers are middle-market companies that carry more default risk than investment-grade corporations. During economic downturns, default rates rise, creating credit losses that reduce NAV and potentially force dividend cuts.

During the 2020 pandemic, many BDCs experienced significant NAV declines as portfolio companies were marked down. While most recovered, the experience demonstrated the credit risk embedded in BDC portfolios.

### Interest Rate Sensitivity

Most BDC loans are floating rate, meaning interest income increases when rates rise. This makes BDCs one of the few high-yield investments that benefit from rising rates, unlike bonds and preferred stocks.

However, higher rates also increase stress on borrowers, potentially leading to more defaults. The net effect depends on the magnitude and speed of rate changes.

### Leverage Risk

BDCs use leverage to amplify returns. Current regulations allow BDCs to operate with asset coverage ratios as low as 150% (leverage of 2:1). While most BDCs maintain more conservative leverage, the amplification effect means credit losses hit NAV harder than the actual portfolio percentage suggests.

### External Management Fees

Most BDCs are externally managed, meaning an external investment advisor makes decisions and charges management fees plus incentive fees. These fees typically consume 3-5% of total assets annually.

High fees reduce shareholder returns and can create conflicts of interest. The external manager benefits from growing the portfolio (earning higher management fees) even if growth comes at the expense of credit quality.

Internally managed BDCs like Main Street Capital eliminate this conflict but are rare.

### Regulatory Risk

BDCs are heavily regulated and changes in regulations can affect their operations, leverage allowances, and distribution requirements. Stay informed about regulatory developments affecting the BDC industry.

## Building a BDC Allocation

### Position Sizing

Limit total BDC exposure to 5-10% of an income portfolio. The credit risk and potential for NAV erosion during recessions warrant conservative position sizing.

### Diversification Across BDCs

Own at least 3-5 different BDCs to diversify manager risk and portfolio concentration. Different BDCs focus on different segments of the middle market and employ different underwriting strategies.

### Quality Over Yield

Within the BDC universe, resist the temptation to buy the highest-yielding BDC. The highest yields often indicate the market expects credit losses or dividend cuts. Focus on BDCs with:

- Consistent NAV stability over 5+ years
- NII consistently covering dividends
- Non-accrual rates below 3%
- Portfolios weighted toward first lien secured loans

### Price to NAV Discipline

Buy BDCs at or below NAV when possible. Paying a 10% premium to NAV for a BDC yielding 10% effectively reduces your yield to approximately 9% while adding capital loss risk if the premium erodes.

## BDC Dividends and Taxes

BDC dividends are generally taxed as ordinary income because the underlying income (loan interest) does not qualify for the lower qualified dividend rate. This means BDC dividends face tax rates of 22-37% for most investors in taxable accounts.

This tax treatment makes BDCs particularly well-suited for tax-advantaged accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s) where the ordinary income tax disadvantage does not apply. Unlike MLPs, BDCs do not generate UBTI concerns in retirement accounts, making them a clean fit.

In taxable accounts, the after-tax yield on a BDC paying 10% is approximately 6.3-7.8% depending on your tax bracket. Compare this after-tax yield to alternatives rather than the pre-tax headline yield.

## Key Takeaways

- BDCs provide access to private credit markets through publicly traded securities with yields typically ranging from 8-12%
- Net Asset Value stability is the most important metric for long-term BDC investing because NAV erosion from credit losses destroys capital
- Net investment income coverage of the dividend should be at or above 1.0x for sustainable dividends
- BDC dividends are taxed as ordinary income, making them best suited for tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs
- Limit BDC exposure to 5-10% of an income portfolio and diversify across at least 3-5 different BDCs

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do BDCs compare to REITs for income?

BDCs and REITs both distribute at least 90% of taxable income but invest in entirely different assets (loans vs. real estate). BDCs typically offer higher yields but with more credit risk and NAV volatility. REITs provide real asset backing and inflation protection through real estate appreciation. Both have ordinary income tax treatment. A diversified income portfolio might include both, with REITs providing real asset exposure and BDCs providing private credit exposure.

### Can BDC dividends be cut?

Yes, and BDC dividend cuts are not uncommon. During economic downturns, rising credit losses reduce net investment income, and BDCs may need to reduce dividends. The best protection is owning BDCs with strong coverage ratios, conservative leverage, and portfolios concentrated in senior secured first lien loans.

### Should I buy BDCs before or after the ex-dividend date?

The ex-dividend price adjustment is typically larger for high-yield BDCs than for regular dividend stocks. Buying after the ex-date at a lower price can be attractive if you plan to hold long-term. However, the same caveat applies as with all dividend stocks: the difference averages out over multiple quarters for long-term holders.

### Are internally managed BDCs always better than externally managed?

Internally managed BDCs eliminate the conflict of interest inherent in external management and avoid hefty management and incentive fees. However, the best externally managed BDCs (like Ares Capital) have delivered strong returns despite their fee structures. Evaluate each BDC individually based on total shareholder returns, NAV stability, and dividend consistency rather than dismissing all externally managed BDCs.

### How do rising interest rates affect BDC dividends?

Most BDC loans are floating rate, so rising rates increase interest income from the loan portfolio. This generally supports higher NII and potentially higher dividends in rising rate environments. However, significantly higher rates can stress borrowers, potentially increasing defaults. The net effect for most quality BDCs has been positive during moderate rate increases.
