## What Are Capital Gains?

A **capital gain** occurs when you sell an investment for more than its purchase price. The difference between the **sale price** and the **cost basis** is your gain. If you sell for less, it’s a **capital loss**.

There are two types of capital gains:

- **Short-term capital gains**: For assets held one year or less. These are taxed as ordinary income.
- **Long-term capital gains**: For assets held more than one year. These benefit from preferential tax rates.

Taxes are only triggered upon **realization** — that is, when you sell the asset.

## Step-by-Step: How to Calculate Capital Gains

Calculating capital gains is straightforward. Follow these steps:

### 1. Determine the Sale Price
This is the net amount you receive from selling the asset after commissions or fees.

### 2. Determine the Cost Basis
Your cost basis includes:
- Original purchase price
- Commissions or fees paid at purchase
- Reinvested dividends (if applicable)
- Stock splits or adjustments

### 3. Subtract Cost Basis from Sale Price
- **Capital Gain** = Sale Price – Cost Basis
- If negative, it’s a capital loss.

### 4. Classify as Short-Term or Long-Term
- **Short-term**: Held 1 year or less
- **Long-term**: Held more than 1 year

### 5. Apply the Correct Tax Rate Based on Income

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Long-Term Gain on VOO

**Scenario**:  
In January 2022, you bought 100 shares of **VOO** (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF) at $380 per share. You sell all 100 shares in March 2026 at $520 per share. Your tax filing status is Married Filing Jointly, with $120,000 in other income.

**Calculation**:
- Purchase cost: 100 × $380 = $38,000
- Commissions: $0 (commission-free trade)
- Reinvested dividends: $3,200 (accumulated over 4 years)
- **Adjusted cost basis** = $38,000 + $3,200 = **$41,200**
- Sale proceeds: 100 × $520 = **$52,000**
- **Capital gain** = $52,000 – $41,200 = **$10,800**
- Holding period: Over 4 years → **long-term gain**

**Tax Impact**:
- Your other income: $120,000
- Total income with gain: $130,800
- Long-term capital gains fall into the 15% bracket (between $89,451 and $553,850)
- **Tax due** = $10,800 × 15% = **$1,620**

### Example 2: Short-Term Gain on Individual Stock (KO)

**Scenario**:  
You bought 200 shares of **Coca-Cola (KO)** in July 2025 at $60/share. You sell them in February 2026 at $68/share. You earn $45,000 in wages and file as Single.

**Calculation**:
- Purchase cost: 200 × $60 = $12,000
- Commissions: $0
- **Cost basis** = $12,000
- Sale proceeds: 200 × $68 = $13,600
- **Capital gain** = $13,600 – $12,000 = **$1,600**
- Holding period: 7 months → **short-term gain**

**Tax Impact**:
- Wages: $45,000
- Add short-term gain: $46,600
- Filing as Single → falls into 22% marginal bracket
- **Tax due** = $1,600 × 22% = **$352**

### Example 3: Netting Gains and Losses (SCHD and VTI)

**Scenario**:  
You sell two positions in 2026:
- 50 shares of **SCHD** bought in 2024 at $45/share, sold at $72/share
- 150 shares of **VTI** bought in 2025 at $240/share, sold at $220/share

You file as Married Filing Jointly with $95,000 in salary.

**Calculations**:

**SCHD (Long-Term)**:
- Cost basis: 50 × $45 = $2,250
- Sale: 50 × $72 = $3,600
- Gain: $3,600 – $2,250 = **$1,350**

**VTI (Short-Term)**:
- Cost basis: 150 × $240 = $36,000
- Sale: 150 × $220 = $33,000
- Loss: $33,000 – $36,000 = **–$3,000**

**Net Capital Position**:
- Long-term gain: $1,350
- Short-term loss: $3,000
- Net loss: $1,650 (short-term)

**Tax Impact**:
- You can offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income with capital losses.
- Remaining $1,650 loss carries forward to 2027.
- **No tax due** on capital gains in 2026 due to offset.

## Adjusted Cost Basis: What Counts?

Many investors overlook adjustments to their cost basis. The IRS allows:

| Adjustment Type | Included in Cost Basis? | Example |
|-----------------|-------------------------|-------|
| Purchase commissions | Yes | $10 fee on $5,000 stock purchase → $5,010 basis |
| Reinvested dividends | Yes | $2.50/share annual dividend reinvested over 5 years → increases basis |
| Stock splits | Yes | 2-for-1 split → double shares, halve price per share |
| Return of capital | Reduces basis | $1/share return of capital → basis drops by $1 |

**Example**:  
You buy 100 shares of **JNJ** at $150/share. Over 3 years, you reinvest $8.40/share in dividends. You also receive a $1.20/share return of capital in 2025.

- Original cost: $15,000
- Reinvested dividends: 100 × $8.40 = $840
- Return of capital: 100 × $1.20 = $120 (reduces basis)
- **Adjusted cost basis** = $15,000 + $840 – $120 = **$15,720**

## Tax-Loss Harvesting in 2026

Tax-loss harvesting involves selling underperforming investments to offset gains. In 2026, with potential market uncertainty, this strategy could save thousands.

**Rules**:
- Offset short-term gains with short-term losses first
- Offset long-term gains with long-term losses
- Remaining losses can offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income
- Excess losses carry forward indefinitely

**Example**:  
You have:
- $12,000 long-term gain from VOO
- $8,000 long-term loss from a tech stock
- Net long-term gain: $4,000
- Tax at 15%: $600 (vs. $1,800 without harvesting)

## 2026 Capital Gains Exemptions and Exceptions

### 1. Primary Residence Exclusion
Up to $250,000 ($500,000 if married) of gain on the sale of a primary home is tax-free if:
- You owned and lived in the home for 2 of the last 5 years
- You haven’t used the exclusion in the past 2 years

### 2. Gift and Inheritance Basis
- **Gifts**: Recipient takes donor’s basis (carryover basis)
- **Inherited assets**: Basis is "stepped up" to fair market value at date of death

**Example**:  
Parent bought **KO** at $20/share in 1980. It’s worth $65/share at death. You inherit 100 shares.
- Your basis = $65/share (not $20)
- If you sell at $70, gain is $5/share → $500 total gain

This could save **$2,250** in taxes compared to carryover basis.

## Capital Gains and Retirement Accounts

**Important**: Capital gains in **tax-advantaged accounts** (IRA, 401(k)) are **not taxed** when realized.

- **Traditional IRA/401(k)**: Taxes deferred until withdrawal (ordinary income rates)
- **Roth IRA/401(k)**: Qualified withdrawals are tax-free

**Example**:  
You hold VTI in a Roth IRA. It grows from $50,000 to $120,000 over 10 years. When you withdraw after age 59½, **$70,000 gain is tax-free**.

This makes Roth accounts ideal for high-growth assets.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### **Q: Will capital gains tax rates increase in 2026?**  
A: Yes, likely. The TCJA expires at the end of 2025. Without new law, long-term capital gains rates will revert to pre-2018 levels, and income thresholds will drop. High earners could see rates rise from 20% to 23.8% (including Net Investment Income Tax).

---

### **Q: How do I report capital gains?**  
A: Use **Form 8949** to list each transaction and **Schedule D** to summarize gains/losses. Brokers provide **Form 1099-B** with cost basis and sale details.

---

### **Q: What if I don’t know my cost basis?**  
A: Contact your broker or check old statements. For pre-2011 stocks, IRS allows reasonable estimates. Missing basis can lead to taxation on full sale amount.

---

### **Q: Are dividends part of capital gains?**  
A: No. Dividends are **income**, taxed separately. Qualified dividends (held 60+ days) are taxed at long-term capital gains rates. Ordinary dividends are taxed as income.

---

### **Q: Can I avoid capital gains tax?**  
A: Not entirely, but you can reduce it by:
- Holding assets >1 year
- Using tax-loss harvesting
- Investing in tax-advantaged accounts
- Donating appreciated stock to charity
- Using the primary residence exclusion

---

### **Q: Do I pay capital gains on mutual fund distributions?**  
A: Yes. Even if you reinvest, fund-level sales trigger capital gains distributions, reported on **Form 1099-DIV**. These are taxable in the year received.

---

### **Q: How does the Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) affect 2026 gains?**  
A: If your modified adjusted gross income exceeds $250,000 (MFJ) or $200,000 (Single), you pay an extra **3.8% NIIT** on investment income, including capital gains.

**Example**:  
$100,000 long-term gain, MFJ with $700,000 MAGI → $100,000 × 3.8% = **$3,800** additional tax.
