# Understanding Capital Gains in 2026

Capital gains—the profits you make when selling investments for more than you paid—are a critical component of investment returns. Understanding how capital gains work, how they're taxed, and strategies to minimize tax impact can significantly enhance your investment results. This guide covers everything you need to know about capital gains in 2026.

## What Are Capital Gains?

A capital gain occurs when you sell an investment for more than your cost basis (what you paid for it):

**Capital Gain = Sale Price - Cost Basis**

Example:
- Purchase 100 shares of Apple at $150/share = $15,000 cost basis
- Sell 100 shares of Apple at $180/share = $18,000 proceeds
- Capital gain: $18,000 - $15,000 = $3,000

Capital gains are part of your total return, alongside dividend income:

**Total Return = Dividend Income + Capital Gains**

## Long-Term vs. Short-Term Capital Gains

The tax treatment of capital gains depends on how long you held the investment:

### Long-Term Capital Gains (Held 1+ years)

**2026 Federal Tax Rates (for single filers):**

| Income Bracket | Tax Rate | 2026 Income Range |
|---------------|----------|------------------|
| 0% rate | 0% | $0 - $47,025 |
| 15% rate | 15% | $47,025 - $518,900 |
| 20% rate | 20% | $518,900+ |

**Example: $50,000 capital gain, 15% bracket**
- Federal tax: $50,000 × 0.15 = $7,500
- State tax (varies): $2,500 (5%)
- Total tax: $10,000
- Net profit: $40,000

### Short-Term Capital Gains (Held <1 year)

Short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%:

**2026 Federal Tax Brackets (Single Filers):**

| Tax Rate | Income Range |
|----------|--------------|
| 10% | $0 - $11,600 |
| 12% | $11,600 - $47,150 |
| 22% | $47,150 - $100,525 |
| 24% | $100,525 - $191,950 |
| 32% | $191,950 - $243,725 |
| 35% | $243,725 - $609,350 |
| 37% | $609,350+ |

**Example: $50,000 short-term gain, 24% bracket**
- Federal tax: $50,000 × 0.24 = $12,000
- State tax (varies): $2,500 (5%)
- Total tax: $14,500
- Net profit: $35,500

**Key Difference:** The same $50,000 gain costs $10,000 in taxes (long-term) vs. $14,500 (short-term)—a $4,500 difference.

## Tax-Efficient Capital Gains Strategies

### Strategy 1: Hold Investments Long-Term

Holding investments at least one year reduces tax from ordinary income rates to capital gains rates:

| Investment | Short-Term Gain (24%) | Long-Term Gain (15%) | Tax Savings |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|------------|
| $100,000 | $24,000 tax | $15,000 tax | $9,000 |
| $500,000 | $120,000 tax | $75,000 tax | $45,000 |
| $1,000,000 | $240,000 tax | $150,000 tax | $90,000 |

This demonstrates why holding investments longer than 1 year is financially significant.

### Strategy 2: Tax-Loss Harvesting

Offset capital gains with capital losses:

**Example:**
- SCHD position up $8,000 (want to sell, take profit)
- VYM position down $3,000 (underperforming)
- Sell both positions
- Net capital gain: $8,000 - $3,000 = $5,000
- Tax at 15%: $750
- Without harvesting: $8,000 gain × 15% = $1,200 tax
- Tax savings: $450

Reinvest the sale proceeds in similar (but not "substantially identical") funds to maintain exposure.

### Strategy 3: Charitable Giving

Donate appreciated securities directly to charity:

**Traditional approach:**
- Sell stock with $50,000 gain
- Pay capital gains tax: $7,500
- Donate $42,500 to charity
- Tax deduction: $42,500

**Appreciated stock donation:**
- Donate stock with $50,000 unrealized gain directly
- No capital gains tax: $0
- Tax deduction: $50,000
- Charity receives $50,000 in stock

This strategy saves $7,500 in capital gains tax while maintaining the same charitable deduction.

### Strategy 4: Account Location Strategy

Place assets with high capital gain potential in tax-advantaged accounts:

| Asset | Best Location | Reason |
|-------|--------------|--------|
| Growth stocks | Roth IRA | Tax-free capital gains forever |
| Dividend stocks | Taxable or IRA | Moderate tax on dividends |
| Bonds | Traditional IRA | Avoid ordinary income tax rates |
| REITs | IRA/401k | Avoid 35-37% tax on REIT dividends |
| Options writing | IRA | Tax-free short-term gains |

Placing $100,000 in growth stocks in a Roth IRA:
- 25-year growth to $1,000,000
- Capital gains: $900,000
- Tax: $0 (tax-free Roth growth)

Versus taxable account:
- Same $900,000 gains
- Tax at 15%: $135,000
- **Roth advantage: $135,000**

## Capital Gains From Different Asset Types

Different investments produce different capital gain patterns:

### Growth Stocks
- Typically produce capital gains, minimal dividends
- Example: Microsoft, Nvidia, Tesla

$50,000 investment over 10 years:
- Dividends: $1,000 (2% of investment)
- Capital appreciation: $80,000
- Total return: $81,000
- Gain: $81,000 (88% from capital gains)

### Dividend Growth Stocks
- Produce both dividends and capital gains
- Example: JNJ, PG, KO

$50,000 investment over 10 years:
- Dividends received: $15,000
- Capital appreciation: $25,000
- Total return: $40,000
- Gain: $40,000 (63% dividends, 38% capital gains)

### Index Funds (Low Turnover)
- Minimal capital gains distributions
- Gains come when you sell

$50,000 in VTI over 10 years:
- Distributions: $5,000 (mostly reinvested)
- Capital appreciation: $45,000
- Total return: $50,000
- Your gain: Only realized when you sell

### Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs)
- Primarily dividends, limited capital gains
- Example: O (Realty Income)

$50,000 investment over 10 years:
- Dividends: $22,000
- Capital appreciation: $5,000
- Total return: $27,000
- Gain: $27,000 (81% dividends, 19% capital gains)

## Capital Gains in Portfolio Rebalancing

Rebalancing can trigger capital gains:

**Scenario: 60/40 portfolio ($100,000)**

Initial: $60,000 stocks, $40,000 bonds

After 1 year:
- Stocks +10%: $66,000
- Bonds +3%: $41,200
- Portfolio: $107,200 (62% stocks, 38% bonds)

Rebalancing requires:
- Sell $2,000 of stocks
- Capital gain on stocks: $2,000 (assuming all appreciation)
- Tax at 15%: $300

This small tax cost maintains your desired risk level.

## Medicare Net Investment Income Tax

High-income earners pay an additional 3.8% Medicare tax on net investment income:

**Applies to net investment income when modified adjusted gross income exceeds:**
- $200,000 (single)
- $250,000 (married filing jointly)

**Example: $100,000 long-term capital gain, single filer earning $250,000**

Federal income tax: Wouldn't apply (capital gains below $200k threshold, so no ordinary income tax)
Medicare tax: 3.8% × $100,000 = $3,800
Long-term capital gains tax (15%): $15,000
**Total tax: $18,800**

Without Medicare tax would be $15,000, so this adds 25% to the tax bill.

## Capital Gains Distributions From Mutual Funds and ETFs

Funds distribute capital gains to shareholders annually:

**How it works:**
1. Fund manager sells securities at gains
2. Fund realizes capital gains
3. Fund distributes gains to shareholders
4. You owe taxes on gains even if you don't sell

**Example:**
- Own SCHD ETF with $10,000 position
- Fund distributes $200 in capital gains
- You owe taxes on $200 gain even though you didn't sell
- Tax at 15%: $30

This is why tax-efficient ETFs (low turnover, index-based) often outperform actively managed funds in taxable accounts.

## Wash Sale Rules

The IRS disallows losses if you buy the same or substantially identical security within 30 days:

**Scenario:**
- Buy Apple at $150, value drops to $130
- Sell at $130, realize $20 loss (harvest for taxes)
- Buy Apple again at $128 five days later
- The $20 loss is disallowed (wash sale)
- Cost basis of new purchase: $150 (includes disallowed loss)

To avoid wash sales:
- Sell losing position
- Wait 31 days before buying same security
- Substitute similar (but not identical) fund in the meantime

Example: Sell VYM (losing position), buy SCHD (similar dividend ETF) for 31 days, then switch back.

## FAQs

**Q: What's the difference between realized and unrealized gains?**
A: Realized gains occur when you sell (taxes due). Unrealized gains exist on paper (no taxes yet). Holding appreciated investments indefinitely defers taxes indefinitely.

**Q: Are capital gains ever tax-free?**
A: Yes. Investments held until death receive a "step-up" in basis at death, erasing capital gains. Also, some capital gains are 0% taxed if income is below certain thresholds.

**Q: Should I avoid selling appreciated positions to avoid capital gains tax?**
A: Not necessarily. If the investment is underperforming or misaligned with goals, the tax cost shouldn't prevent the sale. But do consider harvesting losses and timing sales.

**Q: How do I report capital gains to the IRS?**
A: Form 1040 Schedule D (Capital Gains and Losses). Your broker provides Form 1099-B with transaction details.

**Q: Can I carry forward unused capital losses?**
A: Yes. If losses exceed gains, you can deduct $3,000 against ordinary income. Excess carries forward indefinitely.

**Q: What if I inherited investments with gains?**
A: Inherited assets receive a "step-up" in basis to fair market value at date of death. You inherit with zero capital gains.

**Q: Does cryptocurrency have different capital gains treatment?**
A: No. Crypto is treated as property. Gains are taxed as long-term or short-term depending on holding period.

**Q: What's the difference between capital gains and investment income?**
A: Capital gains are profits from selling. Investment income includes dividends and interest. They're taxed differently and reported separately.
