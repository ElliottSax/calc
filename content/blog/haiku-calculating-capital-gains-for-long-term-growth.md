# Calculating Capital Gains for Long-term Growth

Capital gains represent the profit you earn when you sell an investment for more than you paid for it. Understanding how to calculate capital gains accurately directly impacts your long-term wealth accumulation and tax planning strategy. This comprehensive guide walks you through the mechanics of capital gains calculations and demonstrates how they compound over decades of investing.

## Understanding Capital Gains Fundamentals

A capital gain occurs when the selling price of an investment exceeds its original purchase price, commonly called the cost basis. Long-term capital gains receive favorable tax treatment in the United States—taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your tax bracket, versus ordinary income tax rates that can reach 37%. This distinction makes calculating capital gains essential for tax-efficient investing.

The basic formula for calculating a capital gain is straightforward:

**Capital Gain = Selling Price − Cost Basis**

However, real-world scenarios involve multiple purchases at different prices, dividend reinvestment, and various cost basis accounting methods. Let's explore each component in detail.

## Cost Basis Determination Methods

Your cost basis represents the original purchase price plus any commissions or fees paid to acquire the investment. For stocks purchased over time or through dividend reinvestment, determining your cost basis requires choosing an accounting method.

**First-In, First-Out (FIFO)**
With FIFO, you assume the oldest shares sell first. If you purchased 100 shares of Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI) at $180 per share in 2020, then 100 additional shares at $220 in 2022, and sold 120 shares at $245 in 2024, your calculation would be:

- First 100 shares cost basis: 100 × $180 = $18,000
- Next 20 shares cost basis: 20 × $220 = $4,400
- Total cost basis: $22,400
- Sales proceeds: 120 × $245 = $29,400
- Capital gain: $29,400 − $22,400 = $7,000

**Specific Identification**
This method allows you to specify which shares you're selling, enabling tax optimization. Selling the highest-cost shares first minimizes your taxable gain. Using the same scenario but identifying the 120 highest-cost shares:

- 100 shares at $220 = $22,000
- 20 shares at $180 = $3,600
- Total cost basis: $25,600
- Sales proceeds: $29,400
- Capital gain: $29,400 − $25,600 = $3,800

By choosing specific identification, you reduced your taxable gain by $3,200, potentially saving $480-$640 in federal taxes alone.

## Long-term Capital Gains Tax Rates

The IRS distinguishes between long-term gains (held over one year) and short-term gains (held one year or less). Short-term gains face ordinary income tax rates. Long-term rates for 2026 are:

| Tax Bracket | Long-term Rate | Qualifying Income Range |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Single filer, under $47,025 | 0% | Up to $47,025 |
| Single filer, $47,025-$518,900 | 15% | $47,025 to $518,900 |
| Single filer, over $518,900 | 20% | Over $518,900 |
| Married filing jointly, under $94,050 | 0% | Up to $94,050 |
| Married filing jointly, $94,050-$583,750 | 15% | $94,050 to $583,750 |
| Married filing jointly, over $583,750 | 20% | Over $583,750 |

## Real-World Calculation Example: Dividend Aristocrat Portfolio

Consider an investor who built a diversified dividend portfolio over 12 years. She owns shares in Johnson & Johnson (JNJ), Coca-Cola (KO), and Procter & Gamble (PG)—three Dividend Aristocrats with consistent dividend growth.

**Initial Portfolio (January 2014):**
- JNJ: 50 shares @ $87.50 = $4,375
- KO: 75 shares @ $41.00 = $3,075
- PG: 60 shares @ $64.50 = $3,870
- Total investment: $11,320

**After Reinvesting Dividends for 12 Years (January 2026):**
Through consistent reinvestment, the portfolio grew to:
- JNJ: 73 shares @ $165.40 = $12,074
- KO: 112 shares @ $62.50 = $7,000
- PG: 89 shares @ $168.30 = $14,978
- Total portfolio value: $34,052

**Calculating Capital Gains:**

For JNJ:
- Cost basis: $4,375 (original) + $2,800 (dividends reinvested) = $7,175
- Current value: $12,074
- Capital gain: $12,074 − $7,175 = $4,899

For KO:
- Cost basis: $3,075 (original) + $1,625 (dividends reinvested) = $4,700
- Current value: $7,000
- Capital gain: $7,000 − $4,700 = $2,300

For PG:
- Cost basis: $3,870 (original) + $2,950 (dividends reinvested) = $6,820
- Current value: $14,978
- Capital gain: $14,978 − $6,820 = $8,158

**Portfolio totals:**
- Total cost basis: $18,695
- Total current value: $34,052
- Total capital gain: $15,357
- Gain percentage: 82.1%

## Tax Impact on Long-term Returns

If this investor is in the 15% long-term capital gains bracket and sells the entire position:

- Federal capital gains tax: $15,357 × 0.15 = $2,304
- Net proceeds after tax: $34,052 − $2,304 = $31,748
- After-tax return: 70.1%

Contrast this with short-term holding. If she had held these positions less than one year and paid 32% in ordinary income tax:

- Short-term tax: $15,357 × 0.32 = $4,914
- After-tax proceeds: $34,052 − $4,914 = $29,138
- After-tax return: 57.4%

The tax efficiency advantage of long-term gains amounts to $2,610 in this scenario.

## Strategic Realization of Capital Gains

Sophisticated investors use tax-loss harvesting to offset capital gains. If your portfolio also included a losing position of $3,000, you could sell it to realize a loss that offsets $3,000 of your $15,357 gain, leaving only $12,357 taxable.

Tax liability would drop from $2,304 to $1,854, saving $450 in federal taxes—pure benefit from strategic planning.

## Wash Sale Rules and Capital Loss Strategy

The IRS wash-sale rule prevents claiming a loss if you buy the same security within 30 days before or after selling it at a loss. However, you can harvest tax losses strategically by switching to similar but not identical ETFs.

For example, if VXUS (Vanguard International Stock ETF) declined and you wanted to realize the loss:
1. Sell VXUS at a loss
2. Wait 30 days
3. Repurchase VXUS or similar international fund

This locks in your tax loss while maintaining exposure to the international market. The benefit: offset capital gains dollar-for-dollar, with excess losses carryable forward indefinitely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: When do I pay capital gains tax?**
A: You pay tax in the year you sell the investment and realize the gain. If you hold an investment that grows in value, you owe no tax until you sell.

**Q: Can I avoid capital gains tax by donating appreciated shares to charity?**
A: Yes. Donating appreciated securities to qualified charities eliminates the capital gains tax while allowing you to deduct the full fair market value as a charitable contribution.

**Q: What's the difference between adjusted cost basis and original cost basis?**
A: Original cost basis is what you paid. Adjusted cost basis includes adjustments for corporate actions like stock splits, spinoffs, or reinvested dividends.

**Q: How do I track cost basis for dividend reinvestment?**
A: Most brokers automatically track this. For manual tracking, record each dividend reinvestment as a separate "purchase" at the reinvestment price on the dividend payment date.

**Q: Can I carry capital losses back to previous years?**
A: No. Capital losses can offset current-year gains plus $3,000 of ordinary income, with remaining losses carried forward indefinitely to future years.
