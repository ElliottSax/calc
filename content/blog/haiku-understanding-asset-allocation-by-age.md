# Understanding Asset Allocation by Age

Asset allocation is the cornerstone of long-term wealth building. Rather than chasing individual stock picks, successful investors focus on distributing capital across different asset classes based on their age, risk tolerance, and financial goals. This article breaks down the science and practice of age-based asset allocation.

## The Core Principle: Time Horizon Matters

Your age determines how much risk you can take. A 25-year-old investor has 40+ years until retirement, so they can recover from market downturns. A 65-year-old needs to preserve capital and generate income. The longer your time horizon, the more volatile assets you can hold.

The fundamental relationship is simple:

**Age-Based Stock Allocation = 110 - Your Age** (conservative)
**Age-Based Stock Allocation = 120 - Your Age** (moderate)
**Age-Based Stock Allocation = 140 - Your Age** (aggressive)

These formulas adjust as you age. A 30-year-old using the moderate formula would hold 90% stocks (120 - 30). By age 60, the same formula suggests 60% stocks.

## Classic Age-Based Allocation Models

### The Vanguard Life Strategy Funds Model

Vanguard offers pre-built portfolios for different life stages:

| Life Stage | Stocks | Bonds | Target Audience |
|-----------|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Age 20-35 | 90% | 10% | Growth Phase |
| Age 35-50 | 75% | 25% | Accumulation Phase |
| Age 50-60 | 60% | 40% | Pre-Retirement Phase |
| Age 60-70 | 50% | 50% | Early Retirement Phase |
| Age 70+ | 40% | 60% | Distribution Phase |

This model recognizes that as you age, you shift from growth-focused investing to income and preservation.

### The Rule of 100 vs. Rule of 120

The traditional "Rule of 100" suggests:
- Stock allocation = 100 - your age
- Bond allocation = your age

A 40-year-old would hold 60% stocks, 40% bonds.

The modern "Rule of 120" increases stock exposure:
- Stock allocation = 120 - your age
- Bond allocation = your age minus 20

A 40-year-old would hold 80% stocks, 40% bonds (with slight flexibility).

Given longer lifespans and lower bond yields, many financial advisors now recommend the Rule of 120 or even Rule of 140 for young investors.

## Practical Asset Allocation by Life Stage

### Stage 1: Ages 20-35 (Growth Phase)

**Recommended Allocation:**
- 90% Stocks (U.S., International, Emerging Markets)
- 10% Bonds or Alternatives

**Rationale:** You have decades to recover from market downturns. Focus on capital appreciation. Dollar-cost averaging (monthly investments) smooths market volatility.

**Example Portfolio ($10,000):**
- $4,500: VTSAX (U.S. Total Stock Market)
- $3,000: VTIAX (International Stocks)
- $1,500: VTI or VOO (Large-cap focus)
- $1,000: BND (Total Bond Market)

**Historical Returns:** U.S. stocks averaged 10.2% annually over 100 years. Even after multiple bear markets, starting young compounds to substantial wealth.

### Stage 2: Ages 35-50 (Accumulation Phase)

**Recommended Allocation:**
- 75% Stocks (U.S., International, Small-Cap)
- 20% Bonds
- 5% Alternatives (REITs, Commodities)

**Rationale:** You're in peak earning years. Maximize retirement contributions. Slight bond increase provides stability as you approach mid-career.

**Example Portfolio ($100,000):**
- $45,000: VTSAX (U.S. Stocks)
- $22,500: VTIAX (International Stocks)
- $7,500: VB (Small-Cap Stocks)
- $20,000: BND (Bonds)
- $5,000: VNQ (REITs)

**Tax Consideration:** Use tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA) for bonds. Keep stocks in taxable accounts to benefit from preferential capital gains rates.

### Stage 3: Ages 50-60 (Pre-Retirement Phase)

**Recommended Allocation:**
- 60% Stocks
- 35% Bonds
- 5% Alternatives

**Rationale:** You can catch up on retirement savings. Still have time for recovery, but reducing volatility matters more. Consider increasing bond duration to lock in yields.

**Example Portfolio ($250,000):**
- $105,000: VTSAX
- $52,500: VTIAX
- $42,500: BND
- $35,000: VBTLX (Intermediate-Term Bonds)
- $15,000: VNQ

**Rebalancing:** At this stage, rebalance quarterly to maintain target allocation. Rising stock prices will increase equity exposure beyond your target.

### Stage 4: Ages 60-70 (Early Retirement/Distribution Phase)

**Recommended Allocation:**
- 50% Stocks
- 45% Bonds
- 5% Alternatives

**Rationale:** Still need growth to cover 30+ year retirement. But generating income becomes important. Bonds provide stability and income.

**Example Portfolio ($500,000):**
- $175,000: VTSAX
- $75,000: VTIAX
- $150,000: BND
- $75,000: SCHB (Extended Market, Small/Mid-Cap exposure)
- $25,000: VNQ

**Income Focus:** Increase dividend-paying stocks (VOD, SCHD). Consider bond ladder for predictable income.

### Stage 5: Ages 70+ (Preservation Phase)

**Recommended Allocation:**
- 40% Stocks
- 55% Bonds
- 5% Alternatives

**Rationale:** RMDs (Required Minimum Distributions) provide withdrawals. Preserve purchasing power while generating income. Sequence returns matter significantly.

**Example Portfolio ($600,000):**
- $150,000: VTSAX
- $90,000: VTIAX
- $220,000: BND
- $90,000: VBTLX
- $50,000: VNQ

**Withdrawal Strategy:** Use 4% rule ($24,000 first-year withdrawal). Rebalance annually to maintain target allocation and harvest tax losses.

## Beyond Age: Personalizing Your Allocation

Age-based models are frameworks, not rules. Adjust for:

**Higher Risk Tolerance:** Add 10-15% more stocks if you can emotionally handle 50% drawdowns without panic selling.

**Lower Risk Tolerance:** Subtract 10-15% from stock allocation if volatility prevents you from staying invested during bear markets.

**Income Needs:** If you need current income, prioritize dividend stocks and bonds over growth stocks.

**Pension Availability:** If you have a pension, reduce stock allocation by 10-15% since pension acts like a bond.

**Windfall/Inheritance:** Don't change allocation suddenly. Dollar-cost average windfalls over 6-12 months.

**Employer Stock:** Most employers don't count employee stock in published allocations. If you hold 20% company stock, reduce other stock positions.

## The Mathematics of Allocation Over Time

Using historical data, let's compare two investors:

**Investor A (Age 25):** 90/10 allocation, invests $5,000/year for 40 years
- Average annual return: 9.2% (90% stocks, 10% bonds)
- Ending value: approximately $1,428,000

**Investor B (Age 25):** 60/40 allocation, same contribution
- Average annual return: 7.8% (60% stocks, 40% bonds)
- Ending value: approximately $845,000

The 30-point allocation difference over 40 years creates a $583,000 wealth gap. This illustrates why age-based allocation prioritizes growth when you're young.

## Rebalancing Strategy

Annual rebalancing maintains your target allocation:

**Rebalancing Formula:**
1. Calculate current allocation percentage for each asset
2. Identify drift from target (more than 5% variance)
3. Sell overweighted assets, buy underweighted ones

**Example:** You target 80% stocks, 20% bonds. After strong market years, stocks represent 88% of your portfolio. Sell $8,000 of stocks for every $100,000 to return to 80/20.

**Tax Implications:** Rebalance in tax-deferred accounts (401k, IRA) first. In taxable accounts, use new contributions to buy underweighted positions rather than selling winners.

## Common Allocation Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Ignoring the glide path.** Young investors holding 40% bonds miss 40 years of stock growth. Follow your age-based recommendation.

**Mistake 2: Chasing returns.** After stock market gains, increasing equity allocation to "catch up" locks in gains and creates higher downside risk.

**Mistake 3: Over-diversifying.** More than five holdings create complexity without meaningful diversification benefit. Core holdings (VTI, VTIAX, BND) cover everything.

**Mistake 4: Failing to rebalance.** Without rebalancing, successful stock markets gradually shift you toward 95% stocks—far riskier than intended.

**Mistake 5: Forgetting about inflation.** A 60/40 allocation from 1980 to 1990 preserved nominal wealth but lost purchasing power. Even conservative allocations need real (inflation-adjusted) growth.

## Conclusion

Asset allocation by age provides a scientific framework for building wealth across decades. Start aggressive when young, gradually shift conservative as you age. Stick to your allocation through market cycles. Rebalance annually. This simple discipline outperforms 90% of active investors seeking the next hot stock pick.

The best allocation is the one you'll stay committed to across market booms and crashes. Choose a framework that lets you sleep at night, then execute it consistently.
