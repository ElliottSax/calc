# How to Use Capital Gains with Reinvestment

Capital gains represent the profit realized when selling an investment at a higher price than purchase. Rather than withdrawing these gains, sophisticated investors reinvest them, creating exponential wealth acceleration. This strategy—reinvesting capital gains into additional income-generating assets—compounds returns at multiple levels, transforming moderate profits into substantial wealth over decades. Understanding capital gains mechanics and reinvestment strategies enables investors to maximize after-tax wealth accumulation.

## Capital Gains Fundamentals

Capital gains occur when investment value increases. The tax treatment depends on how long you hold the investment and your income level.

### Capital Gains Formula

**Capital Gain = Sale Price - Purchase Price - Transaction Costs**

**Capital Gain Tax = Capital Gain × Tax Rate**

### Capital Gains Tax Rates (2026 US Tax Code)

| Income Level | Long-Term Rate | Short-Term Rate |
|---|---|---|
| $0 - $47,025 (single) | 0% | Ordinary income (10-12%) |
| $47,025 - $518,900 | 15% | Ordinary income (22-35%) |
| $518,900+ | 20% | Ordinary income (37%) |

Long-term capital gains (held 1+ year) receive preferential tax treatment, making buy-and-hold investing more tax-efficient than frequent trading.

### Real Example: Capital Gain Calculation

**Purchase:** 100 shares of AAPL at $150 = $15,000
**Sale:** 100 shares at $210 = $21,000
**Capital gain:** $21,000 - $15,000 = $6,000
**Hold period:** 2 years (long-term)
**Tax rate:** 15% (long-term capital gains)
**Tax owed:** $6,000 × 15% = $900
**After-tax proceeds:** $21,000 - $900 = $20,100

## Reinvesting Capital Gains: The Power Strategy

Rather than spending capital gains, reinvesting them creates compounding on top of the original investment's compounding.

### Reinvestment Formula

**Future Value = Principal Growth + Reinvested Gains Growth**

**FV = [P(1 + r)^n] + [Capital Gains × (1 + r)^(n-t)]**

Where:
- P = Principal
- r = Return rate
- n = Total years
- t = Year gains were realized

### Real Example: VOO (S&P 500) Capital Gains Reinvestment

**Scenario:** Investor purchases VOO and holds for 15 years

**Investment Details:**
- Initial investment: $10,000
- VOO average annual return: 9.5%
- Realization point: Year 5 (sell at profit, realize gains)

**Year 5 Portfolio Value:**
FV(5) = $10,000(1.095)^5 = $15,407

**Capital gain at Year 5:**
Capital gain = $15,407 - $10,000 = $5,407
After-tax (15% long-term): $5,407 × 0.85 = $4,595

**Without Reinvestment (spend the gains):**
- Years 6-15: Original $10,000 continues growing
- FV(10-year additional growth): $10,000 × (1.095)^10 = $24,437

**With Reinvestment (reinvest the gains):**
- Reinvest $4,595 after-tax gains alongside original $15,407
- Total portfolio at year 5: $15,407 + $4,595 = $20,002
- Years 6-15: $20,002 grows for 10 more years
- FV(15): $20,002 × (1.095)^10 = $48,748

**Comparison:**
- Without reinvestment: $24,437
- With reinvestment: $48,747
- Advantage: $24,310 (99.6% additional wealth)

Reinvesting gains at the midpoint effectively doubles final wealth by allowing gains to compound for the remaining period.

## Strategic Capital Gains Harvesting

Tax-loss harvesting and strategic gain realization optimize capital gains treatment.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting Strategy

When an investment declines, selling at a loss offsets capital gains, reducing tax liability.

**Example: Multi-Position Portfolio Rebalancing**

| Position | Purchase | Current | Gain/Loss | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| VTI | $5,000 | $7,200 | +$2,200 gain | Hold |
| VXUS | $3,000 | $2,400 | -$600 loss | Sell |
| SCHD | $4,000 | $6,100 | +$2,100 gain | Hold |
| BND | $3,000 | $2,850 | -$150 loss | Sell |

**Tax Calculation:**
- Realized gains: $2,200 + $2,100 = $4,300
- Realized losses: $600 + $150 = $750
- Net capital gain: $4,300 - $750 = $3,550
- Tax (15% long-term): $532.50
- Tax savings through harvesting: $532.50

**Reinvestment Plan:**
- Sell VXUS ($2,400) and rebuy similar (IXUS) to reset cost basis
- Sell BND ($2,850) and rebuy similar (BSV)
- Redeploy sales proceeds into harvested positions immediately
- Maintain market exposure while reducing tax liability

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Should I reinvest all capital gains, or keep some cash?**
Reinvest all long-term capital gains unless you need cash for emergencies or upcoming expenses. The compounding benefit of reinvestment typically exceeds any benefit from holding cash earning 4-5%. Maintain emergency funds separately (6-12 months expenses).

**Q2: Is it better to realize gains or let them compound?**
Immediate reinvestment after gain realization outperforms holding unrealized gains because reinvested gains compound for the entire remaining period. However, the tax efficiency difference is minimal (15% long-term vs. 0% in tax-advantaged accounts), so the decision should prioritize maximizing reinvestment amount.

**Q3: How do I handle capital gains on inherited investments?**
Inherited investments receive a "step-up in basis," meaning the cost basis resets to the value at death. You can immediately sell inherited investments with zero capital gains tax. Reinvest the full proceeds—you owe no taxes on inherited gains.

**Q4: What if I realize capital gains from cryptocurrency instead of stocks?**
The mathematics are identical, but cryptocurrency often triggers short-term gains (37% top tax rate vs. 20% long-term). This significantly reduces after-tax reinvestment amount. Holding cryptocurrency 1+ year before sale captures long-term rates, improving reinvestment.

**Q5: Should I use leveraged ETFs to amplify capital gains?**
Leveraged ETFs (3x leverage) create larger gains but also larger losses, higher fees (0.95%+), and tax complexity. For most investors, simple 3x leverage increases risk without proportional return benefit. Standard ETFs with disciplined reinvestment outperform leveraged strategies after taxes and fees.
