# How to Read Financial Statements for Stock Investing

Every publicly traded company in the United States files detailed financial statements with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). These documents tell you everything about a company's financial health: how much money it makes, how much debt it carries, and whether it generates enough cash to sustain its dividend.

For dividend investors, reading financial statements is not optional. A high dividend yield means nothing if the company cannot afford to pay it. Financial statements reveal the truth behind the yield.

There are three primary financial statements: the income statement, the balance sheet, and the cash flow statement. Each tells a different part of the financial story.

## The Income Statement (Profit and Loss)

The income statement shows how much revenue a company earned, what it spent, and the resulting profit or loss over a specific period (quarter or year). Think of it as the company's report card for the period.

### Key Line Items

**Revenue (Sales)**: The total amount of money the company received from selling its products or services. This is the top line. For Johnson & Johnson (JNJ), 2024 revenue was approximately $85.2 billion.

**Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: The direct costs of producing the products or services sold. Includes raw materials, manufacturing labor, and factory overhead. Revenue minus COGS equals gross profit.

**Gross Profit**: Revenue minus COGS. Measures how efficiently the company produces its products. Gross margin (gross profit / revenue) varies by industry: software companies may have 80% gross margins, while retailers operate at 25-35%.

**Operating Expenses**: Costs not directly tied to production, including selling, general, and administrative expenses (SG&A), research and development (R&D), and marketing. These are the costs of running the business.

**Operating Income (EBIT)**: Gross profit minus operating expenses. This is the profit from the company's core business operations before interest and taxes. Operating margin (operating income / revenue) indicates operational efficiency.

**Net Income**: The bottom line. Revenue minus all expenses, including interest, taxes, depreciation, and one-time charges. This is the profit available to shareholders.

**Earnings Per Share (EPS)**: Net income divided by the number of outstanding shares. If a company earns $10 billion in net income and has 2.5 billion shares outstanding, EPS is $4.00. EPS is the most widely followed metric on the income statement.

### What Dividend Investors Should Focus On

**Payout ratio**: Dividends per share divided by earnings per share. If a company earns $5.00 per share and pays $2.00 in dividends, the payout ratio is 40%. A payout ratio below 60% generally indicates a sustainable dividend with room for growth. Above 80% raises concerns about sustainability.

**Earnings trend**: Look at 5-10 years of EPS data. Consistently growing earnings support consistent dividend growth. Declining or volatile earnings are warning signs.

**Revenue growth**: Even the best cost management cannot sustain a company indefinitely without revenue growth. Look for positive revenue growth on a 3-5 year basis.

## The Balance Sheet (Financial Position)

The balance sheet is a snapshot of what the company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the difference (shareholders' equity) at a specific point in time. The fundamental equation is: Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity.

### Key Line Items

**Current Assets**: Cash and assets expected to be converted to cash within one year. Includes cash and equivalents, short-term investments, accounts receivable (money owed by customers), and inventory.

**Long-Term Assets**: Assets with useful life beyond one year. Includes property, plant, and equipment (PP&E), intellectual property, goodwill (premium paid for acquisitions), and long-term investments.

**Current Liabilities**: Debts and obligations due within one year. Includes accounts payable (money owed to suppliers), short-term debt, and accrued expenses.

**Long-Term Liabilities**: Debts and obligations due beyond one year. Primarily long-term debt (bonds, loans) and pension obligations.

**Shareholders' Equity**: Total assets minus total liabilities. Represents the book value of the company, or what shareholders would theoretically receive if all assets were sold and all debts paid.

### What Dividend Investors Should Focus On

**Debt-to-equity ratio**: Total debt divided by shareholders' equity. Measures financial leverage. A ratio below 1.0 means the company has more equity than debt. A ratio above 2.0 indicates heavy leverage, which increases risk during economic downturns and may threaten dividend sustainability.

**Current ratio**: Current assets divided by current liabilities. Measures short-term financial health. A ratio above 1.5 indicates the company can comfortably meet short-term obligations. Below 1.0 is a warning sign.

**Cash position**: Total cash and short-term investments. A large cash position provides a safety buffer for maintaining dividends during temporary earnings declines. Apple, for example, maintains over $60 billion in cash and marketable securities.

**Goodwill and intangible assets**: Large goodwill balances (from expensive acquisitions) can be written down, destroying shareholder equity. If goodwill exceeds 50% of total assets, the company may have overpaid for acquisitions.

## The Cash Flow Statement (Actual Money In and Out)

The cash flow statement tracks the actual movement of cash in and out of the company. It is arguably the most important statement for dividend investors because dividends are paid in cash, not accounting earnings.

A company can report positive net income while hemorrhaging cash (through inventory buildup, excessive capital spending, or accounts receivable delays). The cash flow statement reveals the truth.

### Three Sections

**Operating Cash Flow (OCF)**: Cash generated by the company's core business operations. This is the most important number for dividend investors. OCF must consistently exceed dividend payments for the dividend to be sustainable.

**Investing Cash Flow**: Cash spent on or received from investments. Includes capital expenditures (CapEx), acquisitions, and proceeds from asset sales. This is typically negative, reflecting the cost of maintaining and growing the business.

**Financing Cash Flow**: Cash from borrowing, stock issuance, share repurchases, and dividend payments. Dividend payments appear here as a cash outflow.

### What Dividend Investors Should Focus On

**Free Cash Flow (FCF)**: Operating cash flow minus capital expenditures. This is the cash available to pay dividends, repay debt, buy back shares, or fund growth. Free cash flow is the ultimate measure of a company's ability to pay dividends.

**FCF payout ratio**: Dividends paid divided by free cash flow. This is more revealing than the earnings-based payout ratio. If a company generates $4 billion in FCF and pays $2.5 billion in dividends, the FCF payout ratio is 62.5%. A ratio below 70% is generally sustainable.

**Consistency**: Look for stable or growing FCF over multiple years. One bad year is not concerning, but a declining FCF trend over 3-5 years suggests the company may eventually need to cut its dividend.

## Practical Example: Evaluating Procter & Gamble (PG)

Procter & Gamble is a Dividend King (68 consecutive years of dividend increases). Here is what its financial statements reveal:

**Income Statement (Fiscal Year 2024)**:
- Revenue: $84.0 billion
- Net income: $14.9 billion
- EPS: $6.07
- Dividend per share: $3.83
- Earnings payout ratio: 63.1%

**Balance Sheet**:
- Total assets: $120.1 billion
- Total debt: $33.3 billion
- Shareholders' equity: $47.1 billion
- Debt-to-equity: 0.71
- Current ratio: 0.75 (low but normal for consumer staples companies with reliable cash flow)

**Cash Flow Statement**:
- Operating cash flow: $19.8 billion
- Capital expenditures: $3.5 billion
- Free cash flow: $16.3 billion
- Dividends paid: $9.3 billion
- FCF payout ratio: 57.1%

**Assessment**: PG's 63% earnings payout ratio and 57% FCF payout ratio are comfortably sustainable. The company generates nearly twice the cash it needs for dividends. The debt-to-equity ratio of 0.71 is manageable. Revenue has grown modestly (2-4% annually), and the company has raised dividends for 68 consecutive years. This is a textbook example of a sustainable dividend stock.

## Red Flags in Financial Statements

Watch for these warning signs that may indicate dividend trouble:

**Earnings payout ratio above 90%**: The company is paying out nearly all its earnings, leaving no margin for error.

**Declining free cash flow**: If FCF has decreased for 3+ consecutive years, the company may be struggling to fund operations and dividends simultaneously.

**Rising debt with stagnant revenue**: A company borrowing money to maintain its dividend rather than funding it from operations is on an unsustainable path.

**Large one-time charges**: Repeated "one-time" charges or restructuring costs suggest ongoing operational problems disguised as exceptional items.

**Accounts receivable growing faster than revenue**: Indicates the company may be booking revenue that has not been collected, potentially inflating earnings.

**Declining gross margins**: Suggests the company is losing pricing power or facing rising input costs without the ability to pass them through.

## Where to Find Financial Statements

- **SEC EDGAR** (edgar.sec.gov): Free access to all public company filings including 10-K (annual) and 10-Q (quarterly) reports.
- **Company investor relations pages**: Most companies maintain an investor relations section with financial reports, presentations, and press releases.
- **Brokerage research tools**: Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard provide summarized financial data within their platforms.
- **Financial data sites**: Macrotrends.net, Stock Analysis, and Simply Wall St provide free, formatted financial statement data.

## Key Takeaways

- The income statement shows profitability (is the company making money?), the balance sheet shows financial position (is it financially stable?), and the cash flow statement shows cash generation (can it actually pay its dividend?).
- Free cash flow, not net income, is the most important metric for evaluating dividend sustainability. A company must generate more cash than it pays in dividends.
- The FCF payout ratio should be below 70% for most dividend stocks. Above 90% is a warning sign.
- Look for consistent multi-year trends, not single-year snapshots. Five years of growing revenue, earnings, and free cash flow is far more meaningful than one strong quarter.
- Red flags include rising debt with stagnant revenue, declining margins, and earnings payout ratios approaching or exceeding 100%.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How often should I review a company's financial statements?

Review quarterly earnings reports (10-Q filings) for each stock you own. These are released four times per year. The annual report (10-K) provides the most comprehensive view. At minimum, review the annual report and scan each quarterly report for significant changes in revenue, earnings, and free cash flow.

### Which financial statement is most important for dividend investors?

The cash flow statement, specifically free cash flow. Dividends are paid in cash, not accounting earnings. A company can report positive net income while generating negative free cash flow. Free cash flow tells you whether the company can actually afford its dividend payments.

### Can I rely on brokerage platforms for financial data instead of reading full filings?

Brokerage platforms and financial data sites provide accurate summarized data that is sufficient for most individual investors. However, if you identify a potential red flag (unusual charges, declining margins, rising debt), reading the relevant sections of the actual 10-K or 10-Q can provide context that summaries omit. Management discussion and analysis (MD&A) sections are particularly informative.

### What is the difference between GAAP and non-GAAP earnings?

GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) earnings follow standardized accounting rules. Non-GAAP or "adjusted" earnings exclude items management considers non-recurring (stock compensation, restructuring charges, acquisition costs). Companies often highlight non-GAAP earnings to present a more favorable picture. Be cautious: some companies routinely exclude legitimate costs as "non-recurring." Always evaluate the GAAP earnings alongside non-GAAP figures.
