# Calculating Stock Valuation with Reinvestment

Stock valuation represents one of the most critical skills for investors seeking to identify underpriced opportunities and make informed investment decisions. When combined with reinvestment strategies, proper valuation helps maximize long-term wealth creation through compound growth. This guide explores multiple valuation methodologies and demonstrates how reinvestment amplifies returns from properly-valued stock investments.

## Understanding Stock Valuation Fundamentals

Stock valuation answers a simple question: What is this company worth? Multiple approaches exist, each revealing different aspects of company value. Professional investors use several methodologies to triangulate fair value and identify investment opportunities.

The foundation of all valuation rests on a simple principle: a company is worth the present value of all future cash flows it will generate. This concept underlies every valuation method.

## Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio Method

The P/E ratio is the most widely used valuation metric:

**P/E Ratio = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)**

Example: Apple trading at $190 with $6.05 EPS
- P/E Ratio = $190 / $6.05 = 31.4x

**Valuation interpretation**:
- P/E of 15-20: Undervalued (historical average ~16)
- P/E of 20-30: Fairly valued
- P/E above 30: Growth premium (expecting earnings growth)
- P/E below 10: Distressed/mature industry

**Using P/E for valuation**: If a company's fair P/E should be 18x (based on industry average) and current EPS is $5:
- Fair value = $5 × 18 = $90 per share
- If trading at $75, it's 17% undervalued

## Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio Analysis

P/B ratio compares market price to company book value (assets minus liabilities):

**P/B Ratio = Stock Price / Book Value Per Share**

Example: Bank of America with price $35 and book value per share of $25
- P/B Ratio = $35 / $25 = 1.4x

**Interpretation**:
- P/B below 1.0: Trading below asset value (potential value opportunity)
- P/B 1.0-3.0: Normal valuation range
- P/B above 5.0: Premium valuation (likely growth expectations)

Value stocks typically have P/B ratios below 2.0, while growth stocks often exceed 5.0.

## Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

DDM values stocks based on expected future dividends:

**Stock Value = D1 / (r - g)**

Where:
- D1 = Expected next year dividend
- r = Required return (discount rate)
- g = Long-term dividend growth rate

**Example calculation**:
- Current dividend: $3.00 per share
- Expected growth rate: 5% annually
- Required return: 10%

Next year dividend: $3.00 × 1.05 = $3.15

**Stock value = $3.15 / (0.10 - 0.05) = $3.15 / 0.05 = $63 per share**

If trading at $55, the stock is undervalued by 13%.

## Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) Valuation

FCFE method values companies based on cash available to shareholders:

**FCFE = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures - Debt Repayment + New Debt**

Example annual FCFE calculation:
- Operating cash flow: $1,200 million
- Capital expenditures: $300 million
- Debt repayment: $100 million
- New debt issued: $150 million

**FCFE = $1,200 - $300 - $100 + $150 = $950 million**

With 500 million shares outstanding:
- **FCFE per share = $950M / 500M = $1.90 per share**

Using 8% discount rate and 3% growth:
- **Stock value = $1.90 × 1.03 / (0.08 - 0.03) = $1.957 / 0.05 = $39.14 per share**

## Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

DCF represents the most comprehensive valuation method:

1. Project future free cash flows (5-10 years)
2. Calculate terminal value (value beyond projection period)
3. Discount all cash flows to present value

**DCF Formula**:

**Stock Value = [CF₁/(1+r)¹ + CF₂/(1+r)² + ... + CFₙ/(1+r)ⁿ] + [Terminal Value/(1+r)ⁿ]**

**Example 5-year DCF**:

| Year | Projected FCF | Discount Factor (10%) | Present Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $100M | 0.909 | $90.9M |
| 2 | $110M | 0.826 | $90.9M |
| 3 | $121M | 0.751 | $90.9M |
| 4 | $133M | 0.683 | $90.9M |
| 5 | $146M | 0.621 | $90.7M |
| **Terminal Value** | **$2,190M** | **0.621** | **$1,361M** |

(Terminal value uses 3% perpetual growth: $146M × 1.03 / (0.10 - 0.03))

- **Total enterprise value: $1,816M**
- Less: Net debt: $200M
- **Equity value: $1,616M**
- Shares outstanding: 100M
- **Fair value per share: $16.16**

If trading at $12, the stock is 25% undervalued.

## Reinvestment Impact on Stock Valuations

Reinvestment dramatically changes long-term stock returns:

**Total Return Formula = Dividend Yield + Capital Appreciation + Reinvestment Effect**

**Example: Coca-Cola (KO)**

Investment: 100 shares at $60 = $6,000
Dividend yield: 3.1% annually
Expected capital appreciation: 5% annually
Holding period: 20 years

**Without reinvestment**:
- Dividends collected: $6,000 × 0.031 × 20 = $3,720
- Capital appreciation: $6,000 × (1.05)^20 = $15,933
- **Total wealth: $15,933 + $3,720 = $19,653**

**With reinvestment (DRIP)**:
- Total return with reinvestment: 8.1% annually (3.1% + 5%)
- Value: $6,000 × (1.081)^20 = $28,594
- **Additional wealth from reinvestment: $8,941**

Reinvestment amplifies returns by 45%!

## Comparing Valuations Across Methods

Different valuation methods often produce different fair values. Professional investors use multiple approaches to triangulate:

**Example: Microsoft valuation comparison**

Current price: $400
Shares outstanding: 2.4 billion
Market cap: $960 billion

**Method 1: P/E Valuation**
- Current EPS: $11.89
- Fair P/E: 30x (tech industry average)
- Fair value: $11.89 × 30 = $356.70
- Assessment: Overvalued by 12%

**Method 2: P/B Valuation**
- Book value per share: $15.75
- Fair P/B: 25x (tech company average)
- Fair value: $15.75 × 25 = $393.75
- Assessment: Fairly valued

**Method 3: Dividend Discount Model**
- Expected dividend: $0.68
- Growth rate: 10%
- Discount rate: 9%
- Fair value: $0.68 × 1.10 / (0.09 - 0.10) = Undefined (growth exceeds discount)
- Assessment: Use growth model instead

**Method 4: DCF Model**
- Projected FCF growth: 15% (5 years), 4% thereafter
- Discount rate: 8%
- Fair value: $425
- Assessment: Undervalued by 6%

**Consensus valuation**: $375-$425 (11-6% below to overvalued)

## Valuation Metrics for ETFs and Funds

Valuations apply to ETFs as well:

**Schwab U.S. Dividend (SCHD)**:
- Current price: $82
- Dividend yield: 3.8%
- P/E of holdings: 16.4x (below market)
- Fair value based on holdings: $78-$85
- Assessment: Fairly valued

**Calculate ETF intrinsic value**:

ETF Intrinsic Value = (Sum of All Holdings' Fair Values) / Shares Outstanding

If SCHD holds 100 companies with fair value totaling $820 million and 10 million shares:
- Intrinsic value = $820M / 10M = $82 per share

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Which valuation method is most accurate?**
A: No single method is perfect. Professional investors use 3-5 methods and average results. DCF is theoretically sound but highly sensitive to assumptions. P/E is practical but can be misleading for high-growth companies.

**Q: How often should I revalue my holdings?**
A: Quarterly earnings releases warrant valuation updates. Significant news warrants immediate reassessment. Avoid obsessive valuation checking (monthly is excessive).

**Q: What discount rate should I use in DCF models?**
A: Typically 7-10% for stocks. Use 7-8% for conservative companies, 10-12% for growth companies. Match your opportunity cost of capital.

**Q: Can reinvestment be guaranteed?**
A: No. Companies can cut dividends. However, quality dividend aristocrats with 25+ year increase streaks make reinvestment highly probable.

**Q: How do I value a non-dividend-paying growth stock?**
A: Use DCF (free cash flow method) or P/E ratio. Growth stocks are valued on earnings growth expectations, not current earnings.
