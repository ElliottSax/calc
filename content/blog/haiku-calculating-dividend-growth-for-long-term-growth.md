# Calculating Dividend Growth for Long-term Growth

Dividend growth represents the annual percentage increase in dividends a company pays. Over decades, even modest dividend growth rates compound into substantial income increases, transforming a modest yield into a powerful income stream. Understanding how to calculate and project dividend growth shapes long-term wealth strategies.

## Understanding Dividend Growth Rates

Dividend growth rate measures how much a company increases its dividend per share annually. A company paying $1.00 per share in dividends that increases to $1.05 the next year achieved 5% dividend growth. This metric reveals whether companies prioritize shareholder income and whether they generate sufficient earnings to sustain increases.

The power of dividend growth accumulates over time. A company growing dividends 7% annually will double its dividend payment every 10 years. Over a 30-year career, a 7% dividend growth rate multiplies the initial dividend by roughly 7.6 times.

## The Dividend Growth Formula

Calculate annual dividend growth using this formula:

$$\text{Dividend Growth Rate} = \frac{\text{Current Dividend} - \text{Prior Year Dividend}}{\text{Prior Year Dividend}} \times 100$$

For example, if a company paid $2.40 per share annually last year and increases to $2.52 this year:

$$\text{Dividend Growth Rate} = \frac{\$2.52 - \$2.40}{\$2.40} \times 100 = 5.0\%$$

When calculating long-term growth rates across multiple years, use the Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) formula:

$$\text{CAGR} = \left(\frac{\text{Ending Dividend}}{\text{Beginning Dividend}}\right)^{\frac{1}{n}} - 1$$

where n equals the number of years.

Calculate 10-year CAGR from 2015 to 2024:

$$\text{CAGR} = \left(\frac{\$2.20}{\$1.48}\right)^{\frac{1}{9}} - 1 = (1.4865)^{0.1111} - 1 = 0.0406 = 4.06\%$$

Over 10 years, Coca-Cola grew dividends at 4.06% annually, increasing the per-share dividend by 48.6%.

## Calculating Yield-on-Cost with Dividend Growth

Yield-on-cost measures your dividend based on your original purchase price, not current market price. If you bought KO at $40 per share and it now trades at $60, your yield-on-cost differs from current yield:

- **Current Yield** = $2.20 / $60 = 3.67%
- **Yield-on-Cost** = $2.20 / $40 = 5.50%

Your actual return on the original $40 investment is 5.50%, even though new investors only earn 3.67%. As dividends grow, yield-on-cost increases while the stock price might decline. This creates powerful returns for long-term holders:

| Year | Dividend | Stock Price | Current Yield | Yield-on-Cost |
|------|----------|------------|---|---|
| Year 1 | $2.00 | $50 | 4.0% | 4.0% |
| Year 5 | $2.55 | $48 | 5.3% | 5.1% |
| Year 10 | $3.26 | $52 | 6.3% | 6.5% |
| Year 15 | $4.17 | $55 | 7.6% | 8.3% |
| Year 20 | $5.33 | $60 | 8.9% | 10.7% |

Even if the stock price appreciates modestly, yield-on-cost (the income you receive on your original capital) grows dramatically due to dividend increases.

## Evaluating Dividend Growth Sustainability

Not all dividend growth rates are sustainable. Evaluate sustainability by examining:

- **Payout Ratio**: Dividends divided by net income. Ratios above 80% leave little room for growth.
- **Free Cash Flow Growth**: Dividends must come from growing cash flow.
- **Debt Levels**: High debt limits flexibility for dividend increases.
- **Industry Trends**: Declining industries struggle to grow dividends.

A company with 50% payout ratio and 8% free cash flow growth can sustain 6-7% dividend growth. A company with 90% payout ratio and declining revenues struggles to maintain current dividends.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What's a good dividend growth rate to expect?**
A: Quality dividend growers typically deliver 4-7% annual growth. Mature dividend aristocrats like KO, PG, and JNJ consistently achieve 4-6%. Younger dividend payers might grow faster initially. Average S&P 500 dividend growth runs 4-5%.

**Q: How does dividend growth affect stock price returns?**
A: Dividend growth contributes to total return alongside price appreciation. A stock returning 10% total might deliver 4% from dividends and 6% from price appreciation. Over decades, dividend growth's compounding effect becomes the return's largest component.

**Q: Can I project dividend growth indefinitely?**
A: No. Growth rates eventually moderate as companies mature. Use historical growth rates conservatively—if a company grew 8% for 10 years, assuming 6-7% future growth is more realistic than assuming 8% forever.

**Q: Does dividend growth outpace inflation?**
A: Quality dividend growers typically grow faster than inflation (currently 2-3%). KO's 4% growth and JNJ's 5.8% growth both exceed inflation, protecting purchasing power. This makes dividend stocks valuable in inflationary periods.

**Q: What happens if a company cuts its dividend?**
A: Dividend cuts hurt current income and often signal financial stress. Focus on dividend aristocrats (companies increasing dividends 25+ years) or dividend champions (50+ years) to minimize cut risk. SCHD ETF provides diversification across many growers, reducing single-company risk.

Dividend growth transforms modest initial yields into substantial income streams over decades. By calculating historical dividend growth rates, projecting future income based on sustainable growth, and understanding how growth compounds with reinvestment, you build retirement income independent of market volatility. Focus on quality dividend growers with 4-7% annual growth rates, monitor their sustainability through payout ratios and cash flow, and let compound growth multiply your income over time.
