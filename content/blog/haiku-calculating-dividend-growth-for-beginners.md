# Calculating Dividend Growth for Beginners

Understanding dividend growth is one of the most powerful concepts in wealth building. Whether you're just starting your investment journey or looking to expand your portfolio, calculating dividend growth will help you track the income-generating potential of your investments.

## What Is Dividend Growth?

Dividend growth measures how much the annual dividend payment from a stock or ETF increases year over year. It's the percentage increase in dividend payments, not the stock price. For example, if a company paid $2 per share in dividends last year and $2.20 this year, that's a 10% dividend growth rate.

## The Basic Dividend Growth Formula

The simplest way to calculate dividend growth is:

**Dividend Growth Rate = ((Current Year Dividend - Previous Year Dividend) / Previous Year Dividend) × 100**

For example:
- Previous year dividend: $1.50
- Current year dividend: $1.65
- Growth = (($1.65 - $1.50) / $1.50) × 100 = 10%

## Real-World Examples

Let's look at some actual dividend aristocrats and their growth patterns:

### Procter & Gamble (PG)
- 2022 dividend: $3.65
- 2023 dividend: $3.85
- 2024 dividend: $4.00
- Growth 2023-2024: (($4.00 - $3.85) / $3.85) × 100 = 3.9%

### Coca-Cola (KO)
- 2022 dividend: $1.68
- 2023 dividend: $1.76
- 2024 dividend: $1.84
- Growth 2023-2024: (($1.84 - $1.76) / $1.76) × 100 = 4.5%

### Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)
- 2022 dividend: $4.06
- 2023 dividend: $4.28
- 2024 dividend: $4.52
- Growth 2023-2024: (($4.52 - $4.28) / $4.28) × 100 = 5.6%

## Calculating Compound Dividend Growth

If you want to see how dividends have grown over multiple years, use the Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) formula:

**CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/Number of Years) - 1**

For example, with Johnson & Johnson:
- Beginning dividend (2022): $4.06
- Ending dividend (2024): $4.52
- Number of years: 2
- CAGR = ($4.52 / $4.06)^(1/2) - 1 = 1.0548 - 1 = 5.48%

This means JNJ's dividends grew at an average annual rate of 5.48% over the two-year period.

## Why Dividend Growth Matters

1. **Income increases automatically** - You don't need to do anything; the company pays you more
2. **Inflation hedge** - Growing dividends help offset inflation over time
3. **Compound effect** - Reinvesting dividends in companies that grow them creates exponential returns
4. **Market indicator** - Growing dividends signal company health and confidence

## Dividend Growth vs. Dividend Yield

These are different metrics:
- **Dividend Yield** = Annual Dividend / Current Stock Price
- **Dividend Growth** = Year-over-year percentage increase in the dividend

A stock might have a 2% yield but 8% growth. As dividends grow, the yield based on your original purchase price increases significantly over time.

## Common Mistakes When Calculating Dividend Growth

1. **Confusing total return with dividend growth** - Total return includes stock price appreciation
2. **Using only one quarter** - Use annual figures for consistency
3. **Forgetting special dividends** - Some companies pay one-time bonuses; focus on regular dividends
4. **Not adjusting for stock splits** - Companies that split shares will show artificial dividend changes

## FAQ

**Q: What's considered good dividend growth?**
A: 5-7% annual dividend growth is respectable for mature companies. Younger, growing companies might achieve 10%+ growth. Dividend aristocrats (25+ years of consecutive increases) typically average 6-8%.

**Q: How often do companies increase dividends?**
A: Most companies announce dividend increases once per year, typically in spring. Some increase twice yearly.

**Q: Why would a company cut its dividend?**
A: Economic downturns, industry disruption, or capital allocation changes. Companies cutting dividends signal financial stress.

**Q: Is 1% dividend growth bad?**
A: Not necessarily. Mature companies with stable, high yields (3-4%) might grow at 2-3% while still rewarding shareholders well.

**Q: Can I use dividend growth to predict future performance?**
A: Dividend growth history is one indicator of company health, but past growth doesn't guarantee future results. Always research the underlying business.

## Key Takeaways

Calculating dividend growth is straightforward: divide the difference between two years' dividends by the earlier dividend amount. Over time, dividend growth compounds, creating exponential wealth building. Start tracking your holdings' dividend growth today to understand your income stream better. Focus on companies with consistent 4-8% dividend growth rates, and you'll build a portfolio that pays you increasingly more each year.
