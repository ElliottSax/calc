# How to Use Stock Valuation Using Tools

Stock valuation separates disciplined investors from emotional traders. Rather than guessing whether a stock is "fairly priced," you calculate intrinsic value using standardized financial metrics and valuation tools. This guide walks through practical valuation methodologies, demonstrates how to use popular tools, and applies real calculations to dividend stocks and ETF components.

## Why Stock Valuation Matters

Valuation determines whether you pay a fair price for an investment. Warren Buffett famously stated, "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." Paying $100 for a stock worth $120 generates returns. Paying $100 for a stock worth $60 creates losses regardless of future potential.

The valuation gap explains investment performance variance. Buying undervalued stocks—those trading below intrinsic value—stacks odds in your favor over 5-10 year time horizons.

## Key Valuation Metrics

### Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E)

The P/E ratio divides stock price by earnings per share. It measures how many dollars investors pay for each dollar of earnings.

**Formula:**
P/E Ratio = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share

A stock trading at $120 with $5 earnings per share has a P/E of 24 ($120/$5). Historical S&P 500 average P/E hovers around 18-20, making 24 relatively expensive.

**Example: JNJ (Johnson & Johnson)**
- Stock price: $165
- Trailing twelve months earnings per share: $8.25
- P/E ratio: $165 / $8.25 = 20.0

A P/E of 20 suggests the market values JNJ's stable earnings. For comparison:
- S&P 500 average: 20-22 P/E
- Healthcare sector average: 18-22 P/E
- JNJ's historical range: 16-24 P/E

At 20, JNJ trades at its historical median valuation—neither cheap nor expensive.

### Price-to-Sales Ratio (P/S)

P/S divides market cap by total revenue. It's harder to manipulate than earnings-based metrics because revenue appears before expenses.

**Formula:**
P/S Ratio = Market Cap / Total Annual Revenue

**Example: Apple (AAPL)**
- Market cap: $2.8 trillion
- Annual revenue: $383 billion
- P/S ratio: $2,800B / $383B = 7.31

A P/S of 7.31 means investors value Apple at 7.31 times revenue. For comparison:
- Tech sector average: 4-6 P/S
- Apple's historical range: 4-8 P/S
- AAPL at 7.31 suggests premium valuation

### PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings-to-Growth)

The PEG ratio divides the P/E ratio by projected earnings growth rate. It answers, "Are you paying fairly for expected growth?"

**Formula:**
PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio / Expected Annual Earnings Growth Rate

If a stock has a P/E of 30 but 25% annual earnings growth:
PEG = 30 / 25 = 1.2

Generally, PEG below 1.0 suggests the stock is undervalued relative to growth. Above 2.0 suggests overvaluation.

**Example: Coca-Cola (KO)**
- Stock price: $62.50
- EPS: $2.65
- P/E ratio: $62.50 / $2.65 = 23.6
- Projected earnings growth: 5.5% annually
- PEG: 23.6 / 5.5 = 4.29

KO's PEG of 4.29 is high, suggesting you pay $4.29 for every $1 of expected growth. Historically, a PEG below 2.0 indicates better value. Coca-Cola trades at a premium because of brand strength and dividend reliability, not aggressive growth.

### Free Cash Flow Yield

Free cash flow represents cash available after capital investments. Many consider it the truest earnings measure.

**Formula:**
Free Cash Flow Yield = Free Cash Flow / Market Cap

**Example: Procter & Gamble (PG)**
- Market cap: $375 billion
- Annual free cash flow: $14.2 billion
- FCF yield: $14.2B / $375B = 3.8%

A 3.8% free cash flow yield means the company generates 3.8% of market cap in distributable cash annually. This is dividable (PG currently pays 2.3% dividend), or reinvestable for growth.

## The DCF Model: Calculating Intrinsic Value

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis calculates intrinsic value by projecting future free cash flows and discounting them to present value.

**Formula:**
Intrinsic Value = Σ [FCF_t / (1 + r)^t] + Terminal Value / (1 + r)^n

Where:
- FCF_t = Free cash flow in year t
- r = Discount rate (WACC, typically 8-10%)
- Terminal value = value of cash flows beyond projection period

### Simplified DCF Example: Dividend Growth Stock

**Assumptions for Procter & Gamble:**
- Current annual free cash flow: $14.2 billion
- Historical FCF growth rate: 3.5% annually
- Discount rate: 9%
- Projection period: 10 years
- Terminal growth rate: 2.5% (long-term GDP growth)

**Projected free cash flows:**

| Year | FCF ($B) | Discount Factor | Present Value ($B) |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| 1 | 14.71 | 0.9174 | 13.50 |
| 2 | 15.22 | 0.8417 | 12.80 |
| 3 | 15.76 | 0.7722 | 12.17 |
| 4 | 16.32 | 0.7084 | 11.57 |
| 5 | 16.89 | 0.6499 | 10.97 |
| 6 | 17.49 | 0.5963 | 10.42 |
| 7 | 18.10 | 0.5470 | 9.90 |
| 8 | 18.74 | 0.5019 | 9.40 |
| 9 | 19.39 | 0.4604 | 8.93 |
| 10 | 20.07 | 0.4224 | 8.48 |

**Sum of projected FCF present value: $108.14 billion**

**Terminal value:**
Year 10 FCF × (1 + terminal growth) / (discount rate - terminal growth)
= $20.07B × 1.025 / (0.09 - 0.025)
= $20.57B / 0.065
= $316.46 billion

**Terminal value present value:**
$316.46B × 0.4224 = $133.66 billion

**Enterprise value:**
$108.14B + $133.66B = $241.80 billion

**Equity value (assuming minimal net debt):**
Approximately $241.80 billion

**Per share intrinsic value:**
$241.80B / 2.58 billion shares = $93.70 per share

**Current valuation:**
At current price of $168.30, PG trades at 179% of DCF-calculated intrinsic value, suggesting significant overvaluation based on this model.

This doesn't mean avoiding PG—it means understanding you pay a premium for reliability, dividend growth, and brand strength.

## Graham Number: Conservative Valuation

Benjamin Graham developed the Graham Number as a conservative valuation metric:

**Formula:**
Graham Number = √ (22.5 × EPS × Book Value Per Share)

Where 22.5 represents Graham's recommended maximum P/E × price-to-book ratio.

**Example: Apple (AAPL)**
- EPS: $6.05
- Book value per share: $3.60
- Graham Number = √ (22.5 × 6.05 × 3.60) = √ (490.35) = $22.14

Current AAPL price ($195) is far above the Graham Number ($22.14), indicating aggressive valuations. Graham himself avoided such overvalued growth stocks, preferring deep-value opportunities.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What P/E ratio is "good"?**
A: Historical S&P 500 median P/E is 15-20. P/E below 15 suggests undervaluation; above 25 suggests overvaluation. Compare against sector peers and company historical ranges.

**Q: Is a high P/E always bad?**
A: No. High-growth companies justify higher P/E ratios. Apple's P/E of 29 reflects expectations of sustained growth. Coca-Cola's P/E of 24 reflects mature, stable cash flows. Context matters.

**Q: Which valuation metric is most important?**
A: Free cash flow yield is considered most reliable—cash is harder to manipulate than accounting earnings. P/E is most widely understood. Use multiple metrics for comprehensive assessment.

**Q: How often should I recalculate valuations?**
A: Review quarterly when companies report earnings. Significant changes (20%+ stock price moves, earnings surprises) warrant reassessment.

**Q: Can I value dividend stocks differently than growth stocks?**
A: Yes. Dividend stocks emphasize free cash flow yield and dividend sustainability. Growth stocks require higher P/E acceptance but should show 15%+ earnings growth to justify premium pricing.
