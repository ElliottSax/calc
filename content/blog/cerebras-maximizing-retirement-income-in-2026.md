## Optimize Withdrawal Sequence

How you withdraw funds impacts longevity. Follow a tax-efficient order:

1. **Taxable Accounts** (dividends, capital gains)  
2. **Tax-Deferred Accounts** (401(k), traditional IRA)  
3. **Tax-Free Accounts** (Roth IRA)

### Why This Order Works in 2026

- Qualified dividends are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. In 2026, single filers pay 0% on dividends up to $47,025; married couples up to $94,050.
- Delaying Social Security until age 70 increases benefits by 8% per year after full retirement age (FRA). For someone with a $2,000/month FRA benefit, delaying to 70 boosts it to **$3,040/month**.
- Roth conversions during low-income years (e.g., ages 62–66) reduce future RMDs and tax drag.

**Worked Example: Sequence Optimization**  
A 66-year-old couple retires in 2026 with:
- $300,000 taxable account (SCHD, VTI)
- $500,000 traditional IRA
- $200,000 Roth IRA
- No earned income

**Strategy:**
- Withdraw $40,000/year from taxable account (dividends + $26,180 in cost basis sales).
- File jointly, stay under $94,050 → **0% capital gains/dividend tax**.
- Delay Social Security until 70; invest IRA funds in low-volatility assets.
- At 72, RMDs begin: $500,000 / 27.4 (life expectancy factor) = **$18,248/year**.

By age 80, they’ve preserved $200,000 in Roth assets, withdrawn $560,000 tax-free, and deferred $200,000+ in tax liability.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### **1. What is a safe withdrawal rate in 2026?**  
The 4% rule remains viable with adjustments. For a $500,000 portfolio, $20,000/year is reasonable if:
- Portfolio includes dividend growth assets (yield > 3%)
- Inflation averages ≤ 3%
- Retiree delays Social Security to 70  
Consider 3.5% for early retirees (before 65).

### **2. Should I invest in bonds for retirement income?**  
Long-term bonds are risky in a 3.9% yield environment. Instead, use short-term Treasuries (1–3 years) or TIPS for inflation protection. ETFs like SHV (3.8% yield, 1.8-year duration) offer safety and modest income.

### **3. How much should I keep in cash?**  
Hold 6–12 months of expenses in FDIC-insured accounts or money market funds (yield: 3.2–3.6% in 2026). A $500,000 portfolio with $50,000/year spending needs $25,000–$50,000 in cash.

### **4. Is Social Security enough for retirement?**  
No. The average monthly benefit is $1,950 in 2026. With Medicare premiums ($2,041/year), that covers ~$21,000—less than half of typical expenses. Portfolio income is essential.

### **5. How do taxes impact retirement income?**  
Taxes can consume 15–25% of withdrawals if not planned. Use Roth accounts, tax-loss harvesting, and qualified dividends to minimize liability. A $40,000 withdrawal from a traditional IRA at age 67 could cost $6,000+ in taxes if not offset.

### **6. Can I retire at 60 with $1 million in 2026?**  
Yes, but cautiously. At 4% withdrawal: $40,000/year. Add $1,500/month Social Security at 67 → $37,800/year. Total: $77,800. With healthcare and leisure costs, monitor spending closely. A 50% stock allocation (SCHD, VTI) improves success probability.

### **7. What if the market crashes in 2026?**  
Hold 1–2 years of spending in cash/short-term bonds. Avoid selling equities at lows. Dividend cuts are rare among SCHD holdings—only 3 companies cut payouts during 2020.

### **8. How do I adjust for inflation?**  
Build in automatic increases. SCHD raised dividends for 12 consecutive years. KO has paid dividends since 1893 and increased them for 62 straight years. Pair dividends with TIPS or I-bonds (2026 rate: 3.1%).
