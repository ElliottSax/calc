## Dividend Safety Analysis

### Key Metrics for Sustainability

Evaluate dividend safety using these fundamental metrics:

**1. Payout Ratio (Dividends ÷ Earnings)**
- **Sustainable:** 30-60%
- **Moderate risk:** 60-80%
- **High risk:** 80%+

**2. Free Cash Flow Coverage (FCF ÷ Dividends)**
- **Strong:** 1.5x+ coverage
- **Adequate:** 1.2-1.5x coverage
- **Concerning:** Below 1.2x coverage

**3. Debt-to-Equity Ratio**
- **Conservative:** Below 0.5
- **Moderate:** 0.5-1.0
- **Aggressive:** Above 1.0

**4. Dividend Growth Consistency**
- **Excellent:** 10+ consecutive years of increases
- **Good:** 5-10 years of increases
- **Developing:** Less than 5 years

### Red Flags Indicating Risk

Watch for these warning signs threatening dividend sustainability:

- Declining revenue or market share
- Margin compression reducing profitability
- Rising payout ratios approaching 100%
- Increasing debt levels without corresponding asset growth
- Management guidance suggesting dividend reviews
- Industry disruption threatening business model

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What dividend yield is considered good in 2026?

A "good" dividend yield depends on your investment goals and risk tolerance. In 2026's interest rate environment:

- **Conservative investors:** 3.0-5.0% yields from stable, large-cap dividend growers
- **Moderate investors:** 4.0-6.0% yields balancing growth and income
- **Aggressive investors:** 6.0-10.0% yields accepting higher volatility

Beware yields exceeding 10%, which often signal dividend cut risks or fundamental business challenges. Compare yields to 10-year Treasury rates (currently ~4.5%) to assess relative value.

### How many dividend stocks should I own?

Optimal portfolio diversification requires 15-25 individual dividend stocks across multiple sectors. This diversification:

- Reduces company-specific risk from dividend cuts
- Balances sector exposure across economic cycles
- Provides monthly income through staggered payment dates
- Enables tax-loss harvesting opportunities

Investors with less than $50,000 should consider dividend ETFs or mutual funds providing instant diversification. Those with $50,000+ can cost-effectively build individual stock portfolios.

### Should I reinvest dividends or take cash?

**Reinvest dividends if you:**
- Are more than 10 years from retirement
- Have sufficient income from employment
- Want to maximize long-term wealth accumulation
- Can tolerate short-term volatility

**Take cash dividends if you:**
- Are retired or nearing retirement
- Need income to cover living expenses
- Want to rebalance across asset classes
- Seek psychological benefit of regular income

### How are dividend stocks taxed?

Dividend taxation depends on dividend classification and your income level:

**Qualified Dividends** (most U.S. corporation dividends held 60+ days):
- 0% tax rate: Income under $47,025 (single) / $94,050 (married)
- 15% tax rate: Income $47,026-$518,900 (single) / $94,051-$583,750 (married)
- 20% tax rate: Income above $518,900 (single) / $583,750 (married)

**Ordinary Dividends** (REITs, BDCs, MLPs):
- Taxed at your ordinary income tax rate (10-37%)

**Tax-Advantaged Accounts** (IRA, 401k, Roth IRA):
- No annual taxation on dividends
- Roth IRA: Tax-free withdrawals in retirement

Consult a tax professional to optimize your specific situation and consider holding high-yield, ordinary income dividend stocks in tax-advantaged accounts.

### What's the difference between dividend yield and dividend growth?

**Dividend Yield** measures current income as a percentage of stock price:
- Formula: Annual Dividend ÷ Stock Price
- Example: $4 annual dividend ÷ $100 stock price = 4.0% yield

**Dividend Growth** measures annual percentage increase in dividend payments:
- Formula: (New Dividend - Old Dividend) ÷ Old Dividend
- Example: ($1.10 - $1.00) ÷ $1.00 = 10% growth

**Strategic Implications:**

High current yield (6-10%) often correlates with lower dividend growth (0-5%), while low current yield (1-3%) frequently accompanies higher dividend growth (10-15%). Your investment timeline determines optimal balance:

- **Short-term (1-5 years):** Prioritize current yield
- **Medium-term (5-10 years):** Balance yield and growth
- **Long-term (10+ years):** Emphasize dividend growth
