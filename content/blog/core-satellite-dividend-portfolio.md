# Core-Satellite Dividend Portfolio: Optimal Structure Guide

A core-satellite dividend portfolio uses low-cost, broadly diversified dividend ETFs as the stable foundation (the core) and adds individual high-conviction dividend stocks around it (the satellites). The core provides market-level returns with minimal effort. The satellites aim to generate higher income, faster dividend growth, or both.

This structure solves a practical problem that dividend investors face. Owning 30-40 individual dividend stocks requires substantial research and monitoring. Holding only ETFs limits your ability to overweight exceptional companies or avoid the weaker names inside an index. The core-satellite approach combines the strengths of both while minimizing the weaknesses.

Institutional investors have used core-satellite strategies for decades. The approach works equally well for individual dividend portfolios ranging from $25,000 to $2 million or more.

## The Core: Your Dividend Foundation

The core typically represents 50-70% of the total portfolio. It consists of 2-4 broad dividend ETFs that provide diversified exposure to hundreds of dividend-paying companies.

### Selecting Core ETFs

The best core holdings share these attributes:

- **Broad diversification**: 100+ holdings across multiple sectors
- **Low expense ratio**: Below 0.10% annually
- **Quality screening**: The index methodology filters for dividend sustainability
- **Liquidity**: High daily trading volume for efficient execution

## The Satellites: High-Conviction Positions

Satellites represent 30-50% of the portfolio and consist of 8-15 individual stocks where you have strong conviction in the company's ability to deliver superior dividend income, growth, or total return compared to the broader market.

### Why Add Satellites

**Higher yield**: Individual stocks like Realty Income (5.2%) or Altria (8.5%) offer yields that no diversified ETF matches.

**Faster growth**: Companies like Broadcom or Visa grow dividends at 15-20% annually, significantly outpacing any ETF's average growth rate.

**Tax efficiency**: You control when to sell individual stocks for tax loss harvesting. ETF distributions are less controllable.

**Sector tilts**: If you believe healthcare will outperform over the next decade, satellites let you overweight that sector beyond what the core ETF provides.

### Satellite Selection Criteria

Each satellite position should have a clear reason for inclusion that the core does not already provide:

1. **Yield satellites**: Yield at least 2x the core ETF's yield
2. **Growth satellites**: Dividend growth rate at least 1.5x the core's average
3. **Conviction satellites**: Strong thesis for outperformance based on competitive position, management quality, or industry dynamics

## Managing the Core-Satellite Portfolio

### Rebalancing Schedule

Review the portfolio quarterly. Rebalance when:
- Any satellite position exceeds 7% of total portfolio
- The core drops below 50% of total portfolio
- Any single sector (across core + satellites) exceeds 25%

### Where to Direct New Contributions

When adding money monthly, follow this priority:
1. First, bring any position below its target back to target.
2. Second, add to the most undervalued satellite position.
3. Third, add to the core ETF that has drifted furthest below target.

This systematic approach prevents emotional over-allocation to recent winners.

### When to Replace a Satellite

Remove a satellite when:
- The dividend is cut or frozen for two or more years
- The original thesis has been invalidated by fundamental business changes
- A better opportunity exists that serves the same portfolio role
- The position has appreciated so much that its yield is no longer meaningful

### When to Adjust the Core

Core ETFs rarely need replacing. Consider changes only when:
- A significantly cheaper or better-constructed ETF becomes available
- Your income needs shift (e.g., transitioning from growth core to income core at retirement)
- Tax loss harvesting: swap VIG for DGRO or SCHD for VYM temporarily to realize losses

## Tax Optimization

**Hold satellites in taxable accounts**: Individual stocks allow precise tax loss harvesting and control over realized gains.

**Hold high-yield core and satellites in IRAs**: SCHD, VYM, and yield satellites like REITs and MLPs generate income that is better sheltered in tax-advantaged accounts.

**Hold growth satellites in Roth IRAs**: Positions with the highest expected appreciation benefit most from tax-free growth.

## Key Takeaways

- The core-satellite approach uses 2-4 dividend ETFs (50-70% of portfolio) for broad diversification and 8-15 individual stocks (30-50%) for targeted income, growth, or conviction.
- Core ETFs like SCHD, VIG, and VIGI provide market-level dividend returns with minimal research requirements.
- Satellites should each serve a specific purpose: higher yield, faster growth, or superior total return potential compared to the core.
- A $100,000 core-satellite portfolio can generate over $3,000 annually with a blended growth rate exceeding 8%, reaching nearly $7,000 in annual income within a decade.
- Quarterly rebalancing, systematic contribution allocation, and tax-aware account placement maximize the strategy's effectiveness.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What percentage should the core be?

For most investors, the core should represent 50-70% of the total portfolio. New investors or those who prefer lower maintenance should lean toward 70% core. Experienced investors comfortable with individual stock analysis can reduce the core to 50%. Below 40% core, the benefits of the strategy diminish since you are essentially running an individual stock portfolio with a small ETF position.

### Can I start with just the core and add satellites later?

Absolutely, and this is actually the recommended approach for beginners. Start with 100% core ETFs, learn how dividend investing works, and gradually add satellite positions as you develop conviction in individual companies. Many investors take 1-2 years before adding their first satellite.

### How do I avoid overlap between core ETFs and satellite stocks?

Some overlap is inevitable and acceptable. If you hold SCHD (which includes Home Depot) and also hold Home Depot as a satellite, you simply have a larger effective position in that stock. Track your total exposure to each company across core and satellites. If combined exposure exceeds 5%, either reduce the satellite or accept the concentration consciously.

### Should international exposure be in the core or satellites?

International dividend exposure works best in the core through ETFs like VIGI or SCHY. International individual stock investing adds complexity through currency risk, foreign tax withholding, and different accounting standards. Unless you have specific expertise in foreign markets, keeping international exposure in the core simplifies management significantly.

### How does this strategy perform in bear markets?

The core provides downside protection through diversification, as broad dividend ETFs typically decline less than the overall market during corrections. Satellites may experience more volatility, but this creates opportunities to add to high-conviction positions at lower prices. The income from both core and satellites continues flowing during downturns, providing capital for reinvestment or living expenses regardless of price movements.
