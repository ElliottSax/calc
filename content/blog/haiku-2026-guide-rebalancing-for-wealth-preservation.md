## Introduction

Wealth preservation—protecting accumulated assets while maintaining modest growth to offset inflation—requires fundamentally different rebalancing strategies than accumulation portfolios. For retirees, high-net-worth individuals, and those with $5M+ portfolios, rebalancing serves primarily to reduce volatility and sequence risk while maintaining capital in perpetuity.

This guide explores rebalancing specifically for wealth preservation across 30+ year retirement horizons.

## Wealth Preservation Rebalancing Principles

### The "Core-Stability" Framework

Wealth preservation portfolios use rebalancing to maintain tight risk controls:

**Formula: Volatility Control Index (VCI)**
```
VCI = Annual Maximum Drawdown ÷ Annual Return
```

Target VCI: 1.5 or lower (for every 1% annual return, accept max 1.5% drawdown)

**Example allocation:**

| Asset Class | Allocation | Annual Return | Volatility | Contribution to VCI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dividend stocks | 40% | 7% | 12% | 4.8% |
| Bonds | 40% | 4% | 5% | 2% |
| REITs | 15% | 6% | 10% | 1.5% |
| Cash/Equiv | 5% | 3% | 0% | 0% |
| Blended | 100% | 5.3% | 7.2% | **VCI: 1.36** ✓ |

This allocation targets 5.3% annual return with only 7.2% volatility (exceeds preservation goal).

## Conservative Rebalancing Schedule

### Annual Rebalancing (January Focus)

For wealth preservation, annual rebalancing is sufficient:

**January Rebalancing Process:**

1. **Document year-end positions** (Dec 31 close)
   - Total portfolio value
   - Each position percentage of total
   - Dividend/interest received

2. **Calculate target allocations**
   - Example: 40% stocks, 40% bonds, 15% REITs, 5% cash
   - Determine exact dollar amounts needed

3. **Execute rebalancing trades**
   - Sell positions exceeding target by >3%
   - Buy positions below target by >3%
   - Minimize trading (avoid <3% drift)

4. **Harvest losses if available**
   - Identify positions with losses
   - Sell at loss, buy similar position
   - Document loss for taxes

5. **Set dividend reinvestment instructions**
   - Redirect dividends to underweight positions
   - Maintain allocation throughout year

**Example January Rebalancing:**

Year-end portfolio: $5,000,000

Target allocation:
- Stocks: $2,000,000 (40%)
- Bonds: $2,000,000 (40%)
- REITs: $750,000 (15%)
- Cash: $250,000 (5%)

Actual year-end:
- Stocks: $2,250,000 (45%, drift +5%)
- Bonds: $1,800,000 (36%, drift -4%)
- REITs: $700,000 (14%, drift -1%)
- Cash: $250,000 (5%, on target)

Rebalancing actions:
1. Sell stocks: $250,000
2. Buy bonds: $200,000
3. Buy REITs: $50,000
4. No cash changes

New allocation:
- Stocks: $2,000,000 (40%) ✓
- Bonds: $2,000,000 (40%) ✓
- REITs: $750,000 (15%) ✓
- Cash: $250,000 (5%) ✓

## Advanced: Rebalancing with Systematic Withdrawals

### Managing Withdrawals Through Rebalancing

For retirees drawing income, rebalancing becomes withdrawal management:

**Instead of selling random positions, rebalance to maintain allocation while withdrawing:**

**Example: $5M portfolio, $200k annual withdrawal (4% rule)**

Year-end allocation (before withdrawal):
- Stocks: $2,000,000
- Bonds: $2,000,000
- REITs: $750,000
- Cash: $250,000

Planned withdrawal: $200,000 over the year ($16,667/month)

Rebalancing + withdrawal strategy:
1. January: Rebalance to targets
2. January-December: Direct all dividends/interest to cash (Q1-Q4)
   - Expected dividend income: $105,000 annually
3. December: Withdraw $200,000 from cash
4. Cash balance: $250,000 + $105,000 - $200,000 = $155,000

Next January: Rebalance to restore allocation

Result: Withdrawals taken entirely from income and rebalancing, never forced to sell appreciated positions during down markets.

## Tax-Efficient Rebalancing for High-Net-Worth Portfolios

### Avoiding Unnecessary Capital Gains Taxes

Wealth preservation portfolios face significant tax consequences from rebalancing. Strategies to minimize:

**Strategy 1: Rebalance within Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
- Keep bonds, lower-yield positions in taxable accounts
- Keep stocks, high-growth in 401k/IRA (non-taxed trading)
- When rebalancing needed, execute within tax-deferred accounts

**Strategy 2: Use Dividend Reinvestment for Rebalancing**
- Direct dividend income to underweight positions
- Over 12 months, passive rebalancing occurs
- Zero capital gains taxes

**Strategy 3: Harvest Losses Opportunistically**
- Only rebalance when losses available
- Sell depreciated positions, buy appreciated
- Losses offset gains

**Example: Tax-efficient rebalancing**

Portfolio: $10M
Tax rate on gains: 20% long-term

Unbalanced portfolio: 45% stocks (should be 40%)
- Forced rebalancing would create $500k gain → $100k taxes

Alternative approach:
- Maintain allocation for 18+ months more
- Let dividend income rebalance passively
- When market corrects, harvest losses
- When forced to rebalance, losses offset gains
- Net tax: $5,000-$10,000 vs. $100,000

Patience in rebalancing saves massive taxes for high-net-worth portfolios.

## Volatility Control Through Rebalancing

### Sequence of Returns Risk Management

For retirees, portfolio volatility during withdrawal years is critical:

**Worst-case scenario (without rebalancing):**
- Year 1: Market down 25%
- Forced to sell stocks to fund $200k withdrawal
- Locked in 25% loss on sale
- Remaining portfolio too small

**With rebalancing:**
- Maintain cash position equal to 1-2 years expenses
- Year 1 market decline: Draw from cash, don't sell stocks
- Year 2: Bonds rebalance to cash via dividend income
- Never forced to sell stocks in down market

This cash/bond cushion created through annual rebalancing is the primary protection against sequence risk.

## Multi-Account Rebalancing Strategy

### Managing Rebalancing Across Taxable, IRA, and 401k Accounts

High-net-worth individuals typically have assets across multiple account types. Rebalance globally while minimizing taxes:

**Recommended structure:**

| Account | Best Asset Classes | Rebalancing |
|---|---|---|
| Roth IRA/401k | High-growth stocks | Annual within account |
| Traditional IRA/401k | Bonds, high-dividend | Annual within account |
| Taxable brokerage | Municipal bonds, tax-efficient ETFs | Annual, harvest losses |
| 529 plans | Age-appropriate funds | Annual, use dividend redirect |

**Global rebalancing:**

Calculate overall portfolio target (40/40/15/5), then allocate across accounts to minimize tax friction:
- Growth stocks → Roth (grow tax-free)
- Bonds → Traditional IRA (ordinary income treatment)
- Dividends → Taxable (qualified treatment)
- REITs → 401k (high ordinary income tax)

This structure minimizes tax drag while maintaining target allocation.

## FAQ Section

### Q: How often should wealthy individuals rebalance?

**A:** Annual rebalancing sufficient for most:
- Below $2M: Annual
- $2-10M: Annual or semi-annual
- Above $10M: Consider quarterly due to volatility magnitude

More frequent rebalancing increases trading costs without additional benefit.

### Q: Should I rebalance before or after large withdrawal?

**A:** Rebalance AFTER withdrawal:
1. Make annual withdrawal
2. Portfolio allocation changes from withdrawal
3. Then rebalance to targets

This avoids trading immediately before withdrawing.

### Q: What's the optimal allocation for wealth preservation?

**A:** Conservative approach:
- 40% dividend stocks (SCHD)
- 40% bonds (BND + municipal)
- 15% REITs (O)
- 5% cash/money market

Target return: 4.5% (covers inflation + modest withdrawal)
Target volatility: 6-8%

### Q: How do taxes impact rebalancing for high earners?

**A:** Significantly:
- $100,000 capital gain at 20% = $20,000 taxes
- Tax loss harvesting can offset gains
- Delay rebalancing 6+ months for long-term treatment
- Use tax-loss harvesting annually

Plan rebalancing quarterly for tax opportunities.

### Q: Should rebalancing frequency change in retirement?

**A:** Yes:
- Pre-retirement: Annual or semi-annual
- Year 1-10 of retirement: Semi-annual (active withdrawals)
- Year 10+ of retirement: Annual (income stabilizes)
- Year 20+: Annual (minimal volatility exposure)

Less active trading in later retirement reduces risk.

## Conclusion

Rebalancing for wealth preservation differs fundamentally from accumulation-focused rebalancing. Rather than maximizing returns, preservation rebalancing minimizes volatility, reduces sequence risk, and creates tax-efficient withdrawal systems that allow portfolios to sustain multi-decade retirements while potentially increasing in absolute value.

Through disciplined annual rebalancing combined with strategic tax-loss harvesting, dividend reinvestment, and multi-account optimization, a $5-10M portfolio can sustain $200,000-$400,000+ annual withdrawals indefinitely while growing in real terms.

Begin today with clear asset allocation targets (40/40/15/5 as baseline), implement annual January rebalancing, direct all income to underweight positions, and harvest losses opportunistically. This disciplined approach will preserve your wealth for decades while funding whatever lifestyle you've achieved.
