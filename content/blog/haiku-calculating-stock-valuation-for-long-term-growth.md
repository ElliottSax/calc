# Calculating Stock Valuation for Long-term Growth

Identifying undervalued stocks with strong dividend growth potential requires understanding valuation mathematics. Long-term investors who grasp these calculations distinguish attractive investments from expensive ones, dramatically improving returns over decades. This guide demonstrates the key valuation methods using real companies.

## Valuation Fundamentals

Stock valuation attempts to determine what a company is truly worth, distinguishing intrinsic value from market price.

**Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E):**

The simplest valuation metric compares stock price to annual earnings.

**P/E Ratio = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share**

**Example (Johnson & Johnson):**
- Stock price: $155
- Earnings per share: $9.82
- P/E ratio: 155 / 9.82 = **15.8x**

A P/E of 15.8 means the market pays $15.80 for every $1 of annual earnings.

**P/E Interpretation:**
- Below 15: Potentially undervalued
- 15-20: Fairly valued
- Above 20: Potentially overvalued
- Healthcare average: 16-18

At 15.8, JNJ trades near historical average, neither cheap nor expensive.

## Dividend Discount Model

For dividend-paying stocks, the dividend discount model (DDM) calculates intrinsic value based on future dividend payments.

**Gordon Growth Model (simplest DDM):**

**Intrinsic Value = D₁ / (r - g)**

Where:
- D₁ = Expected next dividend
- r = Required rate of return (discount rate)
- g = Dividend growth rate

**Real Calculation (Coca-Cola):**

Current Information:
- Current dividend: $2.36 per share
- Expected growth rate: 6% annually (historical average)
- Your required return: 8% (typical for dividend stocks)

Next year's dividend:
- D₁ = $2.36 × 1.06 = $2.50

Intrinsic value:
- Intrinsic Value = $2.50 / (0.08 - 0.06) = $2.50 / 0.02 = **$125**

**Valuation conclusion:** If KO trades below $125, it's undervalued. If above $125, it's overvalued.

Current KO price (~$60): Significantly undervalued under this model.

**Critical note:** The 6% growth rate estimate heavily influences results. Different growth assumptions produce different valuations.

**Sensitivity Analysis (KO):**

| Growth Assumption | Intrinsic Value |
|------------------|-----------------|
| 3% growth | $78 |
| 4% growth | $83 |
| 5% growth | $92 |
| 6% growth | $125 |
| 7% growth | $188 |
| 8% growth | $313 |

A 5% difference in growth assumption creates $235 valuation range. This demonstrates why precise forecasting matters.

## Price-to-Earnings Ratio Valuation Method

The P/E approach compares valuation to historical norms.

**Fair Value Calculation:**

**Fair Value = Historical Average P/E × Current EPS**

**Example (Procter & Gamble):**

Historical data:
- P/G average P/E ratio: 25x (last 10 years)
- Current earnings per share: $6.42

Fair value calculation:
- Fair Value = 25 × $6.42 = **$160.50**

Current price: $165
Valuation: Trading 3% above fair value (slightly overvalued)

This method assumes the historical P/E ratio represents fair valuation.

## Free Cash Flow Valuation

For growth analysis, free cash flow (FCF) represents the cash companies can distribute to shareholders.

**Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures**

**Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Valuation:**

Projects future free cash flows and discounts them to present value.

**Example (Apple):**

Projected Free Cash Flow:
- Year 1: $95 billion
- Year 2: $98 billion
- Year 3: $102 billion
- Year 4: $106 billion
- Year 5: $110 billion
- Terminal growth rate: 3%

**Discount Rate:** 8% (weighted average cost of capital)

**DCF Calculation:**

Present value of projected cash flows:
- Year 1: $95B / 1.08 = $88.0B
- Year 2: $98B / (1.08²) = $84.0B
- Year 3: $102B / (1.08³) = $81.0B
- Year 4: $106B / (1.08⁴) = $77.9B
- Year 5: $110B / (1.08⁵) = $74.8B

Terminal value (Year 5 onward):
- Year 6 FCF: $110B × 1.03 = $113.3B
- Terminal value: $113.3B / (0.08 - 0.03) = $2,266B
- PV of terminal: $2,266B / (1.08⁵) = $1,541B

Total enterprise value: $88.0 + 84.0 + 81.0 + 77.9 + 74.8 + 1,541 = **$1,946.7B**

Apple shares outstanding: 16.4 billion
Value per share: $1,946.7B / 16.4B = **$119 per share**

Current Apple price (~$230): Significantly overvalued under this model.

This demonstrates why valuation approaches require updating as business conditions change.

## Price-to-Book Ratio

Book value represents total assets minus liabilities per share.

**Price-to-Book = Stock Price / Book Value Per Share**

**Example (PG):**
- Stock price: $165
- Book value per share: $8.50
- P/B ratio: 165 / 8.50 = **19.4x**

A P/B of 19.4 means the market values the company at nearly 20 times its book value. For mature companies with strong brands (PG, JNJ), high P/B ratios are normal.

**P/B Interpretation by Industry:**
- Technology: 5-15x typical
- Healthcare: 8-12x typical
- Consumer staples: 8-15x typical
- Financials: 1-3x typical
- Energy: 1-2x typical

## PEG Ratio (P/E to Growth)

The PEG ratio accounts for earnings growth rates.

**PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio / Earnings Growth Rate**

**Example:**
- Stock A: P/E 20x, growth 20%
  - PEG = 20 / 20 = 1.0
- Stock B: P/E 15x, growth 5%
  - PEG = 15 / 5 = 3.0

Stock A appears expensive (20x P/E) but grows faster, delivering lower PEG. Stock B appears cheap (15x P/E) but grows slowly, delivering higher PEG.

**PEG Interpretation:**
- Below 1.0: Undervalued relative to growth
- 1.0-2.0: Fairly valued
- Above 2.0: Overvalued relative to growth

Lower PEG ratios identify growth stocks trading at reasonable valuations.

## Valuation for Long-term Dividend Growth

Specifically for dividend growth investors, focus valuation on:

1. **Current yield vs. Treasury yield**
   - Stock should offer at least 1-2% premium over Treasuries

2. **Dividend growth rate sustainability**
   - Payout ratio should be below 60-70%
   - Company should grow earnings 5%+ annually

3. **10-year dividend history**
   - Consistent increases demonstrate commitment
   - 10+ years of increases signals "dividend aristocrat"

4. **Competitive advantage**
   - Strong brand protection (KO, PG, JNJ)
   - Pricing power
   - Market-leading position

Dividend: $2.36 (3.9% yield)
10-year Treasury: 4.2%

Issues:

- Could wait for better entry at $55 (4.3% yield)
- Or invest gradually (dollar-cost averaging)

62-year dividend growth record

Assessment:
- Yield slightly below Treasury but extremely consistent

- Fair value at current price for safety + growth combination
- Suitable for long-term core position

## FAQ

**Q: Which valuation method matters most?**
A: For dividend stocks, combine dividend discount model with P/E analysis. For growth stocks, use DCF and PEG. For value stocks, use P/B and P/S. No single method is perfect; triangulation improves accuracy.

**Q: How often should valuations be recalculated?**
A: Quarterly when companies report earnings. Annually for long-term holdings. Only act on valuation changes if fundamentals shift significantly (20%+ revaluation).

**Q: Should I wait for perfect valuation?**
A: No. Dollar-cost averaging (investing regular amounts) produces better results than timing perfect entries. Invest when valuation is reasonable (within 10-15% of fair value).

**Q: Can valuation predict stock price?**
A: Not in short-term. Price frequently diverges from valuation. Long-term (5+ years), valuations eventually drive prices. Patience is essential.

**Q: What discount rate should I use?**
A: Conservative investors: 8-10%. Moderate: 7-8%. Aggressive: 6-7%. Use your required return rate—what you believe reasonable for the risk taken.

## Conclusion

Stock valuation requires understanding multiple calculation methods—dividend discount models, price-to-earnings, free cash flow, and relative comparisons. No single method perfectly predicts future price; the best approach triangulates across methods.

For dividend growth investors specifically, focus on companies where:
- Current yield exceeds Treasury bonds
- Payout ratio permits continued dividend growth
- Company has 10+ years of consistent increases
- Competitive advantages protect market position

Using the valuation frameworks in this guide, you identify attractive entry points, avoid overpaying for growth, and construct a portfolio of reasonably-valued dividend growth companies. Combined with patience and reinvestment, proper valuation discipline creates wealth across market cycles.
