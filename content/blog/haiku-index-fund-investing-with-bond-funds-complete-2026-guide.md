## Understanding Bond Fund Basics

Bond funds hold multiple bonds, providing diversification and professional management. The Vanguard Total Bond Market ETF (BND) holds approximately 8,000 bonds across government, corporate, and mortgage securities, delivering reliable income with minimal price volatility.

BND's 4.2% current yield provides quarterly distributions while the fund's value remains relatively stable. This contrasts with equity index funds, where price appreciation drives returns.

## Asset Allocation Framework

A 30-year-old investor with $100,000 typically allocates $90,000 to VTI and $10,000 to BND.

## The 60/40 Portfolio Traditional Model

The classic 60% stock / 40% bond allocation has proven robust across market conditions:

| Period | Stock Return | Bond Return | Blended Return | Volatility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Peaceful growth | 10% | 4% | 7.6% | 9.8% |
| Mild recession | -5% | 2% | -1.8% | 8.2% |
| Severe crash | -30% | 5% | -13% | 18.4% |
| Strong recovery | 35% | 1% | 21.2% | 22.1% |

Bonds cushion portfolio declines during crises. When stocks fall 30%, bonds typically gain 5%, reducing total loss to -13% instead of -30%.

## How Interest Rates Affect Bond Values

Bond prices move inversely to interest rates:

**$100,000 Bond Fund at 4.2% yield:**

When interest rates rise to 5.5%:
- BND price falls 6-8% to yield 5.5%
- $100,000 investment now worth $94,000

When interest rates fall to 3.0%:
- BND price rises 7-8%
- $100,000 investment now worth $106,000

This inverse relationship means bonds provide portfolio insurance when stock crash fears drive interest rate cuts—typical recession pattern.

## Duration: Measuring Interest Rate Sensitivity

Duration measures bond price sensitivity: Price Change = -Duration x Rate Change (%)

BND duration: 5.8 years

Scenario: Rates rise 1%
- Price change: -5.8%
- $100,000 position: Falls to $94,200

Scenario: Rates fall 1%
- Price change: +5.8%
- $100,000 position: Rises to $105,800

Higher duration = greater interest rate sensitivity.

## The Mathematics of Rebalancing

Disciplined rebalancing forces buying low and selling high:

**60/40 Portfolio Starting at $100,000:**
- Stocks (60%): $60,000
- Bonds (40%): $40,000

After one year of strong stock returns:
- Stocks: $70,000
- Bonds: $30,000
- Target: $60,000/$40,000
- Rebalance: Sell $10,000 stocks, buy $10,000 bonds

This forced selling of appreciated stocks reduces portfolio risk systematically. Studies show rebalancing adds 0.3-0.5% annual returns through contrarian discipline.

## Inflation and Bond Challenges

Bonds struggle during inflation:
- 1970s: Bonds 6.4%, inflation 7.1% (negative real returns)
- 2000s: Bonds 5.1%, inflation 2.7% (positive real returns)
- 2022-2025: Bonds 2.3%, inflation 3.1% (negative real returns)

This argues for maintaining higher stock allocation during inflation or holding inflation-protected bonds (SCHP, TIP).

## Tactical Bond Timing

Tempting logic suggests timing bond allocations when rates are high. However, timing interest rates is as difficult as timing stocks. Missing the best 10 months reduces returns by 160 basis points annually.

Static allocation beats tactical timing with bonds consistently.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Should I wait for higher interest rates before buying bonds?**
A: No. When rates rise, bond prices fall, but you dollar-cost average into higher yields.

**Q2: Are bond funds safer than individual bonds?**
A: Yes. Individual bonds require $5,000+ per position, while BND owns 8,000+ with $50+ minimum.

**Q3: Can I use bonds to hedge a stock crash?**
A: Partially. Bonds cushion 30-50% of crashes historically.

**Q4: Should I use Treasury bonds or corporate bond funds?**
A: Corporate bonds yield 60+ basis points more. Blending both works well.

**Q5: Is a 50/50 stock-bond portfolio too conservative for a 40-year-old?**
A: Probably. At 40, a 70/30 or 75/25 allocation is more appropriate with 25+ years until retirement.
