# How to Use Capital Gains in 2026

Capital gains tax is one of the largest expenses most investors face yet spend minimal effort optimizing. A $100,000 profit sounds identical whether taxed at 15% (federal long-term rate) or 37% (short-term rate), but the 22% difference is $22,000. This guide explains capital gains taxation in 2026, timing strategies, and techniques to minimize your tax liability.

## Understanding Capital Gains

A capital gain occurs when you sell an investment for more than you paid. If you purchased VTI at $150 and sold at $200, your capital gain is $50 per share.

**Capital Gain = Sale Price - Purchase Price**

Capital gains come in two categories:

**Short-Term Capital Gains:**
- Assets held 12 months or less
- Taxed as ordinary income (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37%)
- Triggered when you sell appreciated holdings too quickly

**Long-Term Capital Gains:**
- Assets held more than 12 months
- Preferential tax rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)
- For most investors, 15% is the applicable long-term rate

The tax difference is substantial. A $100,000 profit:
- Short-term (24% bracket): $24,000 federal tax
- Long-term (15% bracket): $15,000 federal tax
- Difference: $9,000

This demonstrates why holding period matters enormously.

## 2026 Tax Brackets and Capital Gains Rates

In 2026, federal long-term capital gains rates are:

| Taxable Income (Single) | Taxable Income (Married) | Capital Gains Rate |
|------------------------|------------------------|--------------------|
| $0 - $47,025 | $0 - $94,050 | 0% |
| $47,025 - $518,900 | $94,050 - $583,750 | 15% |
| $518,900+ | $583,750+ | 20% |

Most Americans fall into the 15% bracket. However, high earners in the 20% bracket or those with modest income in the 0% bracket can strategically manage gains.

**State Capital Gains Tax:** Many states tax capital gains as ordinary income (California, New York, Massachusetts at 10-13%). Some states have no capital gains tax (Florida, Texas, Washington). Check your state's rules—they significantly impact net tax.

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Strategy

The 12-month holding period determines capital gains treatment. Here's a concrete example:

**Scenario A: Buy and Hold (Long-Term)**
- January 2025: Buy 100 shares of VOO at $400 = $40,000
- March 2026: Sell at $470 = $47,000
- Capital gain: $7,000
- Holding period: 14 months (long-term)
- Federal tax (15% bracket): $1,050
- Net profit after tax: $5,950

**Scenario B: Quick Sale (Short-Term)**
- January 2025: Buy 100 shares of VOO at $400 = $40,000
- May 2025: Sell at $470 = $47,000
- Capital gain: $7,000
- Holding period: 4 months (short-term)
- Federal tax (24% bracket): $1,680
- Net profit after tax: $5,320

By holding slightly longer, you pay $630 less in federal taxes on a $7,000 gain—a 9% tax savings from timing.

## Sell Losers, Keep Winners Strategy

Tax-loss harvesting is using investment losses to offset gains. When a holding declines, selling it realizes the loss, which offsets capital gains elsewhere.

**Example:**
- Gain in VOO: $10,000 (sell at profit)
- Loss in BND (bonds): $3,000 (sell at loss)
- Net capital gain: $10,000 - $3,000 = $7,000
- Federal tax (15% bracket): $1,050

Without the $3,000 loss harvested:
- Capital gain: $10,000
- Federal tax (15% bracket): $1,500

Tax-loss harvesting saves $450 in this scenario.

**Wash Sale Rule:** Be careful. If you sell a security at a loss, you cannot repurchase the same or substantially identical security within 30 days (before or after). The loss is disallowed. Instead, buy a similar but different security. Sold VTI at a loss? Buy VOO (different fund, similar holdings) instead.

## Timing Sales for Tax Efficiency

Strategically time sales to maximize gains in low-income years.

**Example: Physician Taking a Sabbatical**
- Normal year income: $300,000 (marginal tax bracket: 24%)
- Sabbatical year income: $50,000 (marginal tax bracket: 12%)

Realizing $100,000 in capital gains:
- During normal income year: $100,000 × 15% = $15,000 federal tax
- During sabbatical year: $100,000 × 15% = $15,000 federal tax (long-term rate applies regardless)

However, short-term gains fall into ordinary brackets:
- Realize $100,000 short-term gain during normal year: $100,000 × 24% = $24,000
- Realize $100,000 short-term gain during sabbatical year: $100,000 × 12% = $12,000

By timing short-term gains during low-income years, you reduce taxes by $12,000.

## Strategic Gains Realization in Retirement

Once retired, your income often drops dramatically, potentially moving you into lower capital gains brackets.

**Example: Retiree with $40,000 annual income**

In the 15% capital gains bracket, you have room to realize additional gains before moving to the 20% bracket:

- Income threshold for 0% bracket: $47,025 (single)
- Your income: $40,000
- Room for gains at 0% bracket: $7,025

You can realize $7,025 in capital gains with zero federal tax. Beyond that, you're in the 15% bracket.

For a retiree with significant unrealized gains, realizing gains during retirement's low-income years provides substantial tax savings.

## New Asset Basis: Step-Up at Death

One of the most valuable tax provisions is the step-up in basis at death. When you inherit appreciated securities, your cost basis becomes the fair market value at the date of death—not the original purchase price.

**Example:**
- Purchased VTI for $50,000 in 1990
- Current value: $500,000
- Unrealized gain: $450,000
- You pass away (or sell to family)
- Heir's cost basis: $500,000 (stepped-up from $50,000)
- Heir sells immediately: $500,000 (zero gain, zero tax)

The $450,000 gain vanishes through the step-up. This is why high-net-worth individuals often hold appreciated securities through death rather than selling—the tax burden disappears for heirs.

**Planning Implication:** If you plan to leave appreciated securities to heirs, hold them until death. The stepped-up basis eliminates the gains tax entirely. This is particularly valuable for real estate, concentrated stock positions, or highly appreciated investments.

## Donation of Appreciated Securities

Donating appreciated securities to charity provides a double tax benefit:

1. Charitable deduction for fair market value
2. Zero capital gains tax on the appreciation

**Example:**
- Purchased SCHD for $50,000
- Current value: $100,000
- Unrealized gain: $50,000
- Donate to charity (via donor-advised fund or directly)

Benefits:
- Charitable deduction: $100,000 (reduces taxable income by $100,000)
- Capital gains tax: $0 (gain is never realized)
- Tax savings (at 24% marginal rate, 15% capital gains rate): $24,000 (income tax) + $7,500 (avoided capital gains) = $31,500

If you sold the shares, paid tax ($7,500), donated cash ($92,500), and took a deduction:
- Deduction: $92,500
- Avoided capital gains: $0
- Tax savings: $22,200

Donating appreciated securities saves $9,300 compared to selling and donating proceeds.

## Specific Identification vs. FIFO

When you sell only some shares of a holding, choose which specific shares to sell. The IRS allows three methods:

**Method 1: First-In, First-Out (FIFO)**
Sell oldest shares first. Simple but often produces large gains because older shares have lower cost basis.

**Method 2: Last-In, First-Out (LIFO)**
Sell newest shares first. Reduces gains (newer purchases have higher cost basis).

**Method 3: Specific Identification**
Specify exactly which shares you're selling. Most tax-efficient if you maintain detailed records.

**Example:**
You own 100 shares of KO (Coca-Cola):
- 25 shares purchased in 2015 at $40 (cost basis $1,000)
- 25 shares purchased in 2018 at $50 (cost basis $1,250)
- 25 shares purchased in 2021 at $55 (cost basis $1,375)
- 25 shares purchased in 2024 at $65 (cost basis $1,625)
- Current price: $70 per share

Selling 50 shares at $70:

**Using FIFO:** Sell oldest 50 shares (2015 and 2018)
- Cost basis: $1,000 + $1,250 = $2,250
- Proceeds: $3,500
- Gain: $1,250
- Tax (15%): $188

**Using Specific Identification:** Sell 25 new shares (2024) + 25 newest others (2021)
- Cost basis: $1,625 + $1,375 = $3,000
- Proceeds: $3,500
- Gain: $500
- Tax (15%): $75

Specific identification saves $113 in taxes by choosing shares with higher cost basis.

## Capital Gains Harvesting (Opposite of Tax-Loss Harvesting)

In some cases, deliberately realizing gains provides long-term benefits. This applies when:

1. You have substantial losses to offset
2. You're in a low-income year
3. Your heirs would receive a step-up basis

**Example:**
You have:
- $50,000 in unrealized losses (harvested)
- $30,000 in unrealized gains (not harvested)
- Income: $70,000 (15% capital gains bracket)

Realize the $30,000 gain:
- Net capital gain: $30,000 - $50,000 = $20,000 loss
- Offset ordinary income: $3,000 (maximum annual carryover)
- Remaining loss carryforward: $17,000

Tax savings immediately: $3,000 × 22% (marginal rate) = $660

Plus, you now have a $30,000 step-up in basis in those securities, providing future flexibility.

## FAQ Section

**Q: What's the difference between capital gains and ordinary income?**
A: Capital gains are profits from selling investments. Long-term capital gains (held >12 months) are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%. Ordinary income (wages, interest, short-term gains) is taxed at 10-37%. Most investors benefit significantly from prioritizing long-term capital gains.

**Q: Can I deduct capital losses against ordinary income?**
A: Yes, up to $3,000 annually. Excess losses carry forward indefinitely until exhausted. This is why tax-loss harvesting is valuable—it generates deductions.

**Q: Should I sell winners or losers first?**
A: Sell losers to realize tax deductions. Hold winners to defer taxes and benefit from the step-up in basis at death. This is "sell losers, keep winners."

**Q: How do I know my holding period?**
A: It's measured from the purchase date. Purchased March 15, 2025, and selling March 16, 2026 is long-term (more than 12 months). Purchased March 15, 2025, and selling March 15, 2026 is short-term (exactly 12 months).

**Q: What if I inherited a security—what's my cost basis?**
A: The fair market value at the date of death (or six months later if the estate elects alternate valuation date). This is the stepped-up basis. You pay zero tax on any appreciation between the donor's purchase price and their death.

**Q: Is there a capital gains tax on dividends?**
A: Not directly. Dividends are separate from capital gains. Qualified dividends receive the same favorable tax rates (0%, 15%, 20%), but they're reported separately on tax forms.

## Conclusion

Capital gains taxation is complex but manageable with strategy. The spread between short-term and long-term rates (24% vs. 15%) is worth $9,000 per $100,000 in gains. Hold appreciated investments over 12 months to access preferential rates. Use tax-loss harvesting to offset gains. Realize gains during low-income years. Donate appreciated securities rather than cash. Maintain detailed records for specific identification. And plan for the step-up in basis—your heirs will benefit enormously. Together, these strategies transform capital gains taxation from a drag on returns into a manageable cost.
