## What Are Capital Gains?

A **capital gain** occurs when you sell an investment for more than you paid for it. The difference between the sale price and the purchase price (also known as the *cost basis*) is your capital gain.

- **Formula**:  
  `Capital Gain = Sale Price – Cost Basis`

For example:
- You buy 10 shares of **Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)** at $150 per share.
- You later sell them at $180 per share.
- Your capital gain:  
  `($180 – $150) × 10 = $300`

Capital gains only become taxable when you **sell** the asset. Until then, they are *unrealized*.

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capital Gains

The IRS categorizes capital gains based on **how long you hold the asset** before selling it. This distinction is critical because it directly affects your tax rate.

| Holding Period       | Gain Type               | Tax Rate (2024, Single Filers) |
|----------------------|-------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 1 year or less       | Short-Term Capital Gain | Ordinary income tax rates (10%–37%) |
| More than 1 year     | Long-Term Capital Gain  | 0%, 15%, or 20% (based on income) |

### Example: Short-Term vs. Long-Term Tax Impact

Suppose you made a $10,000 capital gain from selling shares of **VOO (S&P 500 ETF)**.

| Scenario           | Holding Period | Tax Rate (If in 24% bracket) | Tax Owed |
|--------------------|----------------|-------------------------------|----------|
| Short-Term         | 11 months      | 24%                           | $2,400   |
| Long-Term          | 13 months      | 15%                           | $1,500   |

**Savings: $900** just by waiting one extra month.

This is one of the fastest ways to reduce tax exposure: **hold assets longer than one year**.

## How Capital Gains Are Triggered

Many investors assume only stock sales create capital gains. But gains can also arise from:

- Selling ETFs or mutual funds
- Selling individual stocks
- Receiving a capital gains distribution from a fund
- Exchanging one investment for another (even in a taxable account)

## Cost Basis: The Foundation of Gains Calculation

Your **cost basis** is what you originally paid for an investment, including commissions and fees.

### Adjusted Cost Basis

Certain events adjust your cost basis:
- Reinvested dividends
- Stock splits
- Return of capital distributions

**Example: KO (Coca-Cola)**

- Buy 100 shares of KO at $60/share:  
  Cost basis = $6,000
- Reinvest $200 in dividends over 3 years
- New cost basis = $6,200
- Sell at $75/share:  
  `($75 × 100) – $6,200 = $1,300 capital gain`

Without adjusting for reinvested dividends, you’d overstate your gain by $200.

## Strategies to Minimize Capital Gains Fast

You don’t need complex tax planning to reduce capital gains. Use these **fast, actionable strategies**:

### 1. Hold for More Than One Year

As shown earlier, the difference between 24% and 15% taxes is substantial.

**Fast Tip**: Mark your calendar. If a stock is approaching the 1-year mark, consider waiting.

### 2. Use Tax-Loss Harvesting

Offset capital gains with capital losses.

- Sell a losing position → book the loss → use it to reduce taxable gains.
- Can offset up to $3,000 in ordinary income if losses exceed gains.

**Example**:
- Gain: $10,000 from selling SCHD
- Loss: $4,000 from selling a tech stock
- Taxable gain: $6,000
- Tax saved: $600 (at 15%)

### 3. Invest in Tax-Efficient Funds

> Lower turnover = fewer trades = fewer capital gains passed to shareholders.

### 4. Donate Appreciated Stock to Charity

Instead of selling stock and donating cash:
- Donate the stock directly
- Avoid capital gains tax
- Claim fair market value as deduction

**Example**:
- Own 100 shares of KO worth $60/share ($6,000 total)

You save **$450 in taxes** (at 15%) and support a cause.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger Unnecessary Gains

Avoid these fast-triggers of unwanted capital gains:

### 1. Selling Too Soon

Selling after 11 months converts a long-term gain into short-term—costing you up to 12% extra in taxes.

**Fix**: Set calendar alerts for 11-month holdings.

### 2. Ignoring Cost Basis

Using the wrong cost basis (e.g., ignoring reinvested dividends) leads to **overpaying taxes**.

**Fix**: Use your brokerage’s **average cost basis** or **specific ID** method.

### 3. Chasing Performance

Frequently trading ETFs like VTI or VOO creates short-term gains.

**Data**: A 2023 study showed that active traders paid **3.2x more in capital gains taxes** than buy-and-hold investors.

## FAQ: Capital Gains Fast

### Q1: Do I owe capital gains tax if I reinvest the proceeds?

**Yes**. Selling triggers the gain, regardless of whether you reinvest or take cash.

> Selling $10,000 of SCHD and buying VOO = $10,000 gain taxed.

### Q2: Are capital gains taxes owed every year?

Only if you **sell** or receive a **capital gains distribution** from a fund.

> Holding VOO without selling? No tax—unless VOO distributes gains.

### Q3: What if I sell at a loss?

You can use **capital losses** to offset gains.

- $5,000 loss + $3,000 gain = $0 taxable gain, $2,000 loss carried forward.
- Up to $3,000 of losses can offset ordinary income annually.

### Q4: How do I report capital gains?

Use **Form 8949** and **Schedule D** on your tax return.

- Brokers send **Form 1099-B** with cost basis and sale details.
- Verify accuracy—errors are common.

### Q5: Can I avoid capital gains by moving to another country?

Some countries don’t tax capital gains (e.g., UAE), but U.S. citizens are taxed on worldwide income.

> Renouncing citizenship has serious consequences—consult a tax attorney.

### Q6: Are gains from ETFs different from individual stocks?

Tax treatment is the same: based on holding period and gain amount.

> But ETFs like VOO have **lower turnover**, making them more tax-efficient than actively managed funds.

### Q7: What’s the wash sale rule?

If you sell a stock at a loss and buy a “substantially identical” stock within 30 days, the loss is disallowed.

> Applies to **taxable accounts only**, not IRAs.

### Q8: Do dividends count as capital gains?

No. Dividends are **income**, taxed separately.

- Qualified dividends: 0%, 15%, or 20% (same as long-term gains)
- Non-qualified: taxed as ordinary income

> JNJ and KO pay qualified dividends if held 60+ days.
