# The Ultimate Guide to Stock Valuation using Tools

Stock valuation determines whether a company's shares represent fair value, are undervalued, or trade at a premium. Professional investors use multiple valuation methodologies to identify opportunities where price diverges from intrinsic value. This comprehensive guide teaches you how to apply valuation tools like a seasoned analyst.

## What Makes Stock Valuation Critical?

Valuation answers one fundamental question: What is this stock worth? Price and value are not the same. A stock might trade at $150 while its intrinsic value is $120 (overvalued) or $200 (undervalued). Valuation tools help you identify these discrepancies before committing capital.

Every dollar you invest has an opportunity cost—it could work elsewhere. Valuation ensures you allocate capital to investments where the price-to-value ratio justifies the risk taken.

## The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

The P/E ratio compares a company's stock price to its earnings per share. It answers: How much am I paying for each dollar of company earnings?

**Formula:**
**P/E Ratio = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)**

### Real-World Example: Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)

Assume JNJ's current metrics:
- Stock price: $165.50
- Annual earnings per share: $6.42
- P/E ratio: $165.50 / $6.42 = 25.74x

This means investors pay $25.74 for every $1 of annual earnings.

**Comparative Analysis:**

| Company | Stock Price | EPS | P/E Ratio | Valuation Assessment |
|---------|------------|-----|----------|----------------------|
| JNJ | $165.50 | $6.42 | 25.74x | Fair (Healthcare median: 25-28x) |
| Coca-Cola (KO) | $71.25 | $2.28 | 31.25x | Premium (Mature company) |
| Procter & Gamble (PG) | $168.40 | $5.12 | 32.89x | Premium (Dividend growth stock) |
| Apple (AAPL) | $235.80 | $6.27 | 37.62x | Premium (Tech sector) |

**PEG Ratio (P/E to Growth):**

The PEG ratio refines P/E analysis by incorporating growth expectations:

**PEG = P/E Ratio / Expected Annual Growth Rate (%)**

If JNJ trades at 25.74x earnings with expected 5% annual growth:
- PEG = 25.74 / 5.0 = 5.15

A PEG below 1.0 suggests undervaluation relative to growth prospects. A PEG above 2.0 suggests the market prices in very aggressive future growth.

## Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

P/B ratio compares market price to book value (assets minus liabilities). It's particularly useful for asset-heavy industries.

**Formula:**
**P/B = Stock Price / Book Value Per Share**

**Example: Analyzing a Dividend Stock**

Consider a mature utility company with:
- Stock price: $42.00
- Book value per share: $28.50
- P/B ratio: $42.00 / $28.50 = 1.47x

This stock trades at 1.47 times its accounting book value. For regulated utilities (expected P/B: 1.2-1.8x), this suggests fair valuation.

## Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio

P/S ratio compares market capitalization to total revenue. Unlike earnings-based metrics, it resists accounting manipulation.

**Formula:**
**P/S = Stock Price / Revenue Per Share**

If a stock trades at $100 with revenue per share of $8:
- P/S = $100 / $8 = 12.5x

Investors pay $12.50 for every $1 of annual revenue. Consumer staples typically trade at 2-4x sales, while high-growth tech trades at 5-15x sales.

## Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

DCF represents the gold standard for valuation. It projects future cash flows and discounts them to present value using a discount rate reflecting risk and opportunity cost.

**DCF Formula:**
**Enterprise Value = Σ [Cash Flow(n) / (1 + r)^n]**

Where:
- Cash Flow(n) = Expected cash flow in year n
- r = Discount rate (typically WACC)
- n = Year number

### Comprehensive DCF Example

**Target: Procter & Gamble (PG)**

**Assumptions:**
- Current free cash flow: $9.5 billion annually
- Growth rate (years 1-5): 4% annually
- Terminal growth rate: 2.5%
- Discount rate (WACC): 7.5%
- Outstanding shares: 2.2 billion

**5-Year Projections:**

| Year | Free Cash Flow | Discount Rate | Present Value |
|------|----------------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | $9,880M | 1.075 | $9,188M |
| 2 | $10,275M | 1.156 | $8,893M |
| 3 | $10,686M | 1.242 | $8,608M |
| 4 | $11,113M | 1.335 | $8,326M |
| 5 | $11,558M | 1.435 | $8,052M |

**Terminal Value Calculation:**

Year 5 free cash flow × (1 + terminal growth) / (discount rate - terminal growth)

= $11,558M × 1.025 / (0.075 - 0.025)
= $11,847M / 0.05
= $236,940M

Present value of terminal value:
= $236,940M / 1.435
= $165,191M

**Total Enterprise Value:**
= $9,188M + $8,893M + $8,608M + $8,326M + $8,052M + $165,191M
= $208,258M

**Per Share Value:**
= $208,258M / 2,200M shares
= $94.66 per share

If PG trades at $168.40, the stock appears overvalued by 77% based on this DCF ($168.40 vs. $94.66 fair value). However, market may assign higher growth expectations or lower risk premium.

## Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

For dividend-paying stocks, particularly those in mature industries, the Dividend Discount Model provides elegant valuation:

**Gordon Growth Model:**
**Stock Value = D1 / (r - g)**

Where:
- D1 = Expected next year's dividend
- r = Required rate of return
- g = Perpetual dividend growth rate

### Real Example: Coca-Cola (KO)

Assumptions:
- Current annual dividend: $1.78
- Expected next year dividend: $1.78 × 1.04 = $1.85 (assuming 4% growth)
- Required return: 8.5%
- Perpetual growth rate: 4%

Stock value = $1.85 / (0.085 - 0.04)
= $1.85 / 0.045
= $41.11 per share

If KO trades at $71.25, this suggests the market either:
1. Expects higher dividend growth (>4%)
2. Or risk is lower than 8.5% assumed
3. Or the stock is overvalued

**Sensitivity Analysis:**

| Growth Rate | Required Return | Calculated Value |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------|
| 3% | 8.5% | $30.83 |
| 4% | 8.5% | $41.11 |
| 5% | 8.5% | $61.67 |
| 4% | 7.5% | $61.67 |
| 4% | 9.5% | $30.83 |

The model's sensitivity to growth and return assumptions demonstrates why different analysts reach different valuations.

## Free Cash Flow (FCF) Analysis

FCF represents cash a company actually generates, not accounting earnings. It equals operating cash flow minus capital expenditures.

**FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures**

**Example Analysis: Apple (AAPL)**

Assume AAPL's annual metrics:
- Operating cash flow: $110 billion
- Capital expenditures: $12 billion
- Free cash flow: $98 billion
- Outstanding shares: 15.2 billion

**FCF Per Share:**
= $98 billion / 15.2 billion
= $6.45 per share

If AAPL trades at $235.80:
- FCF yield = $6.45 / $235.80 = 2.74%

This means the company generates $2.74 in free cash for every $100 of market cap. Healthy mature companies typically yield 3-6% in FCF. AAPL's 2.74% reflects expectations of future growth.

## Enterprise Value-to-EBITDA (EV/EBITDA)

EV/EBITDA compares total enterprise value to earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. It's sector-neutral and useful for comparing companies with different capital structures.

**Formula:**
**EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA**

Where Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Total Debt - Cash

### Real Example

Company metrics:
- Market capitalization: $450 million
- Total debt: $150 million
- Cash: $50 million
- EBITDA: $60 million

Enterprise Value = $450M + $150M - $50M = $550M

EV/EBITDA = $550M / $60M = 9.17x

For industrial companies, median EV/EBITDA is 10-14x, so 9.17x suggests fair or slight undervaluation.

## Valuation Pitfalls to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Relying on Single Metric**

Using only P/E ratio misses critical information. Always use multiple approaches (P/E, P/B, FCF, DDM) to triangulate fair value.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring Growth Quality**

A 20x P/E makes sense for 15% earnings growth but not for 2% growth. Always compare valuation to growth prospects.

**Mistake 3: Using Stale Data**

Quarterly earnings change valuations. Update metrics at minimum quarterly when companies report earnings.

**Mistake 4: Assuming Market is Always Wrong**

Sometimes expensive stocks are expensive because they deserve to be. If your valuation shows massive overvaluation, examine whether you're underestimating growth or overestimating risk.

## FAQ

**Q: What discount rate should I use for DCF?**
A: Use weighted average cost of capital (WACC). For equity investors, 7-10% typically applies. Conservative investors use higher rates; growth investors use lower rates.

**Q: Which metric matters most?**
A: No single metric dominates. A stock can be expensive on earnings yet cheap on cash flow. Use multiple metrics to build confidence.

**Q: How do I calculate expected growth rate?**
A: Use historical growth, analyst consensus estimates, and management guidance. Don't extrapolate past growth indefinitely.

**Q: What's a good margin of safety?**
A: Buy when price is 20-30% below calculated fair value. This margin protects against estimation errors.

**Q: How often should I revalue stocks?**
A: Major company events (earnings misses, analyst downgrades, acquisitions) warrant revaluation. Otherwise, semi-annual reviews work well.

## Conclusion

Stock valuation transforms investing from guesswork into systematic analysis. By mastering P/E ratios, FCF analysis, discounted cash flow models, and dividend discount models, you gain the ability to identify when markets misprice securities.

The tools described here—available freely online—provide access to analyst-grade valuation methodologies. The key to successful application is understanding that valuation produces a range of reasonable values, not a precise number. Your job is identifying stocks where price significantly diverges from that reasonable range, then establishing a margin of safety before deploying capital.

Consistent application of these valuation techniques separates long-term wealth builders from impulse investors.
