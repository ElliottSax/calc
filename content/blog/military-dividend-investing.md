# Military Dividend Investing: TSP, Roth IRA, and Deployment Savings

Military service members hold unique financial advantages that most civilian investors envy: the Thrift Savings Plan with a 5% match, tax-free combat zone pay, housing and food allowances that do not count as income, and the discipline to execute a long-term plan. Yet many service members leave these advantages on the table, investing conservatively in the TSP's G Fund or not investing at all.

A service member who maximizes their TSP, invests deployment savings, and builds a dividend portfolio outside of retirement accounts can achieve financial independence by their mid-40s -- whether they serve 20 years for a pension or transition to civilian life after a single enlistment.

## The Military Financial Advantage

### Pay and Benefits That Enable Investing

Military compensation extends far beyond base pay:

**E-5 with 6 years of service (2025):**
- Base pay: $3,681/month ($44,172/year)
- BAH (housing allowance): $1,200-$2,800/month depending on location
- BAS (food allowance): $452/month
- Special pays (jump, hazardous duty, etc.): $150-$450/month

BAH and BAS are not taxable income. A single E-5 receiving $2,000/month in BAH and $452/month in BAS has $29,424/year in tax-free benefits. This dramatically reduces their effective tax rate and increases investable income.

**O-3 with 4 years of service (2025):**
- Base pay: $5,863/month ($70,356/year)
- BAH: $1,500-$3,500/month
- BAS: $311/month

Officers have even more capacity to invest, with total compensation packages often exceeding $100,000 when accounting for tax-free allowances, healthcare, and other benefits.

### The TSP Advantage

The Thrift Savings Plan is the best government retirement plan in America:
- **Expense ratio**: 0.048% (lower than even Vanguard's cheapest ETFs)
- **Employer match**: 5% of base pay (automatic after 60 days of service)
- **Contribution limit**: $23,500/year (2025), plus $69,000 in combat zones
- **Roth TSP option**: Tax-free growth and withdrawals

The 5% match alone on an E-5's $44,172 base pay contributes $2,209/year in free money. Over a 20-year career at 10% returns, the match alone grows to $140,000+.

## The Military Dividend Investment Strategy

### Tier 1: TSP Foundation

**Contribution target**: At minimum 5% for full match; ideally 15-25% of base pay

**Recommended TSP allocation for dividend growth:**
- C Fund (S&P 500 index): 60% -- Includes the largest U.S. dividend payers
- S Fund (Small/mid-cap index): 20% -- Growth exposure for higher returns
- I Fund (International index): 20% -- Global diversification

**Roth vs. Traditional TSP:**
For most service members, especially those in lower tax brackets (E-1 through E-6), the Roth TSP is superior:
- Current tax rates are low (10-22% bracket for most enlisted)
- Tax-free growth for 20-40 years
- Tax-free withdrawals in retirement (when pension income pushes you into higher brackets)
- No required minimum distributions with Roth TSP

### Tier 2: Roth IRA with Dividend Focus

After maximizing the TSP match, open a Roth IRA for dividend-specific investing:

**Annual contribution**: $7,000 (2025)
**Brokerage**: Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard (all military-friendly)

**Roth IRA Dividend Portfolio:**
- SCHD (40%): Quality dividend stocks, 3.4% yield
- VIG (30%): Dividend growth focus, 1.8% yield
- Individual dividend stocks (30%): 3-5 positions (JNJ, PG, O, MSFT, KO)

This creates your dedicated dividend income stream, growing tax-free.

### Tier 3: Taxable Brokerage for Deployment Savings

After TSP match and Roth IRA, invest excess savings in a taxable brokerage account focused on qualified dividend stocks:

**Focus on qualified dividends**: Taxed at 0-15-20% preferential rates
**Target holdings**: Dividend Aristocrats and Kings for reliability
**Avoid**: REITs and bond funds in taxable accounts (ordinary income tax rates)

## Deployment: The Military Investor's Superpower

### Combat Zone Tax Exclusion (CZTE)

During deployment to a combat zone, all military pay (including base pay) is exempt from federal income tax for enlisted members. Officers receive exclusion up to the highest enlisted pay rate plus imminent danger pay.

This creates several investment superpowers:

**Roth Contributions with $0 Tax Cost**: Contributing to Roth TSP or Roth IRA during a combat zone deployment means you pay zero tax going in AND zero tax coming out. This is the only way to get truly double-tax-free money into a Roth account.

**Higher Investable Income**: Without federal taxes, your take-home pay increases by 15-22%. An E-5 saves approximately $5,500-$7,000 in taxes during a 12-month deployment.

**TSP Combat Zone Contribution Limit**: During deployment, the annual TSP contribution limit increases to $69,000 (the total additions limit, not just elective deferrals). This allows massive tax-free contributions.

### Deployment Savings Strategy

A 12-month deployment is the greatest wealth-building opportunity in military service:

**E-5 Deployment Budget:**
- Base pay (tax-free): $3,681/month
- Hostile fire/imminent danger pay: $225/month
- Family separation allowance: $250/month
- Total monthly income: ~$4,156
- Estimated expenses during deployment: $500/month (phone, subscriptions, personal)
- **Monthly investable surplus: $3,656**

Over 12 months: **$43,872 deployed directly into dividend investments**

That $43,872 invested in dividend stocks at 10% average returns for 20 years becomes $295,000, generating $10,325/year in dividends -- from a single deployment.

**Multiple deployments compound dramatically:**
- 2 deployments: $87,744 invested → $590,000 at retirement → $20,650/year dividends
- 3 deployments: $131,616 invested → $885,000 at retirement → $30,975/year dividends

### Savings Deposit Program (SDP)

The DOD Savings Deposit Program allows deployed service members to deposit up to $10,000 and earn 10% annual interest (guaranteed by the U.S. government). Max this out immediately upon deployment:

- $10,000 at 10% for 12 months = $1,000 guaranteed return
- After deployment, withdraw and invest in dividend stocks
- This is risk-free money -- always take it

## Career Paths and Dividend Outcomes

### Path 1: 20-Year Career (Military Pension + Dividends)

A service member who serves 20 years receives a pension of 40% of their highest 36 months of base pay (under the legacy High-3 system) or a combination of reduced pension + TSP match (under the Blended Retirement System).

**Example: E-7 retiring at 38 with 20 years:**
- Pension (BRS, 40% multiplier): $2,400/month ($28,800/year)
- TSP (15% contributions + 5% match for 20 years): ~$520,000
- Roth IRA + Taxable dividends: ~$350,000
- **Total invested assets**: ~$870,000
- **Annual dividend income**: ~$30,450
- **Total retirement income at 38**: $59,250/year -- and growing

This service member is financially independent at 38 with a growing dividend income that supplements a guaranteed pension. A second career in the civilian world is entirely optional.

### Path 2: Single Enlistment (4-6 Years) + Civilian Dividend Building

Many service members serve one enlistment, build a financial foundation, then transition:

**4-year enlistment, investing $1,000/month + deployment savings:**
- TSP at separation: ~$65,000
- Roth IRA: ~$28,000
- Taxable dividends: ~$20,000
- Deployment savings invested: ~$44,000
- **Total at separation**: ~$157,000

Roll TSP into a Rollover IRA (or leave it in TSP for the low fees), and continue civilian investing. This $157,000 head start at age 23 becomes $2.4 million by 55 at 8% returns -- even without another dollar invested.

### Path 3: Officer Career (Higher Pay, Faster Accumulation)

Officers earn higher base pay and often larger bonuses:

**O-4 with 12 years, investing $3,000/month + two deployment surpluses:**
- TSP: ~$450,000
- Roth IRA: ~$120,000
- Taxable dividends: ~$200,000
- Deployment savings: ~$90,000
- **Total**: ~$860,000 at year 12

An officer investing aggressively can reach $1 million in total invested assets within 12-15 years of commissioning.

## VA Benefits and Dividend Income

### VA Disability Compensation

Tax-free VA disability compensation provides a baseline income that works alongside dividends:
- 10% disability: $171/month ($2,052/year)
- 50% disability: $1,041/month ($12,492/year)
- 100% disability: $3,737/month ($44,844/year)

VA disability + dividend income + military pension creates a triple income stream, all partially or fully tax-advantaged.

### GI Bill as an Investment Tool

Use the GI Bill for education that increases civilian earning power. Higher civilian income = higher dividend investment capacity. The GI Bill housing allowance ($1,500-$3,500/month while enrolled) can also be partially invested.

## Key Takeaways

- The TSP at 0.048% expense ratio is the cheapest retirement plan available -- always contribute at least 5% for the match
- Combat zone deployments offer tax-free income and expanded Roth contribution limits (up to $69,000/year)
- A single 12-month deployment with $44,000 invested grows to $295,000 in 20 years
- A 20-year military career combining pension + TSP + dividends achieves financial independence by age 38-42
- VA disability compensation provides tax-free baseline income alongside dividend investments

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I keep my money in TSP after separating or roll it to an IRA?

The TSP's 0.048% expense ratio is unbeatable, so leaving money in TSP is often the best choice for index fund investing. However, rolling to a self-directed IRA at Fidelity or Schwab gives you access to individual dividend stocks and dividend-focused ETFs not available in the TSP. Consider a hybrid: leave existing TSP balance for low-cost index exposure, and direct new contributions to an IRA for dividend stock access.

### I am an E-3 making $28,000. Can I really invest?

Yes. With BAH and BAS covering housing and food tax-free, your base pay is mostly discretionary after essentials. Even $200/month in a Roth IRA builds meaningful wealth. An E-3 investing $200/month from age 19 to 23 (one enlistment) accumulates $12,000+, which grows to $480,000 by age 60 at 10% returns without any additional contributions.

### How does the Blended Retirement System compare to the legacy pension for dividend investors?

The BRS provides a smaller pension (40% at 20 years vs. 50% under High-3) but adds TSP matching and a continuation pay bonus. For dividend-focused investors, the BRS can actually be superior because the TSP match compounds over your career and the continuation pay can be invested entirely in dividend stocks. The key is actually maximizing TSP contributions -- the match only works if you contribute.

### Can I invest while paying child support or other mandatory deductions?

Yes, but prioritize obligations first. After mandatory deductions, allocate whatever remains using the priority order: TSP to match, Roth IRA, then taxable dividends. Even $100/month invested consistently during a 20-year career grows to $76,000+ and generates $2,660/year in dividends.

### What should I do with my final PCS (permanent change of station) money and separation pay?

Invest it immediately in dividend stocks. Separation pay and unused leave payouts can total $5,000-$25,000. This lump sum invested at separation begins compounding from day one of your civilian life. Combined with your deployed savings and TSP, you enter civilian life with a significant financial head start.
