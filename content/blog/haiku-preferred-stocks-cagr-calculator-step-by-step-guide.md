## Understanding CAGR

Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) measures annualized return over multiple years. It smooths volatility to show true average annual growth.

Formula: CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/Years) - 1

## Step-by-Step CAGR Calculation

**Example: $50,000 PFF (Preferred Stock ETF) investment over 5 years**

Beginning value (Year 0): $50,000
Ending value (Year 5): $62,500

CAGR calculation:
- ($62,500 / $50,000)^(1/5) - 1
- (1.25)^0.2 - 1
- 1.0456 - 1
- **CAGR: 4.56%**

Average annual return: 4.56% (more accurate than simple average).

## CAGR vs. Simple Average Return

Difference becomes significant over long periods:

**5-year example with volatility:**

Year 1: +10%
Year 2: -5%
Year 3: +8%
Year 4: +12%
Year 5: -2%

Simple average: (10 - 5 + 8 + 12 - 2) / 5 = 4.6%

CAGR calculation:
- Starting: $100,000
- Year 5 ending: $100,000 × 1.10 × 0.95 × 1.08 × 1.12 × 0.98 = $113,069
- CAGR: ($113,069 / $100,000)^(1/5) - 1 = 3.09%

CAGR (3.09%) lower than simple average (4.6%) due to volatility impact.

- Year 1 total return: +14.8%

Interest rate movements dominate preferred stock returns more than dividend income.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Is 5-6% CAGR realistic for preferred stocks?**
A: Yes, historically. Interest rate environment affects returns significantly.

**Q2: How does preferred CAGR compare to bonds?**
A: Preferred stocks typically yield 1.5-2.5% more CAGR due to equity characteristics and credit risk.

**Q3: Should I accept lower CAGR for diversification?**
A: Yes. Diversified PFF (5.8% CAGR) better than concentrated individual preferred (4-7% CAGR with higher volatility).

**Q4: Can preferred stocks achieve double-digit CAGR?**
A: Only in rare scenarios (new issue discount + rate decline). Expect 5-7% long-term CAGR.

**Q5: Is CAGR better metric than simple return for preferred stocks?**
A: Yes. CAGR accounts for reinvestment and volatility, providing more accurate long-term performance measure.
