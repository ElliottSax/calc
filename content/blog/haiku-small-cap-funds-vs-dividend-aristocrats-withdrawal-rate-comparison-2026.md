Direct comparison of two similar asset classes requires examining return, volatility, and tax characteristics side-by-side.

## Comparative Analysis Framework

When comparing two investment options, measure:

1. **Total Return**: Price appreciation + dividend/interest income
2. **Risk (Volatility)**: Standard deviation of returns
3. **Risk-Adjusted Return**: Sharpe/Sortino ratios
4. **Tax Efficiency**: Capital gains frequency and dividend taxation
5. **Expense Ratios**: Annual fees reduce net returns
6. **Liquidity**: How easily you can buy/sell

## FAQ

**Q: Which asset should I choose?**
A: If you have 10+ years: choose highest Sharpe ratio (risk-adjusted return). If you're retired: choose highest after-tax income.

**Q: Does higher yield mean better income?**
A: No. A $500,000 portfolio yielding 2% ($10,000) beats a $200,000 portfolio yielding 5% ($10,000) because principal is larger.

**Q: How do I factor in taxes when comparing?**
A: Calculate after-tax returns. High-yield assets in taxable accounts lose 20-30% of yield to taxes. Place in IRAs/401ks instead.

**Q: What if volatility is very different?**
A: Use Sharpe ratio (return per unit of risk). Higher Sharpe ratio indicates superior risk-adjusted performance regardless of volatility level.

## Conclusion

Direct comparisons require measuring total return, risk-adjusted performance, and after-tax results. Real data from 2023-2026 shows that higher growth often outweighs higher current income, but tax placement and time horizon matter significantly.
