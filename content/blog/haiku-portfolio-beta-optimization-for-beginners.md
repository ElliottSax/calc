## Beginning Investor Profile

Beginning investors typically start in their 20s-30s with limited capital but 30+ year time horizons. They prioritize simplicity and consistency over complex optimization.

Target portfolio beta: 0.9-1.1 (approximately market returns with reasonable safety).

## Explaining Beta to Beginners

Think of beta as "market sensitivity":
- Beta 0.75 means portfolio moves 75% as much as market
- Beta 1.0 means portfolio moves same as market
- Beta 1.25 means portfolio moves 125% as much as market

**Example: Market rises 20%**
- Portfolio with beta 0.75: Rises 15%
- Portfolio with beta 1.0: Rises 20%
- Portfolio with beta 1.25: Rises 25%

## Avoiding Common Beginner Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Too many individual stocks**

Why it's wrong: Creates concentration risk, requires monitoring, increases taxes

Solution: Stick with broad index funds (VTI, VXUS) until you have $25,000+

**Mistake 2: Chasing high-yield returns**

Why it's wrong: High beta stocks crater during crashes, causing panic selling

Solution: Accept market returns (10% annual), avoid 15%+ promised returns

**Mistake 3: Market timing**

Why it's wrong: Missing best 10 days costs 50% of returns over decades

Solution: Automatic monthly investing regardless of prices

**Mistake 4: Overcomplicating portfolio**

Why it's wrong: More holdings = more fees, more taxes, more decisions

Solution: 3-fund portfolio (VTI, VXUS, BND) is sufficient for 20 years

## Risk Tolerance Assessment

Beginners should assess actual risk tolerance:

**Question: If portfolio falls 30%, what do you do?**
- Panic sell: Too much volatility. Reduce stock allocation, increase bonds.
- Hold and continue investing: Appropriate for market returns
- Buy more: Appropriate for growth portfolio

**Question: Can you lose 1 year of salary without major stress?**
- No: Reduce stock allocation, increase bond allocation
- Yes: Appropriate for market portfolio
- Yes, and I'd buy more: Appropriate for aggressive portfolio

## Beginner's First Year Portfolio Review

After first year, review and rebalance:

**Example: Initial allocation drifted after market moves**

Beginning: $10,000 (60% stocks, 40% bonds)
- Stocks: $6,000
- Bonds: $4,000

After one year (stocks up 15%, bonds up 3%):
- Stocks: $6,900 (69% of $10,000)
- Bonds: $4,120 (41% of $10,000)
- Total: $11,020

Rebalance to original 60/40:
- Target stocks: $6,612
- Target bonds: $4,408
- Sell $288 stocks, buy $288 bonds

This quarterly/annual discipline maintains target beta.

## Building Good Beginner Habits

**Year 1 habits:**
- Automatic monthly investment
- Ignore daily price movements
- Don't check portfolio more than quarterly

**Year 2-3 habits:**
- Continue monthly investments
- Rebalance annually if allocation drifts >5%
- Learn about tax-loss harvesting

**Year 4+ habits:**
- Consider diversification into tax-advantaged accounts
- Explore tax-efficient positioning
- Increase income/contribution amounts

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What if I only have $500 to invest?**
A: Start with one fund (VTI). Add others as account grows to $5,000+.

**Q2: Should I choose VTI or several smaller ETFs?**
A: VTI alone is sufficient. Diversification within VTI is excellent.

**Q3: How often should I rebalance?**
A: Annually or when allocation drifts >5% from target.

**Q4: Can I simplify further than 3 funds?**
A: Yes. VTI alone works long-term. Add bonds only if uncomfortable with 20% declines.

**Q5: When should I start individual stock investing?**
A: Only after index fund portfolio reaches $25,000+ and you understand investing.
