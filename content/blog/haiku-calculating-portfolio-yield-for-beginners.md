# Calculating Portfolio Yield for Beginners

Portfolio yield measures how much income your investments generate annually. Unlike complex metrics, yield is simple, powerful, and directly impacts your financial independence timeline. This beginner guide reveals step-by-step calculations, real examples, and how to use yield to plan your retirement income.

## The Simplest Yield Formula

**Portfolio Yield = (Annual Dividends) / (Portfolio Value) × 100%**

Example:
- Portfolio value: $100,000
- Annual dividends received: $3,200
- Portfolio yield: ($3,200 / $100,000) × 100% = 3.2%

This means your $100,000 generates $3,200 annually—money paid to you by the companies in your portfolio.

## Step-by-Step Calculation

### Step 1: List All Holdings

Create a simple spreadsheet:

| Holding | Shares | Share Price | Total Value | Annual Dividend/Share | Annual Dividends |
|---------|--------|------------|------------|----------------------|------------------|
| VOO    | 10     | $425       | $4,250     | $0.595               | $5.95            |
| SCHD   | 50     | $65        | $3,250     | $2.08                | $104             |
| JNJ    | 20     | $156       | $3,120     | $4.91                | $98.20           |
| KO     | 30     | $60        | $1,800     | $1.80                | $54              |
| PG     | 25     | $170       | $4,250     | $3.74                | $93.50           |
| **TOTAL** | - | - | **$16,670** | - | **$355.65** |

### Step 2: Calculate Total Annual Dividends

Sum all dividend rows: $5.95 + $104 + $98.20 + $54 + $93.50 = $355.65

### Step 3: Calculate Portfolio Value

Sum all value rows: $4,250 + $3,250 + $3,120 + $1,800 + $4,250 = $16,670

### Step 4: Divide and Multiply by 100

Portfolio yield = ($355.65 / $16,670) × 100% = 2.13%

**Your portfolio yields 2.13%** — It generates $355.65 annually per $16,670 invested.

## Understanding Individual vs. Blended Yield

Individual holdings have different yields. Blending them reveals your true portfolio rate.

### Individual Holdings Yield Breakdown:

Using the example above:

| Holding | Value | Annual Dividend | Yield | Contribution to Portfolio Yield |
|---------|-------|-----------------|-------|--------------------------------|
| VOO    | $4,250 | $5.95           | 0.14% | 0.006%                        |
| SCHD   | $3,250 | $104            | 3.2%  | 0.62%                         |
| JNJ    | $3,120 | $98.20          | 3.15% | 0.59%                         |
| KO     | $1,800 | $54             | 3.0%  | 0.30%                         |
| PG     | $4,250 | $93.50          | 2.2%  | 0.34%                         |
| **TOTAL** | **$16,670** | **$355.65** | **2.13%** | **2.13%** |

**Key insight:** Even though individual holdings range from 0.14% (VOO) to 3.2% (SCHD), the portfolio yield is 2.13% because the holdings are weighted by value. SCHD's 3.2% matters most because it's a larger position.

## Calculating Blended Yield: Weighted Formula

For complex portfolios, use the weighted formula:

**Blended Yield = Σ(Position % × Position Yield)**

### Example with Portfolio C:

1. SCHD: ($80,000 / $200,000) × 3.2% = 1.28%
2. JNJ: ($50,000 / $200,000) × 3.1% = 0.775%
3. KO: ($30,000 / $200,000) × 3.0% = 0.45%
4. PG: ($25,000 / $200,000) × 2.2% = 0.275%
5. BND: ($15,000 / $200,000) × 4.5% = 0.338%

**Sum: 1.28% + 0.775% + 0.45% + 0.275% + 0.338% = 3.14%** (matches our calculation)

## Tracking Yield Over Time

Your portfolio yield changes as holdings and their dividends change.

### Example: SCHD Yield Over 10 Years (Using Dividend Growth)

Assuming you hold 100 shares of SCHD purchased at $65/share = $6,500 position

| Year | Shares | Dividend/Share | Annual Dividend | Yield on Cost | Current Yield |
|------|--------|-----------------|-----------------|--------------|---------------|
| 1    | 100    | $2.08           | $208            | 3.2%         | 3.2%         |
| 3    | 100    | $2.35           | $235            | 3.6%         | 3.2%         |
| 5    | 100    | $2.65           | $265            | 4.1%         | 3.2%         |
| 7    | 100    | $2.99           | $299            | 4.6%         | 3.2%         |
| 10   | 100    | $3.60           | $360            | 5.5%         | 3.2%         |

**Key observation:**
- "Current yield" (dividend / current price) stays approximately 3.2% as price and dividend grow together
- "Yield on cost" (dividend / original cost) grows to 5.5% because the dividend grew while your cost basis remained $65

This illustrates why holding dividend growth stocks long-term creates ever-increasing income from the same capital.

## Adjusting Yield with Market Changes

Market prices affect yield calculations. When stock prices change, yields change (though underlying dividends remain temporarily stable).

### Example: VOO Price Drop Impact

**Scenario A: Normal market**
- VOO price: $425
- Annual dividend: $0.595/share
- Yield: $0.595 / $425 = 0.14%

**Scenario B: Market correction (20% drop)**
- VOO price: $340 (20% lower)
- Annual dividend: $0.595/share (unchanged in first year)
- Yield: $0.595 / $340 = 0.175%

**Observation:** When market drops, yield appears to increase because the same dividend is divided by a lower price. This creates opportunity—buying depressed assets at higher yields.

## Yield in Taxable vs. Tax-Deferred Accounts

Yield calculation is the same, but after-tax income differs.

### Taxable Account Impact:

**Taxable account (22% tax bracket):**
- Portfolio yield: 3.2%
- Tax on qualified dividends: 15%
- After-tax yield: 3.2% × (1 - 15%) = 2.72%
- After-tax income on $100,000: $2,720/year

**Tax-deferred account (IRA, 401k):**
- Portfolio yield: 3.2%
- Tax on dividends: 0% (deferred)
- Current yield: 3.2%
- Current income on $100,000: $3,200/year

**Advantage:** Tax-deferred accounts generate $480 more annual income on $100,000 ($3,200 vs $2,720).

## FAQ

**Q: What's a good portfolio yield?**
A: 2-3% is healthy for balanced portfolios. Higher yields (4%+) indicate concentrated dividend positions or higher risk. Growth portfolios naturally yield 1-2%.

**Q: Should I chase high yields?**
A: No. Yields above 5% often signal companies in trouble (dividend cuts coming). Focus on sustainable 3-4% yields from dividend aristocrats.

**Q: How do I increase portfolio yield without adding capital?**
A: Rebalance toward higher-yielding positions (SCHD over VOO, dividend stocks over growth). This shifts capital to higher income without new contributions.

**Q: What happens to yield in a recession?**
A: Dividend-paying companies reduce or maintain dividends. Portfolio yield temporarily increases (same dividend on lower stock prices), then decreases if companies cut dividends.

**Q: How much portfolio value do I need for retirement income?**
A: Using the 3% rule: Divide desired annual income by 3%. To generate $50,000 annually: $50,000 / 0.03 = $1,667,000 portfolio needed.
