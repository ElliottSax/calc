# Qualified vs Ordinary Dividends: Tax Treatment Explained

The distinction between qualified and ordinary dividends can mean the difference between paying 0% and 37% in federal taxes on the same dollar of income. For a retiree receiving $40,000 in annual dividend income, this classification could save or cost up to $14,800 per year. Yet many investors do not fully understand what makes a dividend qualified, how the holding period works, or which investments generate which type.

This guide explains the qualified vs ordinary dividend classification system, shows you the real dollar impact on your portfolio, and provides actionable strategies for maximizing your after-tax income.

## The Core Difference

**Qualified dividends** are taxed at the long-term capital gains rate: 0%, 15%, or 20%, depending on your taxable income.

**Ordinary dividends** (also called non-qualified dividends) are taxed at your regular income tax rate, which can be as high as 37%.

Both types appear on your Form 1099-DIV. Box 1a shows total ordinary dividends, and Box 1b shows qualified dividends (which are a subset of Box 1a). The ordinary dividend amount you actually pay ordinary income tax on is the difference: Box 1a minus Box 1b.

## Tax Rate Comparison

Here is the 2026 federal tax impact for a married couple filing jointly:

| Taxable Income | Qualified Dividend Rate | Ordinary Income Rate | Tax on $10,000 Qualified | Tax on $10,000 Ordinary |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $60,000 | 0% | 12% | $0 | $1,200 |
| $120,000 | 15% | 22% | $1,500 | $2,200 |
| $250,000 | 15% | 24% | $1,500 | $2,400 |
| $450,000 | 15% | 35% | $1,500 | $3,500 |
| $600,000 | 20% | 35% | $2,000 | $3,500 |
| $800,000 | 20% | 37% | $2,000 | $3,700 |

At higher income levels, add the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax to both rates for taxpayers with MAGI above $250,000 (MFJ).

## Requirements for Qualified Dividend Status

Three conditions must all be met for a dividend to qualify for the preferential tax rate:

### Condition 1: Paid by a Qualifying Entity

The dividend must be paid by one of the following:

- A U.S. corporation
- A corporation incorporated in a U.S. possession
- A foreign corporation whose stock is readily tradable on an established U.S. securities market
- A foreign corporation resident in a country with a qualifying tax treaty with the U.S. (most developed nations qualify)

### Condition 2: Not Specifically Excluded

Certain dividends are excluded from qualified treatment regardless of the source:

- **REIT dividends**: Taxed as ordinary income (though the Section 199A deduction may apply)
- **Master limited partnership (MLP) distributions**: Generally treated as return of capital or ordinary income
- **Money market fund dividends**: Always ordinary income
- **Dividends paid on shares lent out** (substitute payments): Ordinary income
- **Employee stock option dividends**: If exercised within the holding period
- **Dividends from tax-exempt organizations**: Ordinary income
- **Capital gain distributions from mutual funds**: Taxed as capital gains (a separate category)

### Condition 3: Holding Period Met

You must hold the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period that begins 60 days before the ex-dividend date.

**Example**: A stock goes ex-dividend on March 15. The 121-day window begins on January 14 and ends on June 14. You must hold the stock for at least 61 days within this window for the dividend to qualify.

For preferred stock, the holding period extends to more than 90 days during a 181-day window.

This rule exists to prevent investors from buying a stock the day before the ex-dividend date, collecting the dividend at the preferential rate, and immediately selling.

## Which Investments Pay Which Type?

Understanding the typical classification helps you plan your portfolio:

### Primarily Qualified Dividends

- **Blue-chip U.S. stocks**: Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson, Procter & Gamble, and most S&P 500 dividend payers
- **U.S. equity index funds and ETFs**: S&P 500, Total Stock Market, and dividend-focused funds
- **Many international stocks**: From countries with qualifying U.S. tax treaties
- **Preferred stocks**: From qualifying corporations (with the 90-day holding period)

### Primarily Ordinary Dividends

- **REITs**: Equity REITs, mortgage REITs, and REIT ETFs
- **Bond funds**: Interest income distributed as dividends
- **Money market funds**: All distributions are ordinary
- **MLPs**: Most distributions are ordinary or return of capital
- **Covered call ETFs**: Income from option premiums is ordinary
- **Short-held positions**: Stocks held fewer than 61 days around the ex-dividend date

### Mixed Classification

- **Balanced funds**: Mix of qualified (equity dividends) and ordinary (bond interest)
- **Target-date funds**: Varying mix depending on equity/bond allocation
- **International funds**: May include qualified dividends, ordinary dividends, and foreign tax withholding

## The Real Dollar Impact on Your Portfolio

Let us compare two retirees, each receiving $50,000 in annual dividend income but from different sources:

**Retiree A: Primarily Qualified Dividends**
- $45,000 qualified dividends (from blue-chip stocks)
- $5,000 ordinary dividends (from a REIT)
- Total taxable income: $80,000 (MFJ including Social Security)
- Tax on qualified dividends: $0 (within the 0% bracket)
- Tax on ordinary dividends: $600 (12% bracket)
- **Total federal tax on dividends: $600**

**Retiree B: Primarily Ordinary Dividends**
- $15,000 qualified dividends
- $35,000 ordinary dividends (REITs, bond funds, MLPs)
- Total taxable income: $80,000 (MFJ)
- Tax on qualified dividends: $0 (within the 0% bracket)
- Tax on ordinary dividends: $4,200 (12% bracket)
- **Total federal tax on dividends: $4,200**

Retiree B pays $3,600 more in federal taxes annually for the same $50,000 in dividend income. Over a 25-year retirement, that is $90,000 in additional taxes.

## Strategies for Maximizing Qualified Dividends

### Strategy 1: Check Before You Buy

Before purchasing any dividend investment, verify what percentage of its distributions are qualified. This information is available in a fund's prospectus or on the fund company's tax information page. Many dividend ETFs report that 90-100% of their distributions are qualified.

### Strategy 2: Meet the Holding Period

If you trade frequently, you may inadvertently disqualify dividends. Track your holding periods around ex-dividend dates. For most investors who buy and hold, this is never an issue.

### Strategy 3: Place Ordinary Income Generators in Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Hold REITs, bond funds, and other ordinary income producers inside Traditional or Roth IRAs. Hold qualified dividend payers in taxable brokerage accounts. This maximizes the benefit of the preferential tax rate.

### Strategy 4: Be Careful with Covered Call Strategies

Covered call ETFs and strategies generate income primarily from option premiums, which are taxed as short-term capital gains (ordinary income rates). The higher yield may not compensate for the worse tax treatment.

### Strategy 5: Review Your 1099-DIV Annually

Check the qualified dividend percentage of your holdings each year. Fund managers may change strategies or portfolio composition, affecting the qualified/ordinary split.

## Key Takeaways

- Qualified dividends are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%, while ordinary dividends are taxed at rates up to 37%, making the classification worth potentially thousands of dollars per year.
- Three conditions must be met: the dividend must come from a qualifying entity, not be specifically excluded, and the stock must be held for the required 61+ day period.
- REITs, bond funds, money market funds, and MLPs generally pay ordinary dividends, while most U.S. blue-chip stocks and equity index funds pay qualified dividends.
- Place ordinary dividend investments in tax-advantaged accounts and qualified dividend payers in taxable accounts for maximum after-tax income.
- The difference between a primarily qualified vs primarily ordinary dividend portfolio can exceed $3,600 per year for a retiree with $50,000 in dividend income.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my dividends are qualified or ordinary?

Your broker reports this on Form 1099-DIV. Box 1a shows total ordinary dividends, and Box 1b shows qualified dividends. The difference between these two amounts is your non-qualified ordinary dividends. Most brokerages also show this breakdown in their online tax reporting tools.

### Are ETF dividends qualified?

It depends on the ETF. Equity ETFs that hold U.S. stocks (like S&P 500 or dividend-focused ETFs) typically distribute 90-100% qualified dividends. Bond ETFs, REIT ETFs, and covered call ETFs typically distribute ordinary dividends. Check the fund's tax information page for specifics.

### Do qualified dividends count as income for tax bracket purposes?

Yes, qualified dividends are included in your taxable income and can push you into a higher bracket for other income. However, the dividends themselves are taxed at their own preferential rate (0%, 15%, or 20%), not at your marginal ordinary income rate.

### Can I convert ordinary dividends to qualified?

No, you cannot change the classification of a dividend after it is paid. The classification is determined by the source (what type of entity paid it) and your holding period. The only way to receive more qualified dividends is to invest in securities that pay them.

### What about the Section 199A deduction for REIT dividends?

The Section 199A qualified business income deduction allows eligible taxpayers to deduct up to 20% of REIT ordinary dividends. This effectively reduces the maximum federal tax rate on REIT dividends from 37% to 29.6%. The deduction is available regardless of whether you itemize and does not require any active business involvement.
