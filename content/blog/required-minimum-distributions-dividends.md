# RMDs and Dividend Investing: Strategy After Age 73

Required minimum distributions represent one of the most significant tax planning challenges for retirees with substantial Traditional IRA or 401(k) balances. Starting at age 73, the IRS mandates that you withdraw a calculated percentage of your tax-deferred accounts each year, whether you need the money or not. For dividend investors, this creates both a challenge and an opportunity: your dividend income can help satisfy RMD requirements, but the combination of RMDs and dividends can push you into higher tax brackets.

This guide explains how RMDs work, how they interact with dividend income, and strategies for minimizing the tax impact.

## How Required Minimum Distributions Work

The SECURE 2.0 Act raised the RMD starting age to 73 for those born between 1951 and 1959, and to 75 for those born in 1960 or later. Here is the current schedule:

| Birth Year | RMD Starting Age |
|---|---|
| 1950 or earlier | 72 (already started) |
| 1951-1959 | 73 |
| 1960 or later | 75 |

### Calculating Your RMD

Your RMD is calculated by dividing your account balance (as of December 31 of the prior year) by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table:

| Age | Divisor | RMD % of Balance |
|---|---|---|
| 73 | 26.5 | 3.77% |
| 75 | 24.6 | 4.07% |
| 78 | 22.0 | 4.55% |
| 80 | 20.2 | 4.95% |
| 85 | 16.0 | 6.25% |
| 90 | 12.2 | 8.20% |

For a retiree with a $1,000,000 Traditional IRA at age 73, the first RMD would be approximately $37,736 ($1,000,000 / 26.5).

### Which Accounts Require RMDs

- **Traditional IRAs**: Yes
- **401(k), 403(b), 457 plans**: Yes
- **SEP IRAs and SIMPLE IRAs**: Yes
- **Roth IRAs**: No (during the owner's lifetime)
- **Roth 401(k)s**: No (starting in 2024 under SECURE 2.0)
- **Taxable brokerage accounts**: No

## How Dividends Interact with RMDs

Dividends within a Traditional IRA do not automatically satisfy your RMD. This is a common misconception. Here is what actually happens:

### Dividends Inside Your IRA

When stocks in your Traditional IRA pay dividends, those dividends are deposited into the IRA's cash settlement fund. They remain inside the IRA until you actively withdraw them. Dividends earned inside the IRA are not reported as income until you take a distribution.

### Using Dividends to Meet Your RMD

You can direct your IRA dividends to your bank account throughout the year, and each withdrawal counts toward your annual RMD. If your IRA generates enough dividend income to cover the full RMD, you may never need to sell a single share.

**Example**: An $800,000 Traditional IRA generating a 3.5% dividend yield produces $28,000 in annual dividends. At age 73, the RMD is $30,189 ($800,000 / 26.5). The dividends cover 93% of the RMD. You only need to sell $2,189 in shares (or withdraw that much from the cash position) to satisfy the requirement.

### The RMD Income Tax Problem

Here is the challenge: RMD withdrawals from a Traditional IRA are taxed as ordinary income, regardless of whether the underlying investments generated qualified dividends. This means qualified dividends that would be taxed at 0-20% in a taxable account are instead taxed at ordinary rates of 10-37% when distributed through an RMD.

## Strategies for Managing RMDs and Dividend Income

### Strategy 1: Roth Conversions Before Age 73

The most powerful RMD reduction strategy is converting portions of your Traditional IRA to a Roth IRA before RMDs begin. The years between retirement (say, age 62) and age 73 are often called the "tax planning window" because your income is typically lower.

**Example conversion plan**:
- Age 63-72: Convert $60,000 per year to a Roth IRA
- Tax cost: ~$9,600 per year at the 22% bracket (adjusted for deductions)
- Total converted: $600,000 over 10 years
- Traditional IRA reduced from $1,200,000 to approximately $700,000 (after growth)
- RMD at age 73 on $700,000: $26,415 instead of $45,283
- Annual tax savings on RMDs: $4,156 per year at the 22% bracket

The converted Roth amount has no RMDs and generates tax-free dividends forever.

### Strategy 2: Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

If you are 70.5 or older, you can donate up to $105,000 per year (2024 limit, indexed for inflation) directly from your IRA to a qualified charity. QCDs count toward your RMD but are excluded from taxable income. This is one of the most tax-efficient ways to give to charity.

**Example**: Your RMD is $40,000. You donate $15,000 to your church through a QCD. Your taxable RMD is reduced to $25,000, saving $3,300-$5,550 in taxes depending on your bracket.

### Strategy 3: Hold High-Yield Investments in Your Traditional IRA

Since dividends inside a Traditional IRA are taxed as ordinary income when distributed regardless of their classification, there is no benefit to holding qualified dividend payers here. Instead, use your Traditional IRA for:

- REITs (dividends are ordinary income anyway)
- Bond funds (interest is ordinary income)
- High-yield stocks and funds
- Any investment where the tax treatment does not matter until withdrawal

Reserve your taxable account for qualified dividend stocks and your Roth IRA for your highest-growth investments.

### Strategy 4: Satisfy RMDs with In-Kind Transfers

You do not have to sell investments to take your RMD. You can transfer shares in-kind from your IRA to a taxable brokerage account. The shares are valued at their market price on the transfer date, and that value counts as your distribution.

**Benefit**: If you transfer dividend stocks in-kind, they continue paying dividends in your taxable account, and future dividends may qualify for the lower qualified dividend tax rate. You have effectively moved them from a tax-deferred account (where dividends are taxed at ordinary rates) to a taxable account (where qualified dividends are taxed at 0-20%).

### Strategy 5: Aggregate RMDs Strategically

If you have multiple Traditional IRAs, you must calculate the RMD for each one separately, but you can take the total distribution from any one (or any combination) of them. This lets you draw from whichever IRA is most tax-efficient or best aligned with your rebalancing goals.

## RMD Penalties and Compliance

The penalty for failing to take your full RMD has been reduced from 50% to 25% (and to 10% if corrected within 2 years) under SECURE 2.0. However, even a 25% penalty on top of ordinary income tax makes compliance essential.

### Key Deadlines

- **First RMD**: April 1 of the year after you turn 73. But be cautious: delaying to April 1 means you must take two RMDs in one year (the delayed first year plus the current year), which can push you into a higher bracket.
- **Subsequent RMDs**: December 31 of each year.

## Key Takeaways

- RMDs begin at age 73 (or 75 for those born in 1960+) and are calculated by dividing your account balance by an IRS life expectancy factor.
- Dividends earned inside a Traditional IRA do not automatically satisfy your RMD; you must actively withdraw the funds.
- A well-yielding dividend portfolio can cover most or all of your RMD amount, reducing or eliminating the need to sell shares.
- Roth conversions before age 73 are the most powerful strategy for reducing future RMDs and creating tax-free dividend income.
- Qualified charitable distributions allow taxpayers 70.5+ to donate up to $105,000 from their IRA, reducing taxable RMD amounts.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do dividends in my IRA count toward my RMD?

Not automatically. Dividends earned inside your IRA stay in the account until you withdraw them. However, if you set up systematic withdrawals or manually withdraw dividend cash, those withdrawals count toward your RMD. You can configure your IRA to automatically distribute dividends to your bank account.

### What happens if I do not take my RMD?

The penalty is 25% of the amount you should have withdrawn but did not (reduced from the previous 50% under SECURE 2.0). If you correct the error within 2 years, the penalty drops to 10%. In addition to the penalty, you still owe ordinary income tax on the distribution when you eventually take it.

### Can I take my RMD in stock instead of cash?

Yes. You can satisfy your RMD through an in-kind transfer of shares to a taxable brokerage account. The fair market value of the transferred shares on the transfer date counts as your distribution. This can be a smart strategy for moving dividend stocks from tax-deferred to taxable accounts.

### Do Roth IRAs have RMDs?

No. Roth IRAs have no required minimum distributions during the owner's lifetime. This makes the Roth IRA the best account for long-term dividend compounding. Inherited Roth IRAs do have distribution requirements for most non-spouse beneficiaries (10-year rule), but the distributions remain tax-free.

### How do RMDs affect my Medicare premiums?

RMD income is included in your modified adjusted gross income, which determines your Medicare Part B and Part D premiums. Higher income triggers Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amounts (IRMAA) surcharges. For 2026, the first IRMAA threshold is approximately $103,000 (single) or $206,000 (married filing jointly). Strategic Roth conversions before age 73 can reduce RMDs and help avoid IRMAA surcharges.
