# Understanding Preferred Stock Dividend Yields

Preferred stocks offer an attractive middle ground between bonds and common stocks: higher yields than bonds (5-7%), less volatility than stocks, and strong tax treatment. Yet many investors overlook them due to complexity around call risk and payment structure. This guide demystifies preferred stocks and shows how to build an income-generating preferred stock portfolio.

## What Are Preferred Stocks?

Preferred stocks are a hybrid security—they rank between bonds and common equity in a company's capital structure.

**Key characteristics:**
- Fixed dividend payment (like bond coupon)
- Senior to common stock (get paid before common shareholders)
- Junior to bonds (get paid after bondholders in bankruptcy)
- Often callable by issuer (can be redeemed early)
- Can be perpetual (no maturity date) or have maturity dates

**Example: Wells Fargo Preferred Stock (Wells Fargo Capital Trust Preferred F)**

- Annual dividend: $2.70
- Current price: $50
- Yield: 5.4%
- Type: Non-cumulative perpetual
- Callable: Yes, at $25.00

## Preferred vs. Bonds vs. Common Stock

### Comparison Matrix

| Feature | Preferred | Bond | Common Stock |
|---------|-----------|------|---------|
| Dividend/Coupon | 4-7% typical | 4-5% typical | 1-3% typical |
| Tax treatment | Qualified dividend (15%) | Ordinary income (37%) | Qualified dividend (15%) |
| Volatility | Moderate | Low | High |
| Call risk | Yes | Possible | No |
| Priority in bankruptcy | 2nd | 1st | 3rd |
| Upside potential | Limited | None | Unlimited |
| Maturity | Perpetual (often) | Fixed date | Never |

**Tax advantage:** Preferred dividends are typically qualified dividends (taxed at 0-20%), not ordinary income. This makes them more tax-efficient than bond interest.

**Risk:** Preferreds fall between bonds and stocks. They're safer than common stock but riskier than bonds. If company struggles, preferreds are paid after all bondholders.

## Understanding Preferred Stock Yields

### The Yield Formula

**Yield = Annual Dividend / Current Price**

**Example:**

Wells Fargo Preferred F:
- Annual dividend: $2.70
- Current price: $50
- Yield: $2.70 / $50 = 5.4%

### What You Get vs. What You Paid

The stated dividend (5.4%) assumes you bought at $50. But what if you bought higher or lower?

**If you buy at $45:**
- Annual dividend: still $2.70
- Your yield: $2.70 / $45 = 6.0%
- You get 6% yield, better than the listed 5.4%

**If you buy at $55:**
- Annual dividend: still $2.70
- Your yield: $2.70 / $55 = 4.9%
- You get 4.9% yield, worse than the listed 5.4%

Preferred stock prices fluctuate based on interest rates and credit quality. This creates opportunity: buy when prices are depressed (yields are high), sell when they recover.

### Perpetual vs. Term Preferreds

**Perpetual Preferreds:**
- No maturity date
- Pay dividends indefinitely
- Often callable (issuer can redeem)
- Most common structure

**Cumulative Preferreds:**
- Dividends accrue if skipped
- If company struggles, all skipped dividends must be paid before common shareholders get anything
- Provides more protection

**Non-Cumulative Preferreds:**
- If dividends are skipped, they're lost
- Less protection but slightly higher yield
- Riskier structure

**Term Preferreds:**
- Have a fixed maturity date (e.g., 2035)
- Behave more like bonds
- More stable price at maturity

## Call Risk: The Hidden Risk

The biggest risk with preferred stocks is call risk—the issuer can redeem shares at a fixed price, usually higher than the original issue price.

### How Call Risk Works

**Scenario: You buy BAC Preferred at par ($25)**

- Dividend: $1.82 annually (7.28% yield)
- Call price: $26.00
- Call date: 2028

**Scenario 1: Interest rates rise**
- Preferred price falls to $20 (higher yield attracts demand)
- You hold, accepting lower price
- Company unlikely to call (they'd rather let it stay low)

**Scenario 2: Interest rates fall**
- Preferred price rises to $28 (lower yield needed)
- Company calls at $26, forcing redemption
- You get $26 back (loss of $2 appreciation potential)
- Problem: You can't reinvest at 7.28%—new preferreds yield only 5.5%

### Call Price Impact Example

**Preferred purchase:** $25, 7.5% yield, callable at $26

Price scenarios after 5 years:

| Scenario | Price | If Called | Return | Probability |
|----------|-------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Rates rise | $18 | Not called | $18 + dividends | Moderate |
| Rates flat | $25 | Not called | $25 + dividends | Low |
| Rates fall moderately | $27 | Called at $26 | $26 + dividends | High |
| Rates fall sharply | $32 | Called at $26 | $26 + dividends | Very high |

In rising-rate environments, preferreds outperform (no call). In falling-rate environments, preferreds are capped at call price (called away).

This is the inverse of common stocks: prefer falling rates for call protection.

## Preferred Stock Market Dynamics

### Interest Rate Sensitivity

Preferred prices are highly sensitive to interest rate changes:

**Example: Bank of America Preferred C**

When 3-month Treasury yields are:
- 4.0%: Preferred trades at $20.50 (8.9% yield)
- 4.5%: Preferred trades at $19.75 (8.9% yield maintained)
- 5.0%: Preferred trades at $18.75 (9.6% yield)
- 5.5%: Preferred trades at $18.00 (9.8% yield)

When rates rise, preferred prices fall to maintain competitive yields vs. Treasury alternatives.

Conversely, when rates fall:
- 3.0%: Preferred trades at $22.00 (likely called if callable)
- 2.5%: Preferred redeemed at call price (~$25)

### Credit Quality Impact

Company credit deterioration hits preferreds hard:

**Example: Energy Transfer (ET) Preferred Volatility**

| Year | Stock Price | Preferred Price | Yield | Event |
|------|-------------|-----------------|-------|-------|
| 2020 | $8 | $15 | 10.0% | Oil crash |
| 2021 | $13 | $20 | 7.5% | Recovery begins |
| 2022 | $16 | $22 | 6.8% | Rates rise |
| 2023 | $18 | $21 | 7.2% | Stabilization |
| 2024 | $20 | $23 | 6.5% | Recovery completed |

When a company deteriorates, preferreds fall harder than bonds because they're subordinated. But as company recovers, preferreds recover faster (more upside).

## Tax Treatment of Preferred Dividends

Preferred dividends are usually qualified dividends, taxed at preferential rates:

**Qualified Dividend Tax Rates (2026):**

| Filing Status | 0% Rate | 15% Rate | 20% Rate |
|---------------|---------|----------|----------|
| Single | $0-47,025 | $47,025-518,900 | $518,900+ |
| Married Filing Jointly | $0-94,050 | $94,050-583,750 | $583,750+ |

**Example:** $2,000/month preferred income = $24,000/year

- If you're in the 15% federal bracket + 24% state tax (California)
- Total tax: 15% + 13.3% state = 28.3%
- After-tax income: $24,000 × (1 - 0.283) = $17,184

Even with state taxes, after-tax yield on $400,000 in 6% preferreds: $24,000 × 0.717 = $17,208, or 4.3% after-tax.

Compare to high-yield savings: 5.0% pre-tax = 3.6% after-tax (at 28.3% combined rate).

Preferreds provide better after-tax returns than savings accounts despite lower pre-tax yields.

## Common Preferred Stock Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Ignoring call risk.** Buying a 7% preferred expecting 7% yields indefinitely ignores call risk. If rates fall, the preferred is called at a loss on upside appreciation.

**Mistake 2: Concentrating in mortgage REITs.** Mortgage REIT preferreds yield 10-11%, but fail during rate-hiking cycles. Limit to 10-15% of preferred portfolio.

**Mistake 3: Using margin to buy preferreds.** Leveraging preferred income is dangerous. Margin calls during market downturns force sales at losses.

**Mistake 4: Assuming credit quality never changes.** A "safe" bank preferred can become risky if the bank deteriorates. Monitor credit ratings quarterly.

**Mistake 5: Forgetting reinvestment.** Preferred dividends create cash drag if not reinvested. Automatically reinvest through dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs).

## Conclusion

Preferred stocks offer an attractive 5-7% income for conservative investors, 8-11% for aggressive investors willing to accept call risk and credit risk. Build a ladder using a core of ETF preferreds (PFF, VPL) for diversification, add individual bank preferreds for safety and transparency, and avoid concentration in high-yield mortgage REITs.

The key advantage: after-tax yields exceeding bonds due to qualified dividend treatment, plus moderate growth potential if market reprices preferreds higher. For investors needing monthly income, a $400,000-$500,000 preferred stock allocation generates $2,000-$2,500/month in sustainable distributions.
