## Currency Hedging Decision Framework

International investments face currency risk. When investing $100,000 in VXUS, currency fluctuations impact returns.

**Example: Currency Risk Impact**

```
Invest $100,000 in VXUS (60% in EUR, JPY, other currencies)

Scenario 1: Dollar strengthens 10%
- Foreign stocks return: +8%
- Currency loss: -10%
- Net return: -2.2%

Scenario 2: Dollar weakens 10%
- Foreign stocks return: +8%
- Currency gain: +10%
- Net return: +18.8%

Currency can swing returns ±10% (or more)
```

### Unhedged International (Recommended for most)

**Strategy:** Accept currency fluctuations; don't hedge.

**Rationale:**
```
1. Hedging costs 0.5-1.0% annually
2. Currency weakening ($ weakness) benefits foreign investing
3. Over 20+ year periods, currency fluctuations average out
4. Currency diversification provides inflation hedge
```

**Recommended funds:** VXUS, VEA, VWO (all unhedged)

**Long-term currency data (USD vs major currencies):**

| Period | EUR | JPY | GBP | Net Effect |
|--------|-----|-----|-----|-----------|
| 1990-2000 | +18% | -32% | -8% | 0% average |
| 2000-2010 | -35% | -18% | -20% | -24% (helped foreign investing) |
| 2010-2020 | +25% | +32% | +15% | +21% (hurt foreign investing) |
| 2020-2024 | -8% | -15% | -10% | -11% (helped foreign investing) |

**Key insight:** Currency fluctuations over 20+ years average near zero. Cost of hedging (0.5-1.0%) exceeds benefits for long-term investors.

### Currency-Hedged International (For hedgers)

**Strategy:** Use currency-hedged versions of international ETFs.

**Hedged ETF Options:**

| ETF | Type | ER | Strategy |
|-----|------|-----|----------|
| VXUS | Unhedged | 0.08% | Default |

| HEWG | EUR-hedged | 0.58% | Europe only |
| EWJ | Unhedged Japan | 0.69% | Unhedged |
| DXJ | Japan USD-hedged | 0.40% | Hedged |

**When currency hedging makes sense:**

1. **Nearing/in retirement:** Reduce portfolio volatility, know exact currency you'll spend
2. **Planning specific foreign spending:** Moving abroad, large purchase in foreign currency
3. **Concentrated currency risk:** If 50%+ in single currency (e.g., all EAFE = 35% Japan)
4. **Short time horizon:** < 3 years before spending proceeds

**Hedging cost impact:**

```
Unhedged VXUS cost: 0.08%
Hedged equivalent: 0.40-0.60%
Annual cost difference: 0.32-0.52%
10-year cost: $3,200-$5,200 on $100,000

If currency averages net zero, hedging costs you money

Break-even: Currency must decline >0.32-0.52% annually to justify hedging
Given historical volatility of ±10%, unhedged is better 80% of time
```

## Geographic Weighting Strategies

### Market-Weight Approach (Capital Markets Weight)

Allocate to countries by their market cap weight.

**Example: $100,000 international allocation (25% of $400,000 portfolio)**

Global market weight:
- Developed Markets: 70% × $25,000 = $17,500
- Emerging Markets: 30% × $25,000 = $7,500

Within Developed Markets (70% from VXUS DM exposure):
- Europe: 35% = $6,125
- Japan: 18% = $3,150
- Canada: 8% = $1,400
- Australia/NZ: 7% = $1,225
- Other DM: 12% = $2,100
- Emerging Markets: 30% = $7,500

**Implementation:**
```
VXUS $25,000: Automatically provides capital-market weight
Simplest approach, no rebalancing needed for geographic weights
```

**Return expectation:** Matches global market return (~7.5% annually)

### GDP-Weight Approach (Economic Reality Weight)

Allocate based on global GDP rather than market cap.

**Rationale:** Emerging markets (40% of global GDP) underweighted in market-cap approach.

**Example: Shift to GDP weight**

Global GDP breakdown:
- Developed Markets: 50% of global GDP
- Emerging Markets: 50% of global GDP

For $25,000 international:
- Developed Markets: $12,500 (50%)
- Emerging Markets: $12,500 (50%)

**Implementation:**
```
VEA (Developed): $12,500
VWO (Emerging): $12,500
```

**Return expectation:** 5.4% annually (lower DM return + higher EM allocation)

**This approach captures:**
- Higher emerging market growth potential
- Emerging market GDP outgrowth advantages
- But: Market caps eventually match GDP, so market-weight likely more optimal

### Growth-Weight Approach (Future-Oriented)

Overweight fastest-growing markets (China, India, EM growth).

**Example: Growth allocation on $25,000 international**

## Tax Considerations for International Investing

### Foreign Tax Credit

Foreign governments tax dividends. U.S. provides credit for taxes paid.

**Example:**

Hold $10,000 of Canadian stocks, receive $200 dividend.

```
Canadian taxes withheld: $25 (Canadian 15% withholding)
U.S. tax owed: $60 (U.S. 30% on $200)
Foreign Tax Credit: Can reduce U.S. tax by $25 (actual paid)
Net U.S. tax: $60 - $25 = $35
Total tax rate: $25 + $35 = $60 (30% effective)
```

**Key point:** Foreign Tax Credit prevents double taxation. Hold international investments in taxable accounts to claim credit.

### Placement Optimization for International

International bonds have higher foreign withholding taxes.

**Recommended placement:**

| Fund | Preferred Location | Reason |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| VEA (Int'l stocks) | Taxable account | Claim foreign tax credit |
| VWO (EM stocks) | Roth IRA | Tax-free growth, high turnover |
| International bonds | Traditional IRA | Avoid withholding, double tax |
| VXUS | Roth if available | Highest growth potential |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What percentage should I allocate to international?**
A: 25-35% for long-term investors. This matches near global market weights (40% intl) while maintaining U.S. home-country bias. Conservative: 15-20%. Aggressive: 35-40%.

**Q: Should I hedge currency risk?**
A: No, for most investors. Hedging costs 0.5-1% annually, which exceeds benefits over 10+ year periods. Currency fluctuations average near zero long-term.

**Q: Is emerging markets too risky?**
A: Higher volatility, yes. But 10-20% of portfolio in EM is reasonable. EM provides growth potential and diversification. Avoid >40% EM for most investors.

**Q: Which is better: VXUS (all intl) or VEA/VWO (split)?**
A: VXUS simpler (single fund, 0.08% ER). VEA/VWO more control. For <$100k portfolios, VXUS is fine. For >$250k, VEA/VWO splits offer flexibility.

**Q: How often should I rebalance international?**
A: Same as overall portfolio (quarterly or semi-annually). When EM outperforms and grows to 15% of international (from 12% target), trim back.

**Q: Should I hold international bonds?**
A: Optional. 5% of portfolio in international bonds adds minimal diversification. For simplicity, stick with U.S. bonds only.

**Q: Is international diversification still valuable?**
A: Yes. Correlations vary by cycle, but average 0.65-0.75 (not perfectly correlated). This still provides portfolio stabilization during U.S. market stress.
