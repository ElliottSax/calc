# Understanding REITs for Passive Income

Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) solve a fundamental investor problem: real estate generates excellent returns but requires $200,000+ to buy property, plus active management and illiquidity. REITs let ordinary investors own real estate portfolios, collect monthly distributions, and maintain liquidity. This guide explains how REITs work and how to build a diversified real estate income strategy.

## What Are REITs?

A REIT is a company that owns and manages income-generating real estate. Instead of owning a single apartment complex, you own shares in a REIT that owns hundreds of properties. The REIT collects rent, pays expenses, and distributes at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders.

**Key REIT Requirements:**
- Must own real estate or mortgages
- Must distribute 90%+ of taxable income annually
- Must have 100+ shareholders
- Cannot be closely held by few owners

**Result:** REITs provide real estate returns without the capital, leverage, or management burden of direct ownership.

## REIT vs. Direct Property Ownership

### Comparison Matrix

| Feature | REITs | Direct Property Ownership |
|---------|-------|-------------------------|
| Minimum Capital | $1,000+ | $50,000+ (down payment) |
| Liquidity | Instant (market hours) | 3-6 months (sale process) |
| Leverage | Limited | 80% mortgage possible |
| Management | Professional | Your responsibility |
| Diversification | Hundreds of properties | Typically 1-3 properties |
| Dividend Yield | 3-6% typically | Varies (cash flow dependent) |
| Tax Efficiency | Ordinary income (less efficient) | 1031 exchanges, depreciation |
| Leverage Opportunity | Limited | Mortgage amplifies returns |

**REIT advantages:** Diversification, liquidity, passive management
**Direct property advantages:** Leverage, tax deductions, control

**Example:** Invest $50,000 in Realty Income at 5.3% yield

Annual income: $50,000 × 0.053 = $2,650

## REIT Tax Considerations

### Ordinary Income Treatment

REIT distributions are taxed as ordinary income, not capital gains. This is a major disadvantage vs. stock dividends.

**Example:** $2,650 annual REIT distributions

| Tax Status | Tax Rate | Tax Owed | After-Tax |
|-----------|----------|----------|-----------|
| Long-term capital gains | 15% | $398 | $2,252 |
| Qualified dividends | 15% | $398 | $2,252 |
| REIT distributions | Ordinary | $928 | $1,722 |

Same $2,650 income, but REIT treatment costs $530 more in taxes annually. Over 30 years, that's $27,000+ in additional taxes.

**Tax optimization:**
- Hold REITs in tax-deferred accounts (IRA, 401k)
- Use REITs for retirement income, not taxable accounts
- Consider non-qualified accounts only if no tax-deferred space available

### Depreciation Recapture

REITs claim depreciation internally. When you sell REIT shares at a profit, the IRS presumes the underlying properties appreciated due to superior management, not real depreciation. This creates "depreciation recapture" tax of 25% on capital gains.

**Example:** Buy REIT shares at $50, sell at $75

Normal capital gain tax: $25 × 15% = $3.75
With depreciation recapture: $25 × 25% = $6.25

This extra complexity favors long-term REIT holding in tax-deferred accounts.

## Yield Traps: REITs to Avoid

**High yield doesn't always mean good investment.**

### Red Flags for Distressed REITs

| Warning Sign | Example | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| 8%+ yield | Office REITs | Distribution exceeds sustainable income |
| Decreasing occupancy | Retail REITs | Fundamental property decline |
| Rising debt levels | Overleveraged REITs | Financial stress |
| Dividend cuts | Any REIT announcing cuts | Future pain coming |
| Property value declines | REITs with falling NAV | Market reassessment |

**Example:** Office REIT yielding 8.5% in March 2026

- Seems attractive vs. 4-5% yields elsewhere
- But office fundamentals deteriorating (remote work)
- High yield reflects market skepticism
- Distribution may not sustain—dividend cut risk high

**Lesson:** Compare REITs to their sector average. 8.5% yield in a 4% average sector signals distress, not opportunity.

## REIT Portfolio Rebalancing

### Annual Rebalancing Strategy

**Scenario:** Starting portfolio $50,000, 60% growth REITs / 40% income REITs

After strong stock market year:
- Growth REIT holdings: $31,000 (62%)
- Income REIT holdings: $39,000 (38%)

**Rebalancing action:**
1. Sell $1,000 of growth REITs
2. Buy $1,000 of income REITs
3. Return to 60/40 target

**Tax consideration:** In taxable accounts, use capital losses from underperformers to offset gains from winners.

## Conclusion: REITs as Income-Building Tools

REITs democratize real estate investing. A $50,000 investment in diversified REITs generates $2,000-$2,500 in annual distributions. Held in tax-deferred accounts and reinvested through DRIP, this compounds into $150,000-$200,000 over 20 years.

The key decisions:
1. **Allocation:** What percentage of your portfolio?
2. **Type:** Individual REITs or index funds?
3. **Account:** Tax-deferred (ideal) or taxable (avoid)?
4. **Sector:** Industrial, healthcare, retail, or diversified?
5. **Reinvestment:** DRIP or take distributions as income?

For passive income investors, 5-15% of portfolio allocation to quality REITs provides real estate diversification, inflation protection, and consistent distributions. Start with index funds for simplicity, then graduate to individual REIT selection as your expertise grows.
