## Understanding Total Return for Target-Date Funds

Total return measures your complete investment profit including price appreciation and dividend income. For young professionals using target-date funds, tracking total return validates whether your automatic allocation strategy builds sufficient retirement wealth.

## The Total Return Formula

**Total Return (%) = [(Ending Value - Beginning Value + Dividends) / Beginning Value] × 100**

**Annualized Return (CAGR) = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/Years) - 1**

### Worked Example: Target-Date Fund

Michael, age 28, invested $15,000 in Vanguard Target 2060 Fund:
- Beginning value: $15,000
- Ending value (3 years): $17,820
- Dividends/distributions: $540

Total Return = [($17,820 - $15,000 + $540) / $15,000] × 100 = **18.4%**

CAGR = ($18,360 / $15,000)^(1/3) - 1 = **5.7% annually**

## Calculating Target-Date Fund Total Return

### Step 1: Gather Your Data
- Initial investment amount and date
- Current fund value (check 401k or brokerage statement)
- All dividend/distribution payments received
- Contributions made during the period

### Step 2: Apply the Formula
For a target-date fund investment:
- **Automatic rebalancing**: Fund handles allocation shifts
- **Dividend accumulation**: Distributions reinvested or paid out
- **No active trading**: Set-and-forget approach

### Step 3: Calculate Annualized Return
With an 18.4% 3-year total return:
- Simple annual average: 18.4% ÷ 3 = 6.1%
- **True CAGR: 5.7%** (accounts for compounding)

## Factors Affecting Target-Date Fund Total Returns

### Market Conditions
- **Bull Markets**: Target-date funds deliver 10-15% annual returns
- **Bear Markets**: Target-date funds experience -5% to -20% drawdowns (lower volatility than pure stock funds)
- **Inflation Impact**: 3% inflation reduces real returns by 3 percentage points

### Expense Ratios
Compounding cost of fees over 30 years:
- VFFVX (0.10% expense ratio): $15,000 → $59,200
- Typical active target-date fund (0.75%): $15,000 → $53,400
- **Difference: $5,800** (9.8% lifetime drag)

### Glide Path Effectiveness
- Early years (90% stocks): Higher volatility, higher growth potential
- Late years (48% stocks): Lower volatility, income focus
- Automatic adjustment reduces behavioral errors

## FAQ

**Q: How often should I calculate total return for target-date funds?**
A: Annually during portfolio review. Quarterly checks often create emotional reactions to normal volatility.

**Q: Should I stay in target-date fund after retirement?**
A: Consider switching to Vanguard Target Retirement (post-retirement version) around age 60. These funds stop gliding and focus on stable income.

**Q: What's a "good" total return for target-date funds?**
A: Targets vary by fund type:
- Age 25-35 (2060 funds): 8-10% annually
- Age 35-45 (2045 funds): 7-9% annually
- Age 45-55 (2035 funds): 6-8% annually
- Age 55-65 (2025 funds): 4-6% annually

**Q: Should I be concerned if my target-date fund underperforms the S&P 500?**
A: No. Target-date funds include bonds and diversification by design. Compare against balanced benchmarks (60/40 or 70/30), not pure stock indices.

**Q: Can I change target-date funds if I switch jobs?**
A: Yes. If moving from one 401(k) to another, switch to matching target-date fund (e.g., 2060 to 2060). Consolidation reduces complexity.
