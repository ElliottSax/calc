## What is Dividend Yield?

Dividend yield represents the annual dividend payment expressed as a percentage of the current stock or ETF price. For income-focused investors, this fundamental metric indicates how much passive income you generate relative to your capital investment. Dividend yield tells you exactly what percentage annual cash return you receive from holding a security, enabling quick comparison across different investment options.

The formula is elegantly simple and powerful:

**Dividend Yield (%) = (Annual Dividend per Share / Current Share Price) × 100**

This straightforward calculation enables investors to quickly compare income potential across completely different securities, regardless of their absolute price or market capitalization.

### The Critical Relationship Between Price and Yield

An important principle in dividend investing that every investor must understand: when share prices rise, dividend yields fall (assuming the dividend remains constant), and conversely, when prices fall, yields rise. This inverse relationship creates both opportunities and risks depending on market conditions.

| Share Price | Annual Dividend | Calculated Yield |
|---|---|---|
| $60 | $3.00 | 5.0% |
| $70 | $3.00 | 4.3% |

| $90 | $3.00 | 3.3% |

This table demonstrates why dividend investors often get excited about market downturns—the same $3 annual dividend becomes worth proportionally more when the share price is lower. A $10 decline in share price increases yield from 3.75% to 4.3%, a meaningful increase of 0.55 percentage points that compounds over years.

### Tax Treatment of Dividend Income

Long-term dividends receive preferential tax treatment:
- Qualified dividends: 0%, 15%, or 20% based on income
- Non-qualified dividends: Ordinary income rates (10-37%)
- Tax-advantaged accounts: Deferred until withdrawal

This tax advantage makes dividend strategies more attractive on an after-tax basis.

### Commodity ETF Dividend Considerations

Commodity ETFs present unique dividend characteristics. Unlike stock dividends, commodity funds may not generate traditional dividends. Instead, they generate yields through:

- Interest from cash holdings
- Commodity contango structures
- Distribution policies
- Return of capital strategies

Understanding these mechanisms is crucial for accurate yield assessment in commodity investments.

### FAQ

**Q: Is higher yield always better?**
A: No. Sustainable 3% yields are preferable to unsustainable 6% yields that face eventual cuts.

**Q: How often do dividends typically pay?**
A: Most U.S. stocks and ETFs pay dividends quarterly, though some pay monthly or semi-annually.

**Q: Should I reinvest dividends or take them as income?**
A: If accumulating wealth (working), reinvest for compounding. If retired, spend as needed.

**Q: Can companies eliminate their dividends?**
A: Yes, absolutely. However, Dividend Aristocrats like KO rarely do after 60+ years of increases.

**Q: Do dividend-paying ETFs have higher yields than individual stocks?**
A: Often yes, through concentrated holdings of high-dividend companies with better diversification.

**Q: How does inflation affect dividend yields?**
A: Inflation erodes purchasing power. Companies raising dividends faster than inflation protect your real income.
