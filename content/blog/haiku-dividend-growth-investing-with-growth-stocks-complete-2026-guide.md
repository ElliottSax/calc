## Combining Dividend Growth with Growth Stocks

The optimal long-term strategy blends dividend growth stocks (providing income) with non-dividend growth stocks (providing appreciation). This combination captures the best of both approaches: reliable dividend income plus capital appreciation.

Dividend growers provide the foundation (70%), while growth stocks add acceleration (30%).

## Dividend Growth Stocks to Include

### Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)

**Key metrics:**
- Dividend yield: 3.5%
- Dividend growth streak: 61 years
- Price appreciation: 4-5% annually
- Total return: 7.5-8.5% expected
- Volatility: 12%

JNJ represents pharmaceutical and healthcare stability with consistent dividend increases. Core holding for any dividend growth portfolio.

### Procter & Gamble (PG)

**Key metrics:**
- Dividend yield: 2.5%
- Dividend growth streak: 67 years
- Consumer staples (cleaning, personal care)
- Price appreciation: 3-4% annually
- Total return: 5.5-6.5% expected

PG provides defensive exposure during downturns while maintaining dividend payments.

### Coca-Cola (KO)

**Key metrics:**
- Dividend yield: 3.0%
- Dividend growth streak: 60 years
- Beverage and non-alcoholic drink focus
- Price appreciation: 4-5% annually
- Total return: 7-8% expected

KO combines strong dividend with international growth exposure (65% of revenue overseas).

### Merck & Co (MRK)

**Key metrics:**
- Dividend yield: 3.2%
- Dividend growth streak: 32 years
- Pharmaceutical and vaccine focus
- Price appreciation: 5-6% annually
- Total return: 8.2-9.2% expected

MRK offers higher growth potential among dividend stocks due to patent pipeline.

## Tax Efficiency: Dividend vs Growth

### Tax Rates Comparison

**Dividend stock gains (JNJ, KO, etc.):**
- Qualified dividends: 15% (most investors)
- Long-term capital gains: 15%
- Blended tax impact: ~15%

**Growth stock gains (NVDA, QQQ, etc.):**
- Dividends: 0% (minimal or none)
- Long-term capital gains: 15% (if held long-term)
- Short-term capital gains: 37% (if traded actively)
- Blended tax impact: 15% (long-term), 37% (short-term)

**Tax advantage: Holding both long-term eliminates tax difference**

In taxable accounts:
- Dividend stocks: Pay annual tax on dividends (15%)
- Growth stocks: Defer taxes until sale (15%)
- Roth IRA: Both tax-free

### Taxable Account Strategy

**Hold in taxable accounts:**
- Growth stocks (defer taxes on appreciation)
- Use specific lot identification for tax-loss harvesting

**Hold in Roth IRA:**
- Dividend stocks (tax-free reinvestment)
- Growth stocks (tax-free appreciation)

This account-placement strategy optimizes tax efficiency.

## Handling Stock Splits and Dividend Changes

### Stock Splits

When dividend/growth stocks split (e.g., 2-for-1):
- Automatic: Shares double, price halves
- No tax event: Dividend equivalent
- Your holding value unchanged

Example: Own 100 JNJ at $160 = $16,000
After 2-for-1 split: Own 200 JNJ at $80 = $16,000

### Dividend Increases

Dividend growth creates natural rebalancing:
- JNJ increases dividend 6% annually
- KO increases dividend 5% annually
- Combined effect: Portfolio dividend yield creeps higher
- Monitor to ensure 70/30 allocation maintained

**Example: Year 1 dividend = $2,100; Year 5 dividend = $4,050**
That $1,950 increase partly offsets growth/dividend drift.

## Managing Market Cycles with Dual Allocation

### Bull Market Behavior

- Dividend stocks: +8-10% annually
- Growth stocks: +15-20% annually
- Portfolio drifts to 70/30 → 65/35 (overweighted growth)
- Rebalance: Sell growth, buy dividend

### Bear Market Behavior

- Dividend stocks: -15-20% decline
- Growth stocks: -30-40% decline
- Portfolio drifts to 70/30 → 75/25 (overweighted dividend)
- Action: Hold; growth recovery will rebalance

### Sideways Market

- Both stagnate; dividend provides income
- Dividend portfolio returns: ~3% (from yield)
- Growth portfolio returns: ~0% + volatility
- Dividend advantage clear

## FAQ

**Is 70/30 too conservative if I'm young?**
Only if you can stomach 35%+ declines without panic selling. Most young investors benefit from dividend stability.

**Should I use individual stocks or ETFs for each portion?**
ETFs (SCHD for dividend, QQQ for growth) are simpler and equally effective.

**How often should I rebalance?**
Annually or when drift exceeds 5%. Quarterly review sufficient to monitor.

**What if growth stocks outperform significantly?**
Still rebalance. Prevent portfolio from becoming too growth-concentrated.

**Can I skip the growth portion and stay 100% dividend?**
Yes, if you prefer simpler approach. 100% dividend (8% return) vs 70/30 (9.5% return) is modest difference.
