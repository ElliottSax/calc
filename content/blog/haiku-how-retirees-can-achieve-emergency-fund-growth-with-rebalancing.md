## Strategic Rebalancing

Rebalancing is the process of buying and selling assets to restore your portfolio to target allocations. As markets fluctuate, your allocation drifts—rebalancing realigns it, forcing discipline of buying low and selling high.

### Why Rebalancing Matters

Markets create natural allocation drift:
- Bull markets inflate stock percentages above targets
- Bear markets reduce stock percentages below targets
- Without rebalancing, your portfolio becomes unintentionally aggressive/conservative

Rebalancing captures returns by selling winners (capturing gains) and buying losers (purchasing undervalued assets).

### Rebalancing Frequency Trade-offs

| Frequency | Discipline | Cost | Tax Impact | Best For |
|-----------|-----------|------|-----------|----------|
| Quarterly | Very high | Moderate | Moderate | Tax-advantaged |
| Semi-annually | High | Low | Low | Moderate accounts |
| Annually | Moderate | Low | Low | Most investors |
| Threshold | Very high | Variable | Optimizable | Tax-aware |

### Real-World Rebalancing Example

A high-income earner with 60/40 allocation and $300,000:

**Target:** $180,000 stocks / $120,000 bonds

**After 5-year bull market (stocks +70%, bonds +10%):**
- Stocks: $306,000 (70.4% of portfolio)
- Bonds: $132,000 (29.6% of portfolio)
- Total: $438,000

**Without rebalancing:**
- Risk exposure drifted 10.4% too aggressive
- Vulnerable to market corrections

**With rebalancing:**
- Sell $59,300 stocks, buy $59,300 bonds
- Restore to 60/40: $262,800 stocks / $175,200 bonds
- **Benefit:** Locks in stock gains, deploys to undervalued bonds

### Rebalancing Methods

**Calendar-based (Easiest):**
- Rebalance annually on fixed date
- Pro: Predictable, low effort
- Con: Doesn't respond to extremes

**Threshold-based (Best):**
- Rebalance when allocation drifts >5%
- Pro: Captures extremes, tax-efficient
- Con: Requires monitoring

**Opportunistic (Sophisticated):**
- Deploy new contributions to underweighted assets
- Reinvest dividends to rebalance
- Harvest losses and redeploy
- Pro: Tax and cost efficient

### 10-Year Rebalancing Example

50-year-old maintaining 70/30 with $500,000:

**Years 1-3: Bull market (stocks +25%, bonds +5%)**
- Rebalancing: Sell stocks, buy bonds
- **Benefit: Locks gains**

**Years 4-5: Bear market (stocks -20%, bonds +2%)**
- Rebalancing: Sell bonds, buy stocks
- **Benefit: Buys at low prices**

**Years 6-10: Recovery (stocks +40%, bonds +8%)**
- Undervalued stocks appreciate significantly
- **Cumulative benefit: 0.5-0.7% annual outperformance**

### Tax-Efficient Rebalancing

- Use new contributions to rebalance first
- Reinvest dividends into underweighted assets
- Tax-loss harvest when rebalancing
- Defer rebalancing in taxable accounts (use tax-advantaged first)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: How much do I need to invest annually to build $1 million?

Using the future value formula at 8% annual return:

- Starting with $100,000, investing $3,000/month: 11 years

### Q: What if the market crashes right after I invest?

Market crashes are normal and expected. Historical data shows:
- Markets experience 20%+ declines every 3-5 years
- Full recovery within 1-2 years typically
- Missing the best days costs 50% of returns
- Staying fully invested beats 90% of active traders

### Q: Should I try to time the market?

No. Data overwhelmingly shows:
- 70% of market returns occur in just 5% of trading days
- Missing top 10 days costs 50% of 30-year returns
- Market timing almost always underperforms
- Stay invested. Automate. Ignore daily noise.

### Q: When should I start withdrawing (4% rule)?

The 4% rule states: In retirement, withdraw 4% of your portfolio annually.

Example:
- Portfolio at retirement: $2,000,000
- Annual withdrawal: $2,000,000 × 0.04 = $80,000/year
- Historical success: 95% of 30-year retirements

Only start withdrawing when your portfolio reaches 25× your annual expenses.

### Q: Should I adjust my allocation as I age?

Yes. Use the simple rule: **Equity % = 110 - Your Age**

Examples:
- Age 35: 75% stocks, 25% bonds
- Age 45: 65% stocks, 35% bonds
- Age 55: 55% stocks, 45% bonds
- Age 65: 45% stocks, 55% bonds
- Age 75: 35% stocks, 65% bonds

This automatically becomes more conservative as you have fewer years to recover from downturns.
