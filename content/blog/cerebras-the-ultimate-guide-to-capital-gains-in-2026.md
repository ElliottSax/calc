## What Are Capital Gains?

Capital gains are the profits realized when you sell an asset—such as stocks, ETFs, or real estate—for more than its purchase price. There are two types:

1. **Short-term capital gains** – Apply to assets held for one year or less. Taxed at ordinary income tax rates (up to 37% in 2026, depending on income).
2. **Long-term capital gains** – Apply to assets held for more than one year. Taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20% in 2026).

For example, if you bought 100 shares of **Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)** at $150 per share in January 2024 and sold them in March 2025 at $180, your capital gain is:

```
(180 - 150) * 100 = $3,000
```

Since you held the stock for over a year, this qualifies as a long-term gain.

Short-term gains in 2026 will be taxed at ordinary income rates, which top out at 37% for single filers earning over $650,000 and married couples over $750,000.

### Example: Tax Impact on a $20,000 Gain

Consider an investor selling shares of the **Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO)** after holding them for 18 months. Their taxable income is $90,000 (single filer).

- **Gain realized**: $20,000
- **Filing status**: Single
- **Taxable income including gain**: $110,000

Since $110,000 exceeds the $47,025 0% threshold but is under $518,900, the entire $20,000 is taxed at **15%**:

```
$20,000 × 0.15 = $3,000 in capital gains tax
```

Compare this to a short-term gain: if the same profit came from a stock held 10 months, it would be taxed at the marginal income rate—**24%** for income between $47,151 and $100,525—resulting in $4,800 in tax.

**Savings from long-term holding: $1,800.**

## How Inflation Adjustments Affect Gains in 2026

The IRS adjusts cost basis for inflation on certain assets using **"stepped-up basis"** rules, but most equities do not benefit from automatic inflation indexing. However, investors can use **tax-loss harvesting** and **basis averaging** to mitigate inflation-driven nominal gains.

For example, if inflation runs at 2.8% annually from 2023 to 2026, a $10,000 investment in **Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF (SCHD)** grows to $10,864 in nominal terms—yet real growth is only ~$600. Still, the IRS taxes the full $864 gain unless offset.

## Top 5 Strategies to Minimize Capital Gains in 2026

### 1. Hold Assets >1 Year for Long-Term Rates

Timing your sales strategically can slash your tax bill. For instance, if you bought **Coca-Cola (KO)** in November 2024, delaying sale until February 2026 (vs. December 2025) converts a short-term gain to long-term.

| Holding Period | $10,000 Gain Tax (Single, $85k Income) |
|----------------|----------------------------------------|
| <1 year (short-term) | $2,400 (24% bracket)          |
| >1 year (long-term)  | $1,500 (15% rate)             |
| **Savings**           | **$900**                        |

### 2. Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Gains in **IRAs or 401(k)s** are not subject to capital gains taxes. Rebalance within these accounts freely.

Example: Selling VOO within a Roth IRA incurs **$0 tax**, regardless of gain size.

### 3. Tax-Loss Harvesting

Offset gains with losses. In 2026, you can deduct up to $3,000 in net capital losses against ordinary income, with excess carried forward.

Suppose you have:
- $15,000 gain from selling VTI
- $7,000 loss from selling an underperforming tech stock

Net gain: $8,000  
Tax saved: $7,000 × 15% = **$1,050**

### 4. Donate Appreciated Stock to Charity

Instead of selling stock and donating cash, donate shares directly. You avoid capital gains tax and deduct the fair market value.

Example: Donate $50,000 in JNJ stock (basis: $20,000):
- **No capital gains tax** on $30,000 gain
- **$50,000 charitable deduction**
- Combined tax savings: ~$13,500 (federal only, assuming 27% effective rate)

### 5. Use the 0% Capital Gains Bracket

Low- and middle-income investors can pay **0%** on long-term gains if total taxable income stays below threshold.

For a married couple filing jointly in 2026:
- **Max income for 0% rate**: $94,050
- **Strategy**: Withdraw just enough from taxable accounts to stay under threshold.

Example: Couple with $80,000 in pension income sells $14,000 of SCHD with $5,000 gain. Total income: $85,000 → **0% capital gains tax**.

## Capital Gains and Dividend Taxes: A Critical Distinction

Many investors confuse capital gains with dividends. They are taxed differently:

| Income Type | Tax Rate (2026, Long-Term) | Example Asset |
|-------------|-----------------------------|---------------|
| Qualified Dividends | 0%, 15%, or 20% (same as LTCG) | SCHD, VOO, JNJ, KO |
| Ordinary Dividends | Ordinary income rates (up to 37%) | REITs, some preferred shares |
| Capital Gains | As above | Any appreciated security |

**Key point**: Dividends from **SCHD** are mostly qualified, taxed at 15% for middle-income investors. But reinvested dividends increase cost basis, reducing future capital gains.

Example: You buy 100 shares of SCHD at $80 ($8,000 basis). Over 5 years, reinvest $2,000 in dividends. New basis: **$10,000**. Sell at $110/share:  
Gain = (100 × $110) – $10,000 = **$1,000**, not $3,000.

## Capital Gains Reporting: What You Need in 2026

Brokers will issue **Form 1099-B** by February 2027 for 2026 trades. Key data includes:
- Proceeds from sale
- Cost basis (if available)
- Holding period
- Gain/loss amount

Use **IRS Form 8949** and **Schedule D** to report. Mismatches between broker data and your records can trigger audits.

**Pro tip**: Track cost basis manually if using DRIPs or inherited shares. Software like **TurboTax**, **RightCapital**, or **Brokerage Tax Reports** can help.

## FAQ: Capital Gains in 2026

### Q1: Will capital gains tax rates change in 2026?

As of 2024, no automatic changes are scheduled. The current 0%/15%/20% structure remains unless Congress acts. However, proposals to raise the top rate to 28% or tax wealth more aggressively could gain traction post-2024 elections.

### Q2: Are gains from ETFs like VTI taxed the same as individual stocks?

Yes. Whether you sell shares of **VOO**, **VTI**, or **Apple (AAPL)**, the gain is treated identically based on holding period and income level.

### Q3: How does the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) apply in 2026?

High earners (individuals >$200k, married >$250k) pay an additional 3.8% on capital gains. In 2026, a single filer with $220,000 income selling VOO for a $15,000 gain would pay:
- 20% federal rate: $3,000
- 3.8% NIIT: $570
- **Total: $3,570**

### Q4: Can I avoid capital gains by reinvesting in another stock?

No. The IRS does not allow "like-kind" exchanges for stocks (unlike real estate under Section 1031). Selling triggers a taxable event regardless of reinvestment.

### Q5: What’s the best account type to hold growth stocks?

For high-growth assets like **VOO** or **VTI**, use **taxable brokerage accounts** if you plan to hold long-term (to benefit from lower capital gains rates). For frequent traders, use **tax-deferred accounts** (IRA/401k) to avoid annual taxation.

### Q6: How does state tax affect capital gains in 2026?

State rates vary widely:
- **California**: Up to 13.3%
- **Texas/Florida**: 0%
- **New York**: 10.9% (combined local + state)

A $10,000 gain in California (high earner) could cost **$20% federal + 13.3% state = $3,330**.

### Q7: Do dividends increase my capital gains tax?

Reinvested dividends increase your **cost basis**, which *reduces* capital gains when you sell. Not tracking them leads to **overpayment** of tax.

### Q8: What happens if I sell at a loss?

You can use capital losses to offset gains. Excess losses ($3,000/year limit against income) carry forward indefinitely.
