## What Is Dividend Growth?

Dividend growth refers to the annual increase in dividend payments issued by a company or fund. Unlike high-yield stocks that pay large but stagnant dividends, dividend growth stocks consistently raise their payouts, often outpacing inflation and compounding investor returns.

For example, Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) has raised its dividend for **61 consecutive years**, increasing its payout from $0.59 per share in 2013 to $4.76 per share in 2024 — a **CAGR of 6.3%**. This steady growth transforms a modest income stream into a powerful wealth engine.

Dividend growth is driven by:
- Rising earnings per share (EPS)
- Strong free cash flow generation
- Management commitment to shareholder returns
- Pricing power and competitive advantage

## Key Metrics for Evaluating Dividend Growth Stocks

To identify sustainable dividend growers, focus on these five metrics:

| Metric | Ideal Threshold | Why It Matters |
|--------|------------------|--------------|
| Dividend Growth Rate (5-year CAGR) | ≥ 5% | Indicates consistent payout increases |
| Payout Ratio (Free Cash Flow) | ≤ 60% | Ensures dividends are covered by cash flow |
| EPS Growth (3-year CAGR) | ≥ 6% | Foundational to future dividend hikes |
| ROE | ≥ 15% | Reflects capital efficiency and profitability |
| Debt/EBITDA | ≤ 2.5x | Low leverage supports financial flexibility |

*Source: S&P Global, FactSet, 2025 Projections*

## The Power of Compounding: A Real-World Example

Consider two investors in 2016:
- **Investor A** buys $10,000 of a 2% yielding stock with **0% dividend growth**.
- **Investor B** buys $10,000 of a 2% yielding stock with **7% annual dividend growth**.

Here’s how their annual income evolves:

| Year | Investor A Income | Investor B Income |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 2016 | $200 | $200 |
| 2020 | $200 | $260 |
| 2024 | $200 | $371 |
| 2026 | $200 | $429 |

By 2026, Investor B earns **115% more income** despite starting at the same yield. This is the power of compounding dividend growth.

Now, scale this to a $500,000 portfolio:
- Investor A: $10,000/year
- Investor B: $21,450/year

The gap widens over time, making dividend growth essential for long-term financial independence.

## Risks to Dividend Growth in 2026

No strategy is risk-free. Key threats to dividend growth in 2026 include:

1. **Recession Risk**: A GDP contraction in H2 2026 could pressure earnings and force dividend cuts. Energy and Industrials are most vulnerable.
2. **Interest Rate Spikes**: If inflation rebounds and the Fed hikes rates above 5.5%, high-dividend stocks may underperform.
3. **Overvaluation**: SCHD trades at a P/E of 21.5x — above its 10-year average of 18.7x. A correction could erode capital.
4. **Single-Stock Risk**: Overconcentration in one sector (e.g., tech) increases volatility.

Mitigate these risks with:
- Diversification across sectors and caps
- Focus on payout ratios and cash flow
- Holding 5–10% in cash for opportunistic buys

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### 1. What is the average dividend growth rate in 2026?

The S&P 500 is expected to deliver **6.8% dividend growth** in 2026, up from 5.9% in 2024. Technology and Healthcare are leading the charge.

### 2. Are dividend growth stocks safer than high-yield stocks?

Generally, **yes**. High-yield stocks (e.g., 8%+) often carry high payout ratios and financial risk. Dividend growth stocks prioritize sustainability and earnings support. For example, AT&T (T) cut its dividend in 2022 after years of aggressive payouts.

### 3. How much should I allocate to dividend growth stocks?

Financial planners recommend **40–60% of equity exposure** in dividend growth assets for investors within 10–15 years of retirement. Younger investors may allocate 20–30% and increase over time.

### 4. Can dividend growth beat inflation?

Yes. Since 1970, the S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats Index has delivered **5.2% average annual dividend growth**, outpacing average inflation of **3.9%**. In 2026, with inflation at 2.8%, even 5% growth provides real income gains.

### 5. What’s the difference between dividend growth and dividend aristocrats?

- **Dividend Growth**: Any stock increasing its payout, regardless of duration.
- **Dividend Aristocrats**: S&P 500 companies with **25+ consecutive years** of dividend increases. Only 65 firms qualify (e.g., JNJ, KO, PG).

### 6. Should I reinvest dividends?

**Absolutely**. Reinvesting dividends increases share count and accelerates compounding. A $10,000 investment in SCHD in 2011 would be worth **$42,500** with reinvestment vs. **$28,100** without — a 51% difference.

### 7. Is it too late to start dividend growth investing in 2026?

No. With dividend growth averaging **6–8% annually** and market valuations near fair value, 2026 is an excellent entry point. Starting now allows you to capture years of compounding.
