## State and Local Tax (SALT) Considerations

Municipal bond tax treatment varies by state:

- **Federal tax-free**: All investors get federal tax exemption
- **State tax-free**: Only if you invest in your home state's bonds
- **AMT consideration**: Alternative Minimum Tax can reduce benefits

**High-Income Investor Analysis (California resident):**

Like all bonds, municipal bonds face interest rate risk:

**VTEB duration: 6.2 years**

If rates rise 1%:
- VTEB price falls approximately 6.2%
- $100,000 position: Falls to $93,800

If rates fall 1%:
- VTEB price rises approximately 6.2%
- $100,000 position: Rises to $106,200

Investors planning to hold to maturity avoid this risk. Investors needing to sell face mark-to-market losses if rates have risen.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Are municipal bonds safe for a conservative portfolio?**
A: VTEB holds 5,000+ bonds across 50 states—very safe. Diversified funds eliminate concentration risk entirely.

**Q2: Should I buy individual municipal bonds or VTEB?**
A: For investors under $250,000, VTEB (low minimums, diversification, tax-loss harvesting). For larger amounts, consider laddering individual bonds for predictability.

**Q3: Do I need to pay state income tax on out-of-state municipal bonds?**
A: Yes, typically. Federal exemption applies, but state taxes usually apply to out-of-state bonds. VTEB's diversification across states reduces overall tax liability.

**Q4: What's the difference between VTEB and municipal bond CEFs?**
A: ETFs (VTEB) charge 0.05% annually with daily pricing. CEFs often yield 5-6% but charge 0.7-1% expenses plus premium/discount volatility. ETFs win for most investors.

**Q5: Can I use municipal bonds in my 401k?**
A: Yes, but inefficient. You lose the tax exemption. Use taxable accounts for municipal bonds, tax-deferred accounts for taxable bonds.
