# Understanding Dividend Growth for Long-term Growth

Dividend growth investing represents a proven strategy for building wealth through regular income that compounds over decades. Companies that consistently increase dividends—like Coca-Cola (KO), Procter & Gamble (PG), and Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)—deliver both income and capital appreciation. This guide explains how dividend growth works mathematically and demonstrates real calculations using actual dividend histories.

## What Are Dividends and Dividend Growth?

A dividend is a payment made by a corporation to its shareholders, typically quarterly. Dividend growth occurs when a company increases its dividend payment year after year, rewarding long-term shareholders with rising income.

**The Dividend Growth Formula:**

**Annual Dividend Income = Shares Owned × Dividend Per Share**

**Future Dividend Income = Current Dividend × (1 + Growth Rate)^n**

Where:
- Current Dividend = Today's annual dividend per share
- Growth Rate = Dividend increase percentage annually
- n = Number of years

## Dividend Yield and Yield on Cost

Dividend yield measures the current income payment as a percentage of stock price.

**Current Yield Formula:**
**Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend / Current Stock Price × 100**

**Example (JNJ):**
- Current price: $155
- Annual dividend: $6.39
- Dividend yield: $6.39 / $155 × 100 = **4.1%**

However, long-term shareholders experience "yield on cost"—the dividend as a percentage of their original purchase price.

**Yield on Cost Example:**
- Original purchase price (1999): $50
- Current dividend (2024): $6.39
- Yield on cost: $6.39 / $50 × 100 = **12.8%**

An investor who bought JNJ at $50 in 1999 now receives 12.8% annual income on their original investment, far exceeding current market yields.

## Payout Ratio and Dividend Safety

The payout ratio indicates dividend sustainability—what percentage of earnings companies pay as dividends.

**Payout Ratio Formula:**
**Payout Ratio = Annual Dividend / Earnings Per Share × 100**

**Safe Payout Ratio Ranges:**
- Below 50%: Strong dividend safety, room for growth
- 50-75%: Reasonable safety, moderate growth potential
- Above 75%: Higher risk, less growth potential

**Examples:**
- JNJ: ~65% payout ratio (healthy, sustainable growth)
- KO: ~78% payout ratio (mature company, modest growth)
- PG: ~72% payout ratio (stable, consistent increases)

Companies with payout ratios below 50% can raise dividends faster without straining finances.

## Tax Efficiency of Dividend Income

Dividend tax treatment varies by dividend type, making tax planning important.

**Qualified Dividend Taxation (Lower Rate):**
- Long-term holdings (>60 days)
- Taxed at capital gains rates: 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income
- Applies to most corporate dividends

**Non-Qualified Dividend Taxation:**
- Short-term holdings
- Taxed as ordinary income at your tax bracket rate
- Less tax-efficient

**Tax Comparison Example:**
- Dividend income: $10,000 annually
- Tax bracket: 24% ordinary income

**Qualified dividend scenario:**
- Tax at 15% capital gains rate: $1,500
- After-tax income: $8,500

**Non-qualified dividend scenario:**
- Tax at 24% ordinary rate: $2,400
- After-tax income: $7,600

**Tax savings through qualified status: $900 annually, or $27,000 over 30 years**

## FAQ

**Q: What dividend growth rate should I expect?**
A: Mature dividend growers average 5-8% annually. JNJ and PG average 9-14% over long periods. Newer dividend payers may exceed 10%, but consistency matters more than high single-year growth.

**Q: Should I buy stocks or SCHD?**
A: SCHD offers simplicity and diversification. Individual stocks allow customization but require research. Starting with SCHD and learning individual companies is a balanced approach.

**Q: When do dividend payments occur?**
A: Most U.S. stocks pay quarterly dividends. The ex-dividend date (when you must own shares) typically occurs one day before the record date. Dividends usually arrive 1-2 weeks after.

**Q: Can dividend stocks lose value?**
A: Yes. Stock prices fluctuate independently of dividends. However, dividend growers historically recover quickly from downturns due to income appeal.

**Q: How much do I need to start dividend investing?**
A: Most brokers allow stock purchases at any price level. SCHD shares cost ~$75 each. Even $100-200 monthly purchases build substantial wealth over decades.
