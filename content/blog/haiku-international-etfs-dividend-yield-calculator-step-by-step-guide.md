**US Investor receives UK dividend (GBP):**

## Tax Considerations for International Dividends

International dividends face multiple tax layers:

**Step 1: Withholding tax at source**
UK dividend from £1,000: 15% UK withholding = £850 net dividend

**Step 2: Foreign tax credit (US residents)**
Report withholding tax on Form 1040 as tax credit

**Step 3: Ordinary income tax (US)**
Additional US tax on £850 dividend (up to 37% depending on bracket)

**Example: $100 UK dividend**
- Original: $100
- UK withholding (15%): -$15
- Received: $85
- US tax (37% bracket): -$31.45
- After-tax income: $53.55

Effective tax rate: 46.45% (significantly higher than US dividend rates of 20-37%).

## Currency-Hedged vs. Unhedged ETFs

Investors can choose currency exposure:

**Unhedged VXUS ($50,000 position):**
- Dividend yield: 2.1% = $1,050
- Currency exposure: Full exposure to foreign currency movements

**Currency-hedged VSP (Vanguard International Hedged):**
- Dividend yield: 1.8% = $900
- Currency exposure: Minimal (hedged to USD)
- Cost: 0.3% annual hedging expense

Hedging reduces currency volatility but eliminates upside from favorable currency moves.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Should I use hedged or unhedged international ETFs?**
A: Unhedged for long-term investors. Currency diversification benefits outweigh hedging costs over 10+ years.

**Q2: Can I use foreign tax credits for international dividends?**
A: Yes, if eligible. Consult tax professional—rules are complex and situation-dependent.

**Q3: Are international dividend stocks safer than US stocks?**
A: No. International stocks have different risks (currency, political, emerging market volatility).

**Q4: What's the difference between VXUS and VEA?**
A: VXUS includes emerging markets (45% allocation). VEA is developed markets only.

**Q5: Should I prefer developed or emerging market dividends?**
A: Developed markets (UK, Australia) offer higher yields with less political risk. Emerging markets offer growth with higher dividend risk.
