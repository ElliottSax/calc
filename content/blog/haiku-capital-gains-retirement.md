# Understanding Capital Gains for Retirement

Capital gains—the appreciation between purchase price and sale price—represent a significant portion of long-term investment returns. Understanding capital gains taxation, strategic realization timing, and integration with retirement income planning determines whether you keep 70% or 90% of your investment profits. For retirees, capital gains management directly impacts lifestyle sustainability. This guide explains capital gains fundamentals, tax-efficient strategies, and retirement integration.

## Capital Gains Basics: Earned vs. Realized

A critical distinction determines tax obligation:

**Unrealized Gains:**
You own stock purchased at $50/share, now worth $100/share. The $50 gain exists but creates zero tax obligation. As long as you hold, no tax applies.

**Realized Gains:**
You sell the stock at $100/share. The $50 gain becomes real (realized), triggering tax obligation.

This distinction enables powerful tax strategies:

A $500,000 portfolio appreciating to $750,000 generates zero tax while held, despite $250,000 in gains. Taxes only trigger upon sale.

## Capital Gains Tax Rates: 2026 Environment

The United States taxes capital gains favorably compared to ordinary income:

**Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG: Held 1+ year):**

| Federal Bracket | Income | Capital Gains Rate |
|-----------------|--------|-------------------|
| 10% | <$11,600 | 0% |
| 12% | $11,600-$47,150 | 0% |
| 22% | $47,150-$100,525 | 15% |
| 24% | $100,525-$191,950 | 15% |
| 32% | $191,950-$243,725 | 20% |
| 35% | $243,725-$609,350 | 20% |
| 37% | >$609,350 | 20% |

**Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG: Held <1 year):**
Taxed as ordinary income (10-37% depending on bracket)

**Impact Example:**
Sell $100,000 of appreciated stock:
- Long-term (held 1+ year): $15,000 tax (15% LTCG rate in 24% bracket)
- Short-term (held <1 year): $24,000 tax (24% ordinary rate)
- Difference: $9,000 saved simply by holding 1+ year

This explains why long-term holding matters for tax efficiency.

## Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA): Concentrated Position Strategy

An advanced but critical strategy for retirees with concentrated positions:

**Scenario:** Age 62, retired, holding 1,000 shares of company stock (purchased 20 years ago at $50, now $200):

**Normal Approach (Taxable):**
Sell all shares at $200
- Proceeds: $200,000
- Cost basis: $50,000
- Capital gain: $150,000
- Tax (20% LTCG rate): $30,000
- After-tax proceeds: $170,000

**NUA Strategy (Tax-Efficient):**
1. Leave stock in 401k as long as possible
2. At retirement, perform Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) transfer
3. Transfer shares at cost basis ($50,000) to taxable account
4. Hold shares outside 401k for long-term capital gains treatment

Tax advantage:
- Initial transfer: No tax (administrative transfer)
- Upon future sale: Tax on appreciation only
- Appreciated portion ($150,000) taxed at capital gains rate

This can save $5,000-10,000+ depending on situation. Consult tax professional before executing.

## Capital Gains Harvesting: The Opposite of Tax-Loss Harvesting

While tax-loss harvesting saves taxes by realizing losses, capital gains harvesting strategically realizes gains in low-income years:

**Normal Approach:**
Retirement income sources:
- Social Security: $25,000
- Pension: $30,000
- Dividends: $20,000
- Total: $75,000

Realizing capital gains:
- Year's income: $75,000
- Additional gains: $30,000 realized
- New total: $105,000
- Tax bracket: 24% federal
- Capital gains tax: $4,500-6,000

**Capital Gains Harvesting Strategy:**

Years 1-5 (Low Income):
- Social Security: $25,000
- Pension: $30,000
- Total: $55,000 (below full phase-in of 24% bracket)
- Available capacity: ~$45,000 before entering higher bracket

Realize $40,000 in capital gains:
- New total: $95,000 (still in 24% bracket)
- Capital gains tax: $6,000 (15% LTCG rate for this income level)
- Effective rate: 15% (better than 20-24% in higher years)

This accelerates gains realization in low-income years, reducing lifetime tax burden.

## The Step-Up in Basis: The Ultimate Tax Benefit

For wealth preservation and inheritance, the step-up in basis represents the most powerful capital gains tax tool:

**Traditional Estate (No Step-Up):**
Investor purchases stock at $100, holds until death at $500 value.
Heir sells immediately at $500:
- Cost basis: $100
- Capital gain: $400
- Tax (20% LTCG rate): $80
- After-tax proceeds: $420

**Estate with Step-Up in Basis:**
Same situation, but law changes cost basis to market value at death:
- New cost basis: $500 (stepped up from $100)
- Sale price: $500
- Capital gain: $0
- Tax: $0
- Proceeds: $500

The step-up eliminates $80 in taxes (20% of appreciated value). For large estates, step-ups can eliminate hundreds of thousands in capital gains taxes.

**Strategy Implication:**
For retirees with appreciated holdings, deferring sales before death preserves value for heirs. A $1 million appreciated position with $500,000 gain becomes $250,000 in taxes if sold, but zero taxes if held until step-up.

This explains why dividend-focused strategies (minimal selling) benefit retirees: defer gains, enjoy dividends, pass appreciated assets to heirs at stepped-up basis.

## Capital Gains in Retirement Income Planning

### The 4% Rule and Capital Gains

The standard retirement rule suggests withdrawing 4% of portfolio value annually:

$1,000,000 portfolio × 4% = $40,000 annual withdrawal

**Composition matters:**

Option 1 - All Dividends:
- $40,000 annual dividend income (no capital gains)
- Tax at 20% LTCG rate: $8,000
- After-tax income: $32,000

Option 2 - Dividend + Capital Gains:
- $20,000 dividends
- $20,000 capital gains realized from appreciation
- Total: $40,000 (same amount)
- Tax at 20% LTCG rate: $8,000
- After-tax income: $32,000

Both approaches deliver identical after-tax results. The composition (dividends vs. gains) matters less than the total amount.

### Sequencing Capital Gains: Which Holdings to Sell

When retirement requires capital gains realization, strategic sequencing matters:

**Positions held 1+ year:** Sell first (20% LTCG rate)
**Positions held <1 year:** Sell last (24-37% ordinary rate)

By prioritizing long-held positions, you minimize tax drag.

**Real Example:**

Retirees need $60,000 income from $900,000 portfolio:
- $30,000 dividend income (already taken)
- Need $30,000 from capital gains

Holdings available:
- Position A: $100,000 value, $80,000 gain, held 18 months
- Position B: $100,000 value, $70,000 gain, held 4 months
- Position C: $80,000 value, $50,000 gain, held 15 months

Sale order:
1. Sell Position A ($100,000, gain $80,000 taxable at 20% = $16,000 tax)
   Result: $84,000 proceeds, keep $84,000 after tax
2. If more needed, sell Position C (held long-term, favorable rates)
3. Avoid Position B until necessary (short-term gains taxed at 24-37%)

This sequencing minimizes total capital gains taxes by realizing long-term gains first.

## Medicare Premium Impacts: Hidden Capital Gains Taxes

A frequently overlooked consequence: capital gains increase Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI), triggering higher Medicare premiums:

**Medicare Premium Impact (2026):**

| MAGI | Beneficiary Pays | % Increase |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| <$97,000 | Standard | 0% |
| $97,001-$121,250 | Standard × 1.35 | +35% |
| $121,251-$194,000 | Standard × 1.85 | +85% |
| $194,001+ | Standard × 2.85 | +185% |

**Practical Example:**
Beneficiary age 65 with Social Security + dividends totaling $80,000 MAGI (standard Medicare premium).

Realizes $30,000 capital gain:
- New MAGI: $110,000
- Medicare premium tier: +35% surcharge
- Part B monthly: $175 → $236 (+$61/month, $732/year)
- Part D monthly: $42 → $57 (+$15/month, $180/year)
- Annual cost: $912 extra just from Medicare surcharge

Capital gains create hidden taxes beyond direct capital gains taxation.

**Strategy:** Manage capital gains realization timing to minimize MAGI spikes. Spread large gains over multiple years if possible.

## Tax-Loss Harvesting in Retirement

Tax-loss harvesting remains powerful during retirement:

**Example:**
Retiree with $1,200 ordinary income (Social Security, pension, dividends) realizing:
- Long-term capital gain: $40,000
- Tax on gain: $6,000 (15% LTCG rate)

During year, position declines $45,000:
- Harvest loss: -$45,000
- Gain after loss: $40,000 - $45,000 = -$5,000 (net loss)
- Tax result: Carry forward $5,000 loss to next year
- Current year tax: $0 (no capital gain to tax)

Harvesting saves $6,000 in current year taxes and preserves $5,000 loss for future years.

**Retirement Tax-Loss Harvesting Strategy:**
Review portfolio quarterly. When positions decline 15%+, harvest losses to offset gains. This "free" tax management saves thousands over retirement.

## Wash-Sale Rule: The 30-Day Trap

Tax-loss harvesting has one major restriction: the 30-day wash-sale rule.

**Rule:** Cannot buy the same or substantially identical security within 30 days before or after realizing a loss.

**Scenario:**
- March 15: Sell ABC stock at $10,000 loss
- April 10: Purchase ABC stock (within 30 days)
- Result: Wash sale! Loss disallowed, basis stepped up by loss amount

**Tax-Loss Harvesting Workaround:**
Use similar-but-not-identical funds:
- Sell ABC (individual growth stock) at loss
- Buy ABCI (mutual fund holding similar stocks) immediately
- After 31 days, sell ABCI and repurchase ABC if desired

Switching fund types while harvesting allows continuous market exposure without triggering wash-sale rule.

## State Capital Gains Taxes

Nine states have no capital gains tax (Alaska, Delaware, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming). Others apply state-level capital gains taxes:

| State | LTCG Tax Rate |
|-------|------------|
| California | 13.3% (highest state rate) |
| New York | 8.82% |
| Massachusetts | 5% |
| Maryland | 8.75% |
| Most states | 3-5% |
| No-tax states | 0% |

**Implication for Retirees:**
A retiree relocating from California (13.3% combined federal+state = ~33% total) to Florida (0% state, 20% federal = 20% total) reduces capital gains taxes by 13 percentage points on every dollar realized.

For $100,000 in capital gains:
- California: $33,000 tax
- Florida: $20,000 tax
- Savings: $13,000

This explains why retirees often relocate to no-income-tax states.

## Capital Gains vs. Dividend Strategy Comparison

Two wealth-building philosophies:

**Capital Gains Focus:**
- Build appreciation through growth stocks
- Minimal distributions during accumulation
- Concentrate on unrealized gains (no taxes)
- Sell strategically during low-income years
- Result: Lower current taxes, deferred obligations

**Dividend Focus:**
- Build income through dividend-paying stocks
- Regular distributions during accumulation
- Distribute current income annually
- Hold indefinitely (let step-up basis handle gains)
- Result: Current income, eventually-forgiven gains via step-up

**Retirement Advantage:**
Dividend strategies prove superior for retirees:
- Provide income without forced selling
- Enable strategic capital gains timing
- Preserve step-up basis for heirs
- Minimize MAGI impacts on Medicare

This explains why dividend-focused portfolios dominate retirement investing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I sell appreciated holdings after retirement?**
A: Generally no. Dividend income + Medicare access eliminate need for sales. Hold until step-up basis (or strategic low-income years).

**Q: What's the best time to realize capital gains?**
A: Years with low income (early retirement, year of Roth conversion, etc.) when gains qualify for 0-15% rate instead of 20%.

**Q: How does a capital gains distribution affect me?**
A: Mutual funds distributing capital gains trigger tax liability even if you didn't sell. Use tax-efficient ETFs instead.

**Q: Can I avoid capital gains taxes forever?**
A: Essentially, yes. Hold appreciated assets, live on dividends, pass at death. Step-up basis eliminates gain taxation.

**Q: Should I donate appreciated stock to charity?**
A: Yes! Donate instead of selling. Charity avoids selling, you avoid capital gains tax, deduct full appreciated value. Triple win.

**Q: Does ordinary income affect capital gains taxes?**
A: Yes. Higher ordinary income pushes into higher capital gains brackets. Manage total income to minimize bracket creep.

Capital gains management represents the difference between retaining 75% and 90% of portfolio appreciation. For retirees, strategic timing, tax-loss harvesting, and preferential long-term holding create substantial wealth preservation. Combine these strategies with dividend income and you create retirement sustainability without excessive capital depletion.
