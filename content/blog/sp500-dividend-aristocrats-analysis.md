# S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats: Full Analysis and Rankings

The S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats are a select group of companies that have increased their dividends for at least 25 consecutive years while maintaining membership in the S&P 500 index. This combination of scale, profitability, and shareholder commitment makes them some of the most reliable income investments available. In this analysis, we rank and evaluate the full list of Dividend Aristocrats for 2026.

## What Qualifies a Stock as a Dividend Aristocrat?

To be classified as a Dividend Aristocrat, a company must meet three criteria:

1. **Member of the S&P 500 index** — ensuring large-cap status and liquidity
2. **25+ consecutive years of annual dividend increases** — demonstrating sustained commitment
3. **Minimum float-adjusted market cap and liquidity requirements** — ensuring investability

As of 2026, approximately 67 companies qualify. The list is reconstituted annually in January, with companies added or removed based on their eligibility.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many Dividend Aristocrats are there?

As of 2026, there are approximately 67 S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats. The number fluctuates as companies are added (reaching 25-year streaks) or removed (cutting dividends or leaving the S&P 500).

### What is the average yield of Dividend Aristocrats?

The average yield across all Dividend Aristocrats is approximately 2.5%, ranging from under 1% (Cintas, S&P Global) to over 5% (Franklin Resources, Walgreens). The equal-weighted average is slightly higher than the cap-weighted average.

### Can a Dividend Aristocrat cut its dividend?

Yes. Companies like AT&T and General Electric were former Aristocrats that eventually cut their dividends. The Aristocrat designation reflects past behavior, not a guarantee of future performance. Always evaluate current fundamentals.

### What is the difference between Aristocrats and Kings?

Dividend Aristocrats require 25+ years of increases and S&P 500 membership. Dividend Kings require 50+ years of increases but no S&P 500 requirement. All S&P 500-member Kings are also Aristocrats, but many Aristocrats have not yet reached 50 years.
