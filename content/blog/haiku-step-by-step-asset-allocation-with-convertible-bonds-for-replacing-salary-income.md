## Understanding Convertible Bonds in Asset Allocation

Convertible bonds bridge fixed income and equity growth. They provide steady income while offering upside if the underlying stock appreciates. A typical convertible bond yields 3-4% annually while providing equity participation.

## Tax Considerations

Convertible bonds generate ordinary income (taxed at income rates). Structure your allocation:
- Place convertibles in tax-deferred accounts (IRAs, 401k)
- Hold dividend stocks in taxable accounts to benefit from qualified dividend rates
- Monitor wash sales when rebalancing

## Rebalancing Strategy

Quarterly rebalancing maintains your income targets. If convertibles appreciate above 45% allocation, trim and reinvest proceeds into dividend stocks.

## Risk Management

Convertible bonds carry:
- **Interest rate risk**: Rising rates pressure bond prices
- **Equity risk**: If underlying stock declines sharply, conversion becomes less attractive
- **Credit risk**: Issuer default risk

Diversify across 8-12 different convertible issuers to mitigate credit concentration.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I use convertible bonds for early retirement?**
A: Yes, but they work best for salary replacement. Early retirees should emphasize qualified dividends and capital gains for tax efficiency.

**Q: What's the minimum investment for convertibles?**
A: Most brokers allow $1,000 minimum per bond. Consider convertible bond ETFs for diversification with lower minimums.

**Q: How do I monitor conversion risk?**
A: Track the conversion premium. If a bond is trading above conversion value by >15%, the equity upside is limited.

**Q: Are convertible bonds suitable for conservative portfolios?**
A: They're moderate-risk. Conservative investors should cap them at 30% allocation maximum.

**Q: How do I handle convertibles in a down market?**
A: The bond floor provides downside protection if the underlying stock falls. Your yield remains constant.
