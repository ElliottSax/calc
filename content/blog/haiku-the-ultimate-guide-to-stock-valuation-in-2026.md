# The Ultimate Guide to Stock Valuation in 2026

Stock valuation determines whether a security trades at a fair price, discount, or premium relative to its fundamental value. Professional investors employ multiple valuation methodologies to identify opportunities where market prices diverge from intrinsic values. Understanding these techniques—price-to-earnings ratios, dividend discount models, discounted cash flow analysis—enables individual investors to evaluate whether stocks justify their current prices or represent buying/selling opportunities. This guide explores valuation methods applicable in 2026's market environment and demonstrates their application using real securities.

## Stock Valuation Fundamentals

Stock valuation answers a critical question: What is this company worth today? The answer determines whether a stock represents a bargain or an overpriced asset.

### Core Valuation Principle

**Stock Value = Present Value of All Future Cash Flows Discounted to Today**

This principle underlies all rigorous valuation methods. Variations in methodology differ in how cash flows are estimated and discounted.

## Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E) Valuation

The P/E ratio divides stock price by earnings per share, indicating how many dollars investors pay per dollar of earnings.

### P/E Ratio Formula and Interpretation

**P/E Ratio = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)**

**Valuation Method:**
If fair P/E = 20 and EPS = $2, then fair value = 20 × $2 = $40

## Dividend Discount Model (DDM) Valuation

The Dividend Discount Model values a stock based on the present value of future dividend payments.

### Gordon Growth Model (Simplified DDM)

**Stock Value = Next Year Dividend ÷ (Required Return - Dividend Growth Rate)**

**Formula:**
P = D₁ ÷ (r - g)

Where:
- P = Stock price (intrinsic value)
- D₁ = Next year's expected dividend
- r = Required return (discount rate)
- g = Perpetual dividend growth rate

### Real Example: DDM Valuation of JNJ

**JNJ Fundamentals:**
- Current dividend: $3.25 annually
- Historical dividend growth rate: 5.5% annually
- Expected next year dividend: $3.25 × 1.055 = $3.43
- Required return: 9% (typical for healthcare stocks)

**Calculation:**
P = $3.43 ÷ (0.09 - 0.055)
P = $3.43 ÷ 0.035
**P = $98**

**Market Assessment:**
- Calculated intrinsic value: $98
- Current market price (2026): $155
- Valuation status: Overvalued by 58%

This suggests JNJ trades at a significant premium to dividend-based intrinsic value, potentially indicating the market values growth beyond historical dividends.

### Multi-Stage DDM for Higher Growth Companies

Companies with two growth phases require modified DDM:

**Formula:**
P = [D₁ × ((1 + g₁)^n - 1) / (1 + r)^n] + [D_{n+1} / (r - g₂) × 1/(1 + r)^n]

Where:
- g₁ = High growth rate (first n years)
- g₂ = Terminal stable growth rate
- n = Years of high growth phase

### Multi-Stage Example: Technology Company with Dividend

Hypothetical tech company:
- Current dividend: $1.00
- High-growth phase: 15% annually for 5 years
- Terminal growth: 4% annually thereafter
- Required return: 10%

**Year 1-5 Dividend Growth:**
- Year 1: $1.00 × 1.15 = $1.15
- Year 2: $1.15 × 1.15 = $1.32
- Year 3: $1.32 × 1.15 = $1.52
- Year 4: $1.52 × 1.15 = $1.75
- Year 5: $1.75 × 1.15 = $2.01

**Present Value of Years 1-5 Dividends:**
PV = $1.15/(1.10)^1 + $1.32/(1.10)^2 + $1.52/(1.10)^3 + $1.75/(1.10)^4 + $2.01/(1.10)^5
PV = $1.05 + $1.09 + $1.14 + $1.20 + $1.24
PV = **$5.72**

**Terminal Value (Year 6+):**
Year 6 dividend: $2.01 × 1.04 = $2.09
Terminal value: $2.09 ÷ (0.10 - 0.04) = $34.82
PV of terminal value: $34.82 / (1.10)^5 = $21.61

**Total Intrinsic Value:**
P = $5.72 + $21.61 = **$27.33**

## Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

DCF analysis projects a company's future free cash flows and discounts them to present value—the most theoretically sound valuation method.

### DCF Formula

**Enterprise Value = ∑[FCF_t / (1 + WACC)^t] + Terminal Value / (1 + WACC)^n**

Where:
- FCF_t = Free cash flow in year t
- WACC = Weighted average cost of capital
- Terminal value = Final year value assuming perpetual stable growth

### Real DCF Example: Consumer Staples Company

**Company Fundamentals:**
- Current free cash flow: $2,500 million
- FCF growth rate (years 1-5): 4% annually
- Terminal FCF growth rate: 2.5% annually
- WACC: 7%
- Outstanding shares: 2,500 million
- Net debt: $500 million

**Year 1-5 FCF Projections:**

| Year | FCF (millions) | Discount Factor | Present Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $2,600 | 0.935 | $2,431 |
| 2 | $2,704 | 0.873 | $2,361 |
| 3 | $2,812 | 0.816 | $2,294 |
| 4 | $2,924 | 0.763 | $2,231 |
| 5 | $3,041 | 0.713 | $2,168 |
| **Total PV (Years 1-5)** | | | **$11,485 million** |

**Terminal Value:**
Year 6 FCF: $3,041 × 1.025 = $3,117 million
Terminal value: $3,117 ÷ (0.07 - 0.025) = $69,267 million
PV of terminal value: $69,267 / (1.07)^5 = $49,433 million

**Enterprise Value:**
EV = $11,485 + $49,433 = $60,918 million

**Equity Value and Per-Share Price:**
Equity value = $60,918 - $500 (net debt) = $60,418 million
Price per share = $60,418 / 2,500 = **$24.17**

Compare to current market price to determine valuation status.

Alternative metrics value companies by accounting metrics rather than earnings.

### Price-to-Book (P/B) Analysis

**P/B = Stock Price ÷ Book Value Per Share**

## PEG Ratio: Valuation with Growth Considered

The PEG ratio adjusts P/E for earnings growth, providing valuation context.

### PEG Ratio Formula

**PEG = P/E Ratio ÷ Earnings Growth Rate (%)**

**Interpretation:**
- PEG < 1.0: Undervalued (earnings growth exceeds valuation)
- PEG 1.0-2.0: Fairly valued
- PEG > 2.0: Overvalued (valuation exceeds growth)

### Real PEG Analysis

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Which valuation method is most reliable?**
DCF analysis is theoretically most sound but requires forecasting future cash flows accurately (difficult for 5+ years). P/E ratio provides quick market context but ignores company-specific fundamentals. Best practice uses multiple methods—if all indicate similar valuation, confidence increases.

**Q2: Should I buy stocks trading below intrinsic value?**
Yes, but verify the valuation calculation. A stock trading below calculated intrinsic value may indicate either an opportunity or that the market has information you're missing (company problems, industry disruption). Verify the company's fundamental strength before assuming undervaluation represents opportunity.

**Q3: How do interest rates affect stock valuation?**
Rising rates increase the discount rate (WACC) in DCF models, reducing present value of future cash flows. Conversely, falling rates increase valuations. Higher rates also increase competition from bonds, making low-growth stocks less attractive.

**Q4: Is SCHD overvalued at current 2026 prices?**
By P/E and PEG ratios, SCHD appears fairly-to-modestly valued (24.5 P/E, 4.9 PEG). The 3.8% yield provides income not captured in P/E ratios. For income-focused investors, the valuation appears reasonable despite premium to historical averages.

**Q5: How often should I revalue my holdings?**
Quarterly earnings announcements provide new data warranting updated valuations. For buy-and-hold investors, annual reviews suffice. Avoid constant revaluation—price volatility doesn't change intrinsic value, and excessive trading increases costs.
