# Calculating Portfolio Yield with Reinvestment: A Quantitative Guide

Investors seeking long-term wealth accumulation often focus on dividend-paying assets such as stocks and ETFs. But accurately measuring performance—especially when dividends are reinvested—is more complex than simply dividing annual dividends by principal. The true measure lies in **portfolio yield with reinvestment**, a metric that accounts for compounding effects over time.

This article provides a complete framework for calculating portfolio yield with reinvestment using real-world examples, actual securities, and explicit mathematical formulas. We’ll walk through the difference between simple yield and compounded yield, demonstrate step-by-step calculations, and analyze the impact of reinvestment using real ETFs like **VTI**, **SCHD**, and **VOO**, as well as blue-chip stocks like **Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)** and **Coca-Cola (KO)**.

---

## What Is Portfolio Yield with Reinvestment?

**Portfolio yield** is the annual income generated by a portfolio expressed as a percentage of its value. When dividends are **reinvested**, they purchase additional shares, which themselves generate future income. This compounding process increases both share count and total yield over time.

The **yield with reinvestment** reflects this compounding effect and is typically higher than the **simple yield** (dividend income / initial investment), especially over long periods.

There are two main approaches to calculating yield with reinvestment:

1. **Time-Weighted Return (TWR)** – Measures the compound rate of growth of $1 invested, accounting for cash flows.
2. **Internal Rate of Return (IRR)** – Solves for the discount rate that makes the net present value of all cash flows equal zero.

For reinvested portfolios, **TWR** is more appropriate because it isolates investment performance from external cash flows.

## Simple Yield vs. Yield with Reinvestment

Let’s compare the two using a simple example.

### Example: $10,000 Investment in SCHD

Assume:

- Initial investment: $10,000 in **SCHD** (Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF)
- Purchase price: $50.00 per share → 200 shares
- Annual dividend: $2.00 per share → $400/year
- Dividends reinvested annually at year-end
- Share price grows at 5% per year

| Year | Share Price | Shares Held | Dividend/Share | Total Dividend | Reinvested Shares | Total Shares |
|------|-------------|-------------|----------------|----------------|-------------------|--------------|
| 0    | $50.00      | 200.000     | –              | –              | –                 | 200.000      |
| 1    | $52.50      | 200.000     | $2.00          | $400.00        | 7.619             | 207.619      |
| 2    | $55.13      | 207.619     | $2.10          | $435.99        | 7.909             | 215.528      |
| 3    | $57.88      | 215.528     | $2.20          | $474.16        | 8.192             | 223.720      |

*Dividend growth: 5% per year. Price growth: 5% per year.*

After 3 years:

- Total shares: **223.72**
- Portfolio value: 223.72 × $57.88 = **$12,948.53**
- Total return: (12,948.53 − 10,000) / 10,000 = **29.49%**
- **CAGR (Yield with Reinvestment)** = (12,948.53 / 10,000)^(1/3) − 1 = **9.14%**

Compare this to **simple yield**:

- Average annual dividend: ~$437
- Simple yield = $437 / $10,000 = **4.37%**

The difference—**9.14% vs. 4.37%**—is due to **compounding through reinvestment** and **price appreciation**.

> 🔍 Key Insight: Reinvestment amplifies yield by increasing share count, accelerating income growth.

## Formula: Compounded Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)

To compute portfolio yield with reinvestment over time, use:

$$
\text{CAGR} = \left( \frac{\text{Ending Value}}{\text{Beginning Value}} \right)^{\frac{1}{n}} - 1
$$

Where:
- Ending Value = Final portfolio value (including reinvested dividends)
- Beginning Value = Initial investment
- $ n $ = number of years

This formula captures the **effective annual yield** when dividends are reinvested.

## Step-by-Step: How to Calculate Your Portfolio Yield with Reinvestment

### Step 1: Gather Data
For each holding:
- Initial investment date and price
- All dividend payments (amount and date)
- Reinvestment prices (or use adjusted close)
- Final share count and value

### Step 2: Track Reinvested Shares

For each dividend payment:
$$
\text{Reinvested Shares} = \frac{\text{Dividend Received}}{\text{Share Price at Reinvestment}}
$$

Update cumulative share count.

### Step 3: Compute Final Portfolio Value

$$
\text{Final Value} = \text{Final Share Count} \times \text{Current Price}
$$

### Step 4: Calculate CAGR

$$
\text{CAGR} = \left( \frac{\text{Final Value}}{\text{Initial Investment}} \right)^{1/n} - 1
$$

This is your **portfolio yield with reinvestment**.

## The Mathematics of Compounding with Reinvestment

The future value of a portfolio with reinvested dividends can be modeled as:

$$
FV = P \times \prod_{t=1}^{n} (1 + r_t)
$$

Where:
- $ P $ = initial principal
- $ r_t $ = total return (price change + dividend yield) in year $ t $

Alternatively, if returns are constant:

$$
FV = P \times (1 + g)^n
$$

Where $ g $ is the **geometric average return**, i.e., CAGR.

But in reality, $ r_t $ varies. So we use:

$$
g = \left( \prod_{t=1}^{n} (1 + r_t) \right)^{1/n} - 1
$$

This is the **time-weighted return**, the correct measure for yield with reinvestment.

## Common Pitfalls in Calculating Yield

1. **Using simple yield instead of CAGR**  
   → Ignores compounding and growth.

2. **Ignoring reinvestment price volatility**  
   → Reinvesting at high prices reduces share gains.

3. **Using trailing yield instead of actual payments**  
   → Trailing yield can overstate income if dividend was cut.

4. **Not adjusting for splits and spin-offs**  
   → Fails to track true share count.

Always use **actual transaction data** or **total return indices** (e.g., VOO’s total return version).

## FAQ: Portfolio Yield with Reinvestment

### Q1: Is portfolio yield with reinvestment the same as total return?

**Yes**, when no external cash flows (deposits/withdrawals) occur. Total return includes price appreciation and reinvested dividends. It is synonymous with **CAGR** in a buy-and-hold scenario.

---

### Q2: How do I calculate yield if I make periodic deposits?

Use the **Internal Rate of Return (IRR)** or **Modified Dietz Method**.

Example: You invest $1,000/month in SCHD for 5 years.

Use Excel’s `XIRR` function:
```excel
=XIRR(values, dates)
```

Where:
- `values` = negative for contributions, positive for final value
- `dates` = corresponding dates

This gives your **personalized yield with reinvestment**.

---

### Q3: Does reinvesting dividends always outperform taking cash?

**Long term: yes.** Reinvestment harnesses compounding.

**Short term: not necessarily.** If the stock is overvalued, reinvesting at high prices reduces long-term returns. Tactical investors may take cash during bubbles.
