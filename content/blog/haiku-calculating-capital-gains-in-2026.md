# Calculating Capital Gains in 2026

Capital gains represent profits from selling investments for more than you paid. Understanding how to calculate gains, determine tax liability, and optimize timing controls your actual after-tax returns. In 2026, preferential capital gains tax rates make strategic selling decisions critical for maximizing wealth.

## Understanding Capital Gains Basics

Capital gains occur when you sell an investment for more than your purchase price:

$$\text{Capital Gain} = \text{Sale Price} - \text{Purchase Price}$$

If you buy 100 shares of VOO at $380 per share ($38,000 total) and sell at $420 per share ($42,000 total):

$$\text{Capital Gain} = \$42,000 - \$38,000 = \$4,000$$

You owe taxes on the $4,000 gain, not the $42,000 proceeds. This distinguishes capital gains taxes from capital gains (taxes apply to profits, not returns of capital).

Capital gains divide into two categories:
- **Short-term capital gains**: Assets held less than 1 year (taxed as ordinary income at up to 37%)
- **Long-term capital gains**: Assets held 1+ years (taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%)

**Example: Married couple with $120,000 income plus $50,000 capital gain**
- Combined income: $170,000
- Income falls in 15% long-term capital gains bracket
- Capital gains tax: $50,000 × 15% = $7,500

**Example: Single person with $40,000 income plus $10,000 capital gain**
- Combined income: $50,000
- First $7,025 of gain in 0% bracket
- Remaining $2,975 in 15% bracket
- Capital gains tax: $0 + ($2,975 × 15%) = $446
- Effective capital gains rate: 4.46%

Substantial income flexibility exists, particularly for married couples in the 0% bracket with income up to $94,050.

## Calculating Gains in Dividend Growth Portfolios

**Example: 10-year SCHD holding**

| Current value | $98,500 |

- Long-term capital gain: $48,500

- Total capital gains tax at 15% rate: $48,500 × 0.15 = $7,275

## Harvest Loss Strategy in 2026

Tax-loss harvesting allows you to sell positions with losses to offset capital gains:

**Scenario: Portfolio with mixed results**

| Position | Original Cost | Current Value | Gain/(Loss) |
|---|---|---|---|
| SCHD | $40,000 | $52,000 | +$12,000 |
| JNJ | $20,000 | $18,500 | -$1,500 |
| PG | $15,000 | $21,200 | +$6,200 |
| VOO | $25,000 | $23,000 | -$2,000 |
| **Total Portfolio** | **$100,000** | **$114,700** | **+$14,700** |

Without harvesting:
- Realized gains: $18,200 ($12,000 + $6,200)
- Capital gains tax at 15%: $2,730

With harvesting (sell JNJ and VOO, buy replacements):
- Realized gains: $18,200
- Realized losses: $3,500
- Net capital gains: $14,700
- Capital gains tax at 15%: $2,205
- Tax savings: $525

The wash-sale rule prevents repurchasing the same security within 30 days. You could buy JNJ competitor (PFE) and VOO alternative (VTI) to maintain desired exposure.

## Qualified Dividend Income vs. Capital Gains

Both receive preferential tax treatment but at different rates. For certain income levels, capital gains rates exceed dividend rates:

**Example: Single person with $500,000 income**

| Income Source | Amount | Tax Rate | Tax Owed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qualified Dividend | $10,000 | 20% | $2,000 |
| Long-term Capital Gain | $10,000 | 20% | $2,000 |
| Short-term Capital Gain | $10,000 | 37% | $3,700 |
| Interest Income | $10,000 | 37% | $3,700 |

At high income levels, all preferential rates converge to 20%, and short-term gains are disadvantageous at 37%.

**Example: Married couple with $100,000 income**

| Income Source | Amount | Tax Rate | Tax Owed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qualified Dividend | $10,000 | 0% | $0 |
| Long-term Capital Gain | $10,000 | 0% | $0 |
| Short-term Capital Gain | $10,000 | 12% | $1,200 |
| Interest Income | $10,000 | 12% | $1,200 |

At moderate income, both qualified dividends and long-term gains receive 0% treatment, while short-term gains are taxed at ordinary rates.

## Specific vs. Average Cost Basis Methods

When selling partial positions, you can specify which shares you sell, controlling tax liability:

**Scenario: Selling 500 of 1,000 VOO shares**

Shares purchased at different prices:
- 300 shares at $200/share (original cost $60,000)
- 400 shares at $250/share (original cost $100,000)
- 300 shares at $350/share (original cost $105,000)

Current price: $420/share

**Method 1: Average Cost Basis**
- Average cost: ($60,000 + $100,000 + $105,000) / 1,000 = $265/share
- Selling 500 shares: Cost $132,500
- Proceeds: 500 × $420 = $210,000
- Gain: $77,500
- Tax at 15%: $11,625

**Method 2: Specific Identification (sell highest-cost shares)**
- Sell 300 shares at $350 and 200 shares at $250
- Cost: ($350 × 300) + ($250 × 200) = $105,000 + $50,000 = $155,000
- Proceeds: 500 × $420 = $210,000
- Gain: $55,000
- Tax at 15%: $8,250
- Tax savings: $3,375

Specific identification allows selling highest-cost shares, minimizing capital gains when prices appreciate. This saves $3,375 in taxes on this transaction.

## Net Capital Loss Carryforward

Capital losses reduce capital gains. If losses exceed gains, you carry forward unused losses:

**Year 1: Realizing losses exceeding gains**

| Position | Gain/(Loss) |
|---|---|
| SCHD | +$5,000 |
| Failed Tech Stock | -$8,000 |
| VOO | +$2,000 |
| **Net** | **-$1,000** |

- No capital gains tax (losses offset gains)
- Carry forward $1,000 loss to next year

**Year 2: Using carried-forward losses**

| Position | Gain/(Loss) |
|---|---|
| JNJ | +$12,000 |
| International Stock | +$4,000 |
| Carried-forward loss | -$1,000 |
| **Net** | **+$15,000** |

- Capital gains before carryforward: $16,000
- Reduced by carryforward loss: -$1,000
- Net taxable capital gains: $15,000
- Tax at 15%: $2,250 (instead of $2,400 without carryforward)
- Tax savings: $150

Losses carry forward indefinitely, eventually offsetting gains. In retirement, you might strategically harvest losses to avoid realized gains entirely.

## Estate Tax and Stepped-Up Basis

A powerful tax advantage occurs at inheritance: assets receive "stepped-up basis," eliminating capital gains taxes:

**Example: Apple stock inherited by beneficiary**

Original owner:
- Purchased 100 shares at $50/share (cost basis: $5,000)
- Stock grows to $150/share
- Value: $15,000
- Unrealized gain: $10,000

Upon inheritance (stepped-up basis):
- Beneficiary's new cost basis: $150/share
- Beneficiary's value: $15,000
- Beneficiary's unrealized gain: $0

If beneficiary sells immediately, they pay zero capital gains tax on the $10,000 appreciation from original purchase. This provides powerful tax efficiency for multi-generational wealth building.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What's the difference between capital gains and losses?**
A: Gains occur when you sell for profit (taxed). Losses occur when you sell at a loss (reduce taxes). Both must be realized through sales; unrealized appreciation/depreciation doesn't trigger taxes until you sell.

**Q: How long must I hold for long-term capital gains treatment?**
A: More than 1 year. If you buy on January 15, 2026, and sell on January 16, 2027, it qualifies as long-term. Short-term holding produces ordinary income tax rates up to 37%.

**Q: Should I sell stocks at a loss to reduce taxes?**
A: Only if you're not "wash-sale" violating (repurchasing within 30 days). Otherwise, yes—harvesting losses reduces overall capital gains taxes. However, don't sell quality positions just to realize losses; buy alternatives temporarily, then repurchase if desired.

**Q: Are there strategies to defer capital gains?**
A: Limited options. Specific identification of shares, tax-loss harvesting, charitable donations of appreciated securities (eliminate gains entirely), and qualified opportunity zone investments (defer gains) exist. Mostly, you realize gains when you sell.

**Q: Does reinvesting dividends affect capital gains taxes?**
A: No. Dividends themselves are taxed in the year received. The shares purchased from reinvested dividends create new cost basis. When you eventually sell, gains are calculated from that new cost basis, not the original investment.
