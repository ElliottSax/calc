# Understanding TIPS for Inflation Protection

Inflation silently erodes wealth. A dollar in 1980 is worth 18 cents today. Traditional Treasury bonds protect against default but not purchasing power loss. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) solve this problem by adjusting principal and interest for inflation. This guide explains how TIPS work and whether they belong in your portfolio.

## What Are TIPS?

TIPS are Treasury securities where the principal value adjusts with inflation, measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI-U). You receive interest on the inflation-adjusted principal, not the original amount.

**Key Features:**
- Issued by U.S. Treasury
- Principal adjusts monthly based on CPI-U
- Interest (coupon) paid semi-annually on adjusted principal
- Maturity periods: 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year
- Currently paying 2.0-2.4% real yield (as of March 2026)

## How TIPS Work: Step by Step

### Basic Example

**Purchase:** 10-year TIPS at par ($10,000)
**Coupon rate:** 1.875% (real yield)
**Initial CPI:** 315.0

**Year 1 Inflation:** 3.5%

**Adjusted Principal:**
- New CPI: 315.0 × 1.035 = 325.975
- Inflation adjustment: 325.975 / 315.0 = 1.0350
- New principal value: $10,000 × 1.0350 = $10,350

**Semi-annual Interest:**
- Interest = (New Principal × Coupon Rate) / 2
- Interest = ($10,350 × 0.01875) / 2 = $96.98

**After Year 1:**
- Principal value: $10,350
- Coupon received: $193.97 (2 × $96.98)
- Total account value: $10,543.97

### Year 2: Continued Inflation

**Year 2 Inflation:** 2.5%

**New CPI:** 325.975 × 1.025 = 334.125
**Inflation adjustment factor:** 334.125 / 315.0 = 1.0608
**New principal:** $10,000 × 1.0608 = $10,608

**Semi-annual interest:**
- Interest = ($10,608 × 0.01875) / 2 = $99.45

**After Year 2:**
- Principal value: $10,608
- Total coupons received: $193.97 + $198.90 = $392.87

This continues every period until maturity. If cumulative inflation over 10 years reaches 35%, your principal grows to $13,500, and your interest payments compound on that larger base.

## TIPS vs. Nominal Treasury Bonds: The Comparison

### Head-to-Head Example

**Scenario:** 2% annual inflation, 10-year time horizon

**Investor A: $100,000 in Nominal Treasury Bonds**
- Yield: 4.5%
- Annual interest: $4,500
- After 10 years with 4.5% return: $147,231
- Purchasing power of $147,231 at 2% inflation: $120,581

**Investor B: $100,000 in TIPS**
- Real yield: 1.5% (actual current TIPS yield minus inflation)
- Principal adjusts for inflation
- After 10 years with 1.5% real return: Principal grows to $121,136 in nominal terms
- Purchasing power preserved: $121,136 (unchanged because it's adjusted for inflation)

**Breakeven Analysis:**

TIPS outperform nominal bonds when inflation exceeds the **breakeven inflation rate**.

Breakeven Rate = Nominal Bond Yield - TIPS Real Yield

**Current Market (March 2026):**
- 10-year Treasury yield: 4.2%
- 10-year TIPS yield: 2.0%
- Breakeven inflation: 2.2% annually

If inflation averages above 2.2%, TIPS win. If inflation averages below 2.2%, nominal bonds win.

## TIPS Allocation Models

### The Case for TIPS

**1. Inflation Protection:** TIPS guarantee real returns, preserving purchasing power.

**2. Bonds still decline in rising-rate environments:** When rates rise, both TIPS and nominal bonds decline in value. But TIPS protect your purchasing power if you hold to maturity.

**3. Positive real yields:** At 2.0% real yield, TIPS offer genuine inflation-adjusted returns—better than money market funds at 4.0% nominal (2.0% real after inflation).

## TIPS Maturity Strategy: The Ladder

Rather than buying all 10-year TIPS, build a ladder to manage interest-rate risk:

### Five-Year TIPS Ladder Example

**Purchase $20,000 in TIPS across five maturities:**

| Year | Amount | Maturity | Real Yield |
|------|--------|----------|-----------|
| 2026 | $4,000 | 5-year | 1.2% |
| 2027 | $4,000 | 6-year | 1.4% |
| 2028 | $4,000 | 7-year | 1.6% |
| 2029 | $4,000 | 8-year | 1.8% |
| 2030 | $4,000 | 9-year | 2.0% |

**Benefits:**
- $4,000 matures every year (liquidity)
- Benefit from yield curve (longer TIPS pay more)
- Reduce concentration risk
- Auto-rebalancing (reinvest each maturity in longest-duration TIPS)

## TIPS Mathematics: Calculating Real Returns

### Formula for Real Return

**Nominal Return = (1 + Real Return) × (1 + Inflation Rate) - 1**

Rearranging:

**Real Return = (1 + Nominal Return) / (1 + Inflation Rate) - 1**

### Example Calculation

**Nominal Bondfund return:** 6% (nominal)
**Inflation:** 3%

**Real return:** (1.06) / (1.03) - 1 = 0.0291 = 2.91%

The nominal 6% is really just 2.91% of actual purchasing power improvement.

**TIPS comparison:**
**TIPS real return:** 1.5% (guaranteed)

The 6% nominal bond is effectively worth more today because you get 2.91% real return, but that return fluctuates with inflation expectations. TIPS guarantee 1.5% no matter what inflation does.

## When to Use TIPS vs. When to Skip

### Use TIPS If:

1. **High inflation expectations:** If you believe inflation will exceed 2.5% over the next decade
2. **Inflation-sensitive spending:** Your major expenses (healthcare, education) outpace overall inflation
3. **Long time horizon:** TIPS shine over 10+ years as compounding inflation adjustments accumulate
4. **Risk aversion:** You want guaranteed real returns, not nominal returns eroded by inflation
5. **Retired or semi-retired:** Your spending patterns are fixed; TIPS income adjusts with inflation

### Skip TIPS If:

1. **Deflation expected:** Unusual now, but if deflation occurs, nominal bonds outperform
2. **Short time horizon:** 2-3 year investors care about nominal returns, not real
3. **Full equity portfolio:** You already have inflation protection through stock growth
4. **Yield-hungry:** TIPS real yields are 1.5-2.0%, lower than nominal bonds at 4.0-4.5%
5. **Income maximization:** Nominal bonds provide higher current income

## Tax Considerations

### Phantom Income Problem

TIPS create "phantom income"—you pay taxes on inflation adjustments even though you don't receive cash until maturity.

**Example:**
- Year 1: Receive $100 in coupon interest (cash in pocket)
- Year 1: Inflation adjustment adds $200 to principal (no cash, but taxable)
- Year 1: Total taxable income: $300 (but only received $100)

**Tax optimization:**
- Hold TIPS in tax-deferred accounts (IRA, 401k)
- Use I-Bonds (Series I) in taxable accounts (defer tax until cashed)
- If forced into taxable account, use only in low-income years

## I-Bonds vs. TIPS

| Feature | I-Bonds | TIPS |
|---------|---------|------|
| Inflation protection | Yes | Yes |
| Issued by | Treasury | Treasury |
| Current rate | CPI + 0.6% | Real yield 1.5-2.0% |
| Minimum hold | 1 year | None |
| Penalty | 3 months interest | None |
| Purchase limit | $10,000/year | Unlimited |
| Tax treatment | Can defer | Phantom income |

**When to use I-Bonds:** Low-income years, emergency fund, tax deferral strategy

**When to use TIPS:** Core bond allocation, large sums, laddered strategy

## Conclusion

TIPS protect purchasing power—something no other bond does. For conservative investors and retirees concerned about inflation eroding fixed income, TIPS deserve 10-20% of bond allocations. Build a ladder, hold to maturity, and let inflation adjustments compound your wealth in real terms.

In a world where inflation averages 2-3% annually, TIPS guarantee real returns of 1.5-2.0%. That seems modest until you realize nominal bonds often deliver negative real returns after inflation. TIPS solve that problem with mathematical elegance.
