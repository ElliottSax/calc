# Vanguard International Dividend ETFs: VYMI, VEU, VXUS Compared

Vanguard has built its reputation on low-cost index funds that provide broad market exposure. For investors seeking international dividend income, Vanguard offers several ETF options, each with different strategies and characteristics. Understanding the differences between VYMI, VEU, and VXUS can help you choose the right fit for your portfolio.

## Overview of Vanguard International Dividend ETFs

While Vanguard offers numerous international equity funds, three stand out for dividend-focused investors:

**VYMI (Vanguard International High Dividend Yield ETF):**
- Tracks the FTSE All-World ex US High Dividend Yield Index
- Focuses specifically on high-yielding international stocks
- Screens for dividend sustainability
- Expense ratio: 0.22%
- Current yield: ~4.2%

**VEU (Vanguard FTSE All-World ex-US ETF):**
- Tracks the FTSE All-World ex US Index
- Broad international exposure (developed and emerging)
- Market-cap weighted, not dividend-focused
- Expense ratio: 0.08%
- Current yield: ~2.8%

**VXUS (Vanguard Total International Stock ETF):**
- Tracks the FTSE Global All Cap ex US Index
- Includes small-cap stocks (broader than VEU)
- Market-cap weighted total market approach
- Expense ratio: 0.08%
- Current yield: ~2.7%

## Key Takeaways

- VYMI offers the highest yield (4.2%) with a focused dividend strategy but higher expense ratio (0.22%)
- VEU and VXUS provide broader diversification with ultra-low costs (0.08%) but lower yields (~2.7-2.8%)
- VYMI holds ~1,200 stocks concentrated in financials and energy; VEU/VXUS hold 3,500-8,000+ stocks
- Tax efficiency favors VEU/VXUS in taxable accounts due to lower turnover
- All three funds face similar foreign withholding tax treatment averaging 10-15%
- VYMI suits income-focused investors; VEU/VXUS suit total return investors wanting international dividends

## VYMI: Vanguard International High Dividend Yield ETF

### Strategy and Holdings

VYMI focuses exclusively on high-dividend-paying international stocks, screening for both yield and sustainability. The fund tracks the FTSE All-World ex US High Dividend Yield Index, which selects stocks based on:

**Selection Criteria:**
- Forecasted dividend yield (top-yielding stocks)
- Dividend sustainability (excludes stocks likely to cut dividends)
- Liquidity requirements
- Free float requirements

**Portfolio Composition (as of May 2026):**
- Number of holdings: ~1,200 stocks
- Median market cap: $28 billion
- Top 10 holdings: ~15% of assets
- Geographic concentration: Europe (50%), Asia (30%), Emerging (15%), Other (5%)

**Sector Allocation:**
- Financials: 28%
- Energy: 15%
- Healthcare: 12%
- Consumer Staples: 10%
- Materials: 9%
- Utilities: 8%
- Industrials: 8%
- Technology: 5%
- Other: 5%

The heavy weighting to financials and energy reflects these sectors' traditional focus on dividend payments. This creates sector concentration risk but also delivers higher income.

### Performance and Yield

**Historical Returns (Annual Average):**
- 1-year: +12.3%
- 3-year: +8.1%
- 5-year: +7.4%
- 10-year: +6.2%

**Dividend Metrics:**
- Current yield: 4.2%
- Dividend growth (5-year): 5.8% annually
- Payout frequency: Quarterly
- Distribution stability: High (rarely cut)

**Yield Comparison:**
VYMI's 4.2% yield significantly exceeds the broad international market (VT: 2.1%) and U.S. market (VTI: 1.6%), making it attractive for income investors.

### Advantages of VYMI

**1. High Current Income**
The 4.2% yield provides substantial cash flow, particularly valuable for:
- Retirees living off portfolio income
- Investors seeking to reinvest dividends for compounding
- Those needing income in low-interest environments

**2. Dividend Sustainability Screening**
Unlike simple high-yield strategies, VYMI screens for sustainability:
- Excludes stocks with deteriorating fundamentals
- Filters out potential dividend cuts
- Results in more stable income stream

**3. Broad International Diversification**
With ~1,200 holdings across developed and emerging markets:
- Single-country risk is minimized
- Exposure to multiple currencies and economic cycles
- Reduces impact of any individual stock's dividend cut

**4. Automatic Rebalancing**
The index methodology continuously:
- Adds new high-yielders that meet criteria
- Removes stocks that no longer qualify
- Maintains yield target without active management

### Disadvantages of VYMI

**1. Higher Expense Ratio**
At 0.22%, VYMI costs nearly 3x more than VEU/VXUS (0.08%):
- On $100,000: $220/year vs. $80/year
- Over 30 years, expense difference can exceed 4% of portfolio value
- Yield advantage helps offset but doesn't eliminate this cost

**2. Sector Concentration**
43% in financials and energy creates risks:
- Regulatory changes affecting bank dividends
- Energy price volatility impacting oil company payouts
- Less exposure to growth sectors like technology

**3. Potential Value Trap**
High-yield strategies can overweight:
- Declining industries with artificially high yields
- Companies in financial distress maintaining dividends
- "Yield traps" where high current yield precedes dividend cuts

**4. Lower Total Return Potential**
Focus on current income may sacrifice growth:
- Historical returns lag broader international indexes slightly
- Less exposure to fast-growing emerging companies
- Dividend focus excludes many growth stocks

### Performance and Yield

- 3-year: +9.5%
- 5-year: +8.8%
- 10-year: +7.1%

**Dividend Metrics:**
- Current yield: 2.8%
- Dividend growth (5-year): 6.2% annually
- Payout frequency: Quarterly
- Distribution: Four payments per year

VEU has historically outperformed VYMI on a total return basis by 0.5-1% annually, though with lower current income.

### Advantages of VEU

**1. Ultra-Low Costs**
0.08% expense ratio is among the lowest available:
- $80 annually per $100,000 invested
- Over time, cost advantage compounds significantly
- More assets remain invested working for you

**2. Broader Diversification**
3,500+ holdings versus 1,200 for VYMI:
- Greater exposure to mid and large-cap stocks
- More comprehensive emerging market representation
- True market portfolio approach

**3. Stronger Total Return History**
Balanced approach has delivered better long-term returns:
- Less sector concentration risk
- Participation in growth sectors
- Better positioned for capital appreciation

**4. Lower Turnover**
Market-cap weighting requires less trading:
- More tax-efficient in taxable accounts
- Lower transaction costs
- Fewer taxable capital gain distributions

### Disadvantages of VEU

**1. Lower Current Yield**
2.8% yield is 33% less than VYMI:
- Less immediate income for income-focused investors
- May not meet cash flow needs for retirees
- Requires larger portfolio for same income

**2. No Dividend Focus**
Holdings selected for market cap, not dividend quality:
- Includes many non-dividend payers
- No screening for dividend sustainability
- Some holdings may cut or eliminate dividends

**3. Higher Valuation Sensitivity**
Market-cap weighting overweights expensive stocks:
- Popular stocks get larger weights regardless of value
- Can result in concentration in overvalued sectors
- Less natural value tilt than dividend strategies

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I hold all three funds, or is that redundant?**
A: There's significant overlap (VYMI holdings are subset of VEU/VXUS). Holding both VYMI and VEU/VXUS creates a tilt toward high-dividend stocks. Not necessarily bad, but recognize you're overweighting dividend payers versus market portfolio.

**Q: Which fund is best for a Roth IRA?**
A: VYMI's higher yield is attractive, but can't escape foreign withholding in Roth IRAs. If you prioritize long-term growth, VXUS may deliver better total returns. For income, VYMI. For growth, VXUS.

**Q: How do these compare to emerging market dividend ETFs?**
A: VYMI/VEU/VXUS include emerging markets (20-25%) but are developed-market heavy. Pure emerging market dividend ETFs (like EDIV) offer 5-7% yields but with significantly higher risk and volatility.

**Q: Do these funds automatically reinvest dividends?**
A: No. ETFs distribute dividends to your account. You must set up automatic reinvestment through your brokerage if desired.

**Q: What's the tax treatment of dividends from these funds?**
A: Distributions are mostly "qualified dividends" taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). Some portion may be ordinary income. You'll receive Form 1099-DIV detailing the breakdown.

**Q: How often do these funds pay dividends?**
A: All three pay quarterly (four times per year), typically in March, June, September, and December.

**Q: Can I use these funds in a three-fund portfolio?**
A: Yes. A classic three-fund portfolio includes U.S. stocks (VTI), international stocks (VEU or VXUS), and bonds (BND). VYMI can substitute for VEU/VXUS if you prefer income focus.

**Q: What's the minimum investment?**
A: One share. As of May 2026:
- VYMI: ~$68/share
- VEU: ~$62/share
- VXUS: ~$65/share

## Conclusion

Vanguard's international dividend ETF options cater to different investor needs. VYMI delivers the highest current income with a focused dividend strategy, making it ideal for retirees and income investors willing to accept higher costs and sector concentration. VEU and VXUS offer broader diversification with rock-bottom expenses, better suiting investors focused on total return who want some international dividend income as part of a balanced portfolio.

For most investors, VEU or VXUS provides the best combination of diversification, cost efficiency, and reasonable dividend income. The 0.08% expense ratio and broad market exposure are hard to beat. VYMI makes sense as a satellite holding for income enhancement or as a core position in retirement accounts where current income is the priority.

Whatever you choose, all three funds provide high-quality international exposure from one of the industry's most trusted providers. The "wrong" choice among these three is far better than not diversifying internationally at all.
